# Council Value Test: Coercion and Bargaining at Hormuz

Status: `completed-narrow`
As of: `2026-07-09`
Time cap: one working day

## Question

What governs whether coercive pressure around Hormuz remains bargaining
leverage or becomes a self-sustaining escalation mechanism, and which observable
would most clearly show that the governing logic has changed?

## Why This Test

Hormuz has enough archived depth to test reconstruction rather than retrieval
scarcity. It also permits comparison with the completed July value test. The
experiment asks whether a council exposes useful causal disagreement that the
ordinary system-assisted brief compressed or omitted.

## Council

- Robert Pape: coercive mechanisms, thresholds, and falsifiers.
- John Mearsheimer: structural incentives, security competition, and bargaining
  geometry.
- Seyed Mohammad Marandi: Iranian signaling, legitimacy, and regional political
  interpretation.

These are analytical functions, not assumed positions. Corpus depth is unequal
and must be disclosed in the completed dialogue.

## Control and Treatment

Control: use the existing system-assisted Hormuz note as the ordinary synthesis
baseline.

Treatment: independently reconstruct the three responses under
[the dialogue contract](../../../method/dialogue-contract.md), then add one
round of premise-level cross-examination and a separate moderator synthesis.

Do not let participants see the moderator synthesis before their responses are
fixed. Do not supplement the archive with external evidence.

## Success Criteria

The council must:

- pass source fidelity, attribution precision, and generative restraint;
- pass at least six of the seven fidelity dimensions;
- surface at least two consequential disagreements hidden by the baseline;
- produce at least one discriminating observable that is not merely a renamed
  baseline forecast hook;
- take no more than twice the analytical time of the baseline workflow;
- avoid making the moderator's judgment appear to be participant consensus.

## Decision Rule

- `continue`: clearly improves insight and passes all hard fidelity gates;
- `narrow`: valuable only for mature voices and deep crisis objects;
- `redesign`: differentiation is promising but reconstruction is not reliable;
- `stop`: dialogue adds performance or attribution without analytical value.

No dialogue automation or public conversational product is authorized by this
plan. A passing manual result is the prerequisite for either.

## Result

The manual treatment passed all three hard fidelity gates and six of seven
review dimensions. It exposed two consequential disagreements and produced a
more discriminating transition test than the baseline forecast hook. The time
comparison could not be resolved because the baseline did not preserve a
comparable elapsed-time record.

Decision: `narrow`.

The council adds analytical value for this deep crisis object, but the result
does not justify automation or generalization to thin voices. See the
[completed dialogue](council-dialogue.md) and [scorecard](scorecard.md).
