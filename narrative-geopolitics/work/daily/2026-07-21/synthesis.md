## Lead Judgment

The central change is not simply another exchange of strikes. It is the narrowing of the off-ramp. Iran’s pressure on bases, Gulf infrastructure, Hormuz, and the Red Sea creates several escalation channels at once; U.S. and Israeli signaling toward deeper nuclear-site attacks and continued regional deployments answers that pressure with threats that require further force to remain credible. The asymmetry is that Iran can impose recurring risk on exposed bases and shipping, while Washington must defend a distributed footprint and explain every casualty or withdrawal as a strategic choice.

## Crisis Object

Crisis object: Can Washington convert limited strikes into a credible settlement before Iranian pressure on exposed bases, Gulf infrastructure, and shipping forces a politically irreversible expansion of the war?

## Draft Judgment

The war is entering a constraint phase: each side can still intensify violence, but neither has demonstrated a reliable path to convert tactical damage into political surrender. Iran’s most consequential advantage is not a single strike; it is the ability to keep multiple pressure points active—bases, shipping, energy, and allied infrastructure—while forcing Washington to defend a wide perimeter. The U.S. answer is increasingly public threat and continued forward exposure, which creates the very casualty event that could make withdrawal politically harder. The most likely near-term pattern is therefore continued calibrated escalation with intermittent pauses, not a clean ceasefire or decisive victory.
