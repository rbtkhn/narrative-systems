# The Bypass Test: Hormuz Remains a Contest over Permission

Title standard: `reader-facing`

Title rationale: `Names the alleged event that tests the brief's central distinction between nominally open water and passage that remains politically conditioned.`

Date: `2026-07-10`

Status: `internal source-bounded brief`

The July 10 archive supplies the clearest account yet of what would test the Hormuz governance thesis: an alleged attempt to route vessels through an Oman-side corridor without Iranian coordination, followed by alleged Iranian coercive action and renewed US strikes. If that sequence occurred as described, the Strait's central question is no longer whether water is physically open. It is whether commercially tolerable passage can occur without Iranian permission.

That conclusion remains conditional. Crooke provides the detailed sequence; Mearsheimer interprets the claimed MOU as recognizing Iranian control; Macgregor argues that military alternatives lack a clean political end-state. Their functional convergence strengthens the mechanism but does not independently verify ships, warnings, attacks, damage, or orders. The channels overlap in their skepticism of US strategy, and no commercial or operational evidence enters the batch.

The same discipline prevents the day's NATO rhetoric from taking over the run. Mercouris describes Russian trust as exhausted after the summit and treats Patriot-production promises and drone support as further escalation. Mearsheimer and Macgregor also see continued Western involvement. But the archive does not establish that those statements have produced a new deployable capability, force posture, or settlement threshold. Russia–NATO therefore remains a watch candidate.

Three Hormuz explanations survive. Iran may possess enough selective control to condition passage. The incident may instead be temporary disruption inflated into a governance narrative. Or both sides may be conducting limited coercive tests without stable control. Ship tracks, insurance response, repeated route behavior, official notices, and independently verified damage would discriminate among them; none is available here.

No new forecast is added. Existing hook `NG-20260708-F02` already asks whether a visible bypass attempt produces a visible coercive response. [VER-20260710-01](../../verification/packets/VER-20260710-01-hormuz-bypass-test/README.md) confirms three damaged vessels along the Oman-side route and subsequent US strikes, but it does not establish the claimed US command chain or attributable trigger-response sequence. The hook remains open.

## Evidence Status

- `source_assertion`: an attempted Oman-side bypass, Iranian warnings or attacks, renewed US strikes, and claimed transit terms.
- `cross-source_convergence—interpretive`: coercion has not restored an uncontested political baseline.
- `operationally_contested`: vessel damage and approximate locations are supported; attribution, orders, and the causal sequence remain unproven.
- `unknown—commercial evidence absent`: transit volume, routing adherence, insurance pricing, and tolerable operating conditions.

## What Would Change the Judgment

- Repeated passage outside Iranian coordination without coercive response would weaken the managed-passage hypothesis.
- Verified Iranian enforcement against a documented bypass would strengthen it.
- Commercial normalization despite continued political rhetoric would favor temporary disruption over durable governance.
- A verified NATO capability or force-posture change that alters battlefield or settlement terms would justify a separate Russia–NATO promotion review.
