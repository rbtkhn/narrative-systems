## The Mechanism Is Permission, Not Physical Closure

The Hormuz cluster is more discriminating because it concerns behavior that would separate two hypotheses. Under the managed-passage hypothesis, nominally open water remains politically governed when vessels must coordinate with Iran and bypass attempts invite coercion. Under the temporary-disruption hypothesis, commercial normalization should proceed without durable Iranian permission or repeated coercive tests. The alleged July episode fits the first mechanism, but its evidentiary status remains `source_assertion`.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Managed passage:** Iran can condition enough transit to preserve bargaining authority without total closure.
2. **Narrated control:** overlapping commentators inflate a temporary maritime incident into a new governance order.
3. **Mutual signaling:** Washington and Tehran stage limited tests for bargaining purposes while neither possesses stable control.

The archive cannot yet discriminate decisively among them because the same missing observables—ship tracks, warnings, damage, insurance response, official orders, and repeated passage behavior—would do the most work.
