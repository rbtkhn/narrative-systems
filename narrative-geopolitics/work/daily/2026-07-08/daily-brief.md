### NGI-20260708-S01 - Hormuz Becomes a Transit Governance Fight

July 8 is the cleanest transit-governance day in the early July sequence. The source stack points to a narrower and harder crisis object than general escalation with Iran. The real dispute is who governs actual passage through Hormuz after the war. Washington wants restored transit without fully accepting Iranian management. Iran is treating management of passage as an enforceable floor of the postwar order.

The archive does not need to verify every route, ship-flow, strike, or damage claim for the issue to matter. Its bounded value is that multiple same-day voices describe the same practical asymmetry: Iran only has to make passage politically conditioned, while the United States has to prove that passage is commercially tolerable, depoliticized, and no longer dependent on Iranian permission or a wider Lebanon bargain. That is a harder task than declaring the Strait open.

Marandi shapes the judgment because he states the Iranian enforcement logic directly. Davis pressure-tests the same object from the U.S. side by arguing that force has not restored the previous transit baseline. Johnson adds a second U.S.-facing claim that the MOU may already be dead even while practical routing still favors Iran. Hoh and Barnes deepen the U.S. failure-mode reading by arguing that renewed bombing increases economic and escalation risk rather than restoring control. Mercouris and Krapivnik stay secondary because their value is cross-theater room constraint, not ownership of the Hormuz object.

The three July 8 hooks express that bounded reading. `NG-20260708-F01` asks whether Hormuz still appears as governed or conditional passage. `NG-20260708-F02` asks whether a bypass or dilution attempt produces a visible coercive test. `NG-20260708-F03` defines the falsifier: practical normalization plus reduced political salience of Iranian-managed routing or Lebanon linkage. Those hooks let the issue remain testable without promoting source assertions.

For archive use, the day should be read beside July 7 and July 9. July 7 names the settlement-breakdown gates. July 8 names the transit-governance mechanism. July 9 tests whether the burden of proving normalization has shifted onto Washington. The issue is ready because it preserves that sequence and marks where confidence ends.

The verification boundary is central. Routing, ship-flow, target, and strike claims remain source assertions until a later packet controls public factual use or forecast resolution. The issue owns the mechanism and the comparison among voices. It does not own operational truth.

The final use rule is procedural. Later synthesis should carry this issue forward when the same governed-passage mechanism recurs, when public actors continue to contest routing authority, or when shipping and pricing evidence becomes available for verification. It should not carry forward the issue merely because the transcript language is dramatic.

The migration from the older brief shape to the issue schema makes one analytical gain: it separates the lead mechanism from the pressure tests. The lead is not "bombing resumes" or "Iran escalates." The lead is the governed-passage object. Bombing, sanctions, Ukraine pressure, and NATO strain matter because they test whether the United States can restore a normal commercial order without accepting Iranian authority. They are not, by themselves, the day owner.

That distinction protects the archive from two opposite errors. The first error would be to treat every high-drama strike claim as proof that the Strait is closed or that war has fully expanded. The second error would be to treat nominal movement through the Strait as proof that the old order is restored. The July 8 issue sits between those errors. It says movement can continue while governance is still contested, and coercion can continue while normalization remains unproven.

The source mix supports that middle reading. Marandi gives the conditional-normalization logic. Davis and Johnson show that U.S.-side voices are also describing the old baseline as broken. Hoh and Barnes identify the economic and political trap in trying to bomb a commercial governance problem into submission. Mercouris and Krapivnik keep the wider room constraint visible. Pape provides the escalation mechanism that makes the day more than a set of anecdotes.

For monthly synthesis, July 8 should be treated as the naming day for the Hormuz transit-governance object. Later rows can strengthen it with observed route behavior, insurance pricing, official statements, or repeated coercive tests. Later rows can weaken it with durable normalization in both practice and language. Until then, the issue remains source-bounded but analytically useful.

The repair pass also makes the older brief easier to audit. The issue desk shows that all eight July 8 sources sit behind the lead, while the briefs narrow the two main supporting pressures: U.S. coercion risk and cross-theater room limits. That structure prevents the day from becoming a loose bundle of dramatic claims.

The most important retained uncertainty is the difference between public language and operating behavior. If later evidence shows that route use, pricing, and political language normalized together, the July 8 thesis weakens. If official claims of openness coexist with governed routing, sanctions escalation, or renewed coercive tests, the thesis strengthens.

That is the reason the issue belongs in the current schema. It lets later synthesis compare the same object across days without losing the verification boundary that keeps operational claims from being promoted too early.

A later reviewer should ask four bounded questions. Did major actors still describe passage as supervised, conditional, routed, or politically governed? Did a challenge to that authority produce a coercive response? Did commercial conditions normalize in both practice and language? Did Ukraine/NATO pressure continue to constrain U.S. bandwidth? Those questions keep the issue attached to observables rather than mood.

The answer may eventually narrow the July 8 thesis. That is fine. The point of the schema migration is not to inflate the old brief; it is to make the old brief legible to the current issue machinery and useful for later comparison.

That comparison is the practical payoff. July 8 can now sit beside July 7, July 9, and the later backfill issues as the named transit-governance day rather than as an older-format note that validation could not render.

It also gives the later monthly synthesis a stable label for the middle step in the sequence: not settlement-gate breakdown, and not yet promoted burden-of-proof argument, but the day when the archive names the operating object itself.

### NGI-20260708-S02 - U.S. Bombing Deepens the Coercion Trap

Davis, Hoh, Barnes, and Pape all pressure-test the U.S. coercion lane. Their shared implication is not that every bombing claim is verified, but that tactical action may worsen the bargaining environment if it fails to restore stable transit. That makes renewed bombing a trap mechanism rather than a simple display of control.

This brief supports the lead by showing why transit governance matters. If force cannot restore ordinary passage, then the issue is not the number of strikes alone. It is whether coercion can produce a usable commercial and political order.

### NGI-20260708-S03 - Ukraine and NATO Limit Strategic Room

Mercouris and Krapivnik keep Ukraine/NATO pressure in the background. Their role is contextual: they show that Washington is not operating from unlimited strategic slack while trying to manage Iran and Hormuz.

This brief should not displace the lead. It matters because cross-theater strain makes a long, disciplined coercive campaign harder to sustain and makes the transit-governance burden more difficult for Washington to discharge.
