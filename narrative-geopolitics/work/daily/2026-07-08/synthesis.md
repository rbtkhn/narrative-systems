## Lead Judgment

The July 8 batch says the MOU has crossed from stressed truce into practical
breakdown. The owning object is no longer "whether war resumes" in the abstract
or whether a nuclear track can be revived later. The owning object is whether
the Gulf now operates under Iranian-managed transit rules or under a restored
American assumption of free passage without Iranian enforcement authority.

Marandi states the Iranian logic most clearly: the strait normalizes if
Washington honors the agreement and accepts Iranian supervision; it
re-tightens if the United States tries to restore old transit habits while also
violating commitments on Lebanon and assets. Davis and Johnson, from the
U.S.-facing lane, converge on the same practical conclusion despite not sharing
Marandi's politics: the MOU may already be functionally dead, and U.S. strikes
do not appear to have removed Iranian control over the usable route. Hoh and
Barnes deepen rather than alter that judgment by arguing that renewed bombing
is more likely to worsen the oil and escalation trap than to restore stable
transit.

## Crisis Object

The contested object on July 8 is not simply "war resumed" or "nuclear talks
collapsed." It is whether the postwar Gulf order will operate under Iranian
managed passage or under a restored American assumption of free passage without
Iranian enforcement authority.

Why this matters: if the object is named incorrectly, the whole day gets
misread. A "war risk" framing pushes attention toward strikes, personalities,
and headlines. A "transit governance" framing puts attention where this batch
keeps putting it: route control, supervision, sanctions credibility, Lebanon
sequencing, and the practical question of who can decide what commercial
movement counts as normal.

## Draft Judgment

- Step 1: July 8 is the day the MOU stops reading like a damaged truce and starts reading like a broken transit-order arrangement.
- Step 2: The key evidence is the split between rhetoric and operating reality. Public U.S. escalation language is rising, but multiple same-day voices still describe actual ship movement as occurring through Iranian-approved routing.
- Step 3: Marandi's faucet model explains why this matters. Iran is not presenting itself as shutting Hormuz absolutely; it is presenting itself as the authority that can normalize or constrict passage depending on compliance.
- Step 4: Davis and Johnson matter because they narrow the gap between adversary rhetoric and U.S.-side recognition. Both imply that Washington is striking targets without restoring the old commercial baseline.
- Step 5: Hoh and Barnes matter because they identify the likely U.S. failure mode: tactical action without a durable commercial or political settlement path.
- Step 6: The bounded judgment is therefore narrower than "Iran is winning" or "the U.S. is losing." It is that the old transit baseline has not been reimposed, and the burden of proof has shifted onto Washington to show that force can produce normalized passage at all.
- Step 7: Mercouris and Krapivnik matter only insofar as they make that burden harder to discharge by showing that the wider strategic environment is already crowded, stressed, and less forgiving than U.S. rhetoric implies.

## Uncertainty

The batch is rich in mechanism and poor in independently verified operating
data. That means confidence is uneven and should be stated unevenly.

- High confidence: the day is centered on transit governance, supervision, and
  settlement credibility rather than on a narrow nuclear-only frame.
- Medium confidence: Iranian-managed routing remains practically salient even as
  U.S. strikes intensify.
- Lower confidence: the exact military effect of strikes, the precise state of
  ship flow, target damage, hidden losses, or the immediate tactical balance.

Several specific claims about strikes, target counts, carrier damage, and exact
shipping conditions remain source assertions rather than checked facts.
Marandi, Johnson, and Krapivnik all speak from politically loaded positions.
Davis is more valuable on practical-room framing than on definitive live
verification. Hoh and Barnes are strongest as pressure voices on U.S. failure
mode, not as neutral arbiters of battlefield fact.

The strongest grounded part of the judgment is therefore not any one tactical
claim. It is the cross-source convergence that Hormuz governance, not nuclear
paperwork, is the active object, and that U.S. coercion has not yet clearly
restored a normal transit baseline.
