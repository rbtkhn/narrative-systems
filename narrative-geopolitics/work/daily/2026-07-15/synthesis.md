## Lead Judgment

July 15 sharpens the July 14 Hormuz object from regional participation pressure into a ground-power dilemma. The source batch converges on a grim asymmetry: Washington can keep striking, but air and missile pressure is not described as sufficient to restore commercially tolerable passage on U.S. terms. The next temptation is therefore terrain: Kharg Island, coastal nodes, Kurdish or separatist pressure, or proxy "other people" on the ground. That temptation is precisely what the day treats as the trap.

The bounded judgment is not that a U.S. ground campaign has begun. It is that the political requirement to reverse a passage-governance loss is pushing the discourse toward ground options even as the voices most focused on military feasibility argue that such options would deepen the war without solving the underlying permission problem.

## Competing Explanations

1. **Ground-power trap:** Air and missile strikes cannot restore passage on U.S. terms, so the system moves toward ground options that increase lock-in.
2. **Coercive bargaining:** Public ground talk, infrastructure threats, and Gulf-base pressure are designed to produce a revised bargain before full ground escalation.
3. **Narrative overstatement:** Commentators may be converting ambiguous incidents and political rhetoric into a more coherent escalation story than the operational record supports.
4. **Managed-passage consolidation:** Iran does not need total closure if insurers, shippers, and Gulf states internalize that passage depends on Iranian tolerance.

## Judgment

It shows that the archive's central question has moved: the problem is no longer whether the MOU can be rhetorically revived, but whether Washington can accept a passage regime it does not control without reaching for ground options that would make the war harder to exit.
