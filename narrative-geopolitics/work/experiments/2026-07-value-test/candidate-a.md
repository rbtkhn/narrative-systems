# Candidate A: Who Governs Passage Through Hormuz?

As of: `2026-07-09`

Evidence boundary: selected June 23, July 7, July 8, and July 9 archive
sources only. Claims below describe this source set; they are not independently
verified accounts of ship movements, military effects, or market conditions.

The clearest judgment supported by these sources is narrower than either
“Hormuz is closed” or “Hormuz is open.” The dispute concerns whether passage
has returned to an ordinary commercial baseline or remains conditional on an
Iranian-administered political arrangement. Across the selected dates, several
speakers describe ships as continuing to move while also claiming that routing,
supervision, or the number of permitted vessels remains subject to Iranian
control. If that description is accurate, continued movement does not by itself
show that the earlier transit order has been restored.

The June 23 sources introduce the proposed mechanism. Marandi describes a new
Hormuz arrangement alongside delayed IAEA access, placing transit inside a
wider implementation sequence rather than treating it as a standalone freedom
of navigation question. The same day’s source stack also includes disagreement
about whether Iran could trust U.S. commitments and whether a memorandum could
survive pressure from the Lebanon and Israel files. These are source
assertions, not proof that any particular operating arrangement existed, but
they identify the terms on which later claims should be read.

By July 7, the selected sources describe renewed U.S. strikes and waiver
reversals while continuing to connect Hormuz to Lebanon. Marandi’s appearance
claims that there will be no deal unless Lebanon is resolved. Barnes argues
that reversible sanctions relief damages the credibility of any bargain, while
Davis describes material limits on prolonged U.S. military action. The sources
therefore converge on one interpretive point: renewed coercion had not produced
a settlement accepted by both sides. They do not independently establish
Iranian control of shipping, the effectiveness of strikes, or the size of U.S.
inventories.

The July 8 batch sharpens the distinction between rhetoric and operating
reality. Marandi, Davis, and Johnson each describe ships as using routes allowed
or supervised by Iran even as public escalation intensifies. This is
cross-source convergence in wording, but its independence is limited: the
speakers inhabit an overlapping interview ecosystem and may be repeating the
same underlying reports. The strongest responsible conclusion is therefore
that managed-passage claims remained central to the public argument—not that
those claims were conclusively demonstrated.

July 9 adds a practical counterpressure. Aguilar discusses possible U.S. naval,
air, and limited ground options for contesting control of the strait, but also
describes such operations as risky and without guaranteed success. His account
frames Hormuz as leverage Washington would need to obtain before it could trade
that leverage in negotiations over Lebanon. Again, this is a scenario offered
inside an interview, not evidence that such an operation had been selected or
launched.

Three hypotheses remain live. First, Iranian-managed passage may have become
the operative arrangement even without formal closure. Second, the arrangement
may be temporary coercive disruption that will fade as commercial traffic
normalizes. Third, political rhetoric may be outrunning actual operational
control on all sides. The authorized source set cannot distinguish these
hypotheses through ship counts, route data, insurance pricing, or official
implementation records.

What matters next is observable rather than rhetorical: whether ships use a
distinct supervised corridor, whether attempts to bypass it generate a visible
response, and whether Lebanon remains an explicit condition of bargaining. No
new probability-bearing forecast is offered here because the selected source
set does not independently establish the baseline needed to score one.
