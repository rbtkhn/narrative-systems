# Pape / Mearsheimer Comparison

This note records the retrieval difference between Robert Pape and John Mearsheimer for Narrative Geopolitics synthesis.

Status: `working-comparison`

## Core Distinction

Pape is the mechanism-and-falsifier voice.

Mearsheimer is the structure-and-bargaining-geometry voice.

They often converge on anti-coercion conclusions, but they are not interchangeable. Pape explains how coercive pressure can widen conflict or fail on observable thresholds. Mearsheimer explains why incentives, red lines, and great-power geometry make coercive success unlikely in the first place.

## Quick Retrieval Rule

- Use [Pape](../pape/README.md) first when the question is about escalation mechanism, forecast hooks, blockade timing, MOU fragility, or what would falsify a claim.
- Use [Mearsheimer](../mearsheimer/README.md) first when the question is about structural constraint, security dilemma, bargaining geometry, or why punishment is unlikely to produce surrender.
- Use both when the daily item turns on coercive failure and we need both the mechanism and the structural reason.

## Comparison Grid

| Retrieval question | Pape | Mearsheimer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main unit of analysis | Mechanism, threshold, forecast hook | State incentive, red line, bargaining structure |
| Main question | What escalation mechanism is unfolding, and what would falsify it? | What structural incentives make this crisis likely or hard to end? |
| Best evidence form | Authored pieces plus guest pressure tests | Host-conditioned interview lanes plus structural claim map |
| Strongest value | Falsifiable escalation and deal-fragility analysis | Durable constraint and anti-surrender logic |
| Main distortion risk | Forecast overextension or threshold urgency | Over-structuring and underweighting agency or implementation |
| Best use in synthesis | Daily mechanism tracking and review hooks | Strategic framing and bargaining-location |

## Where They Overlap

- Both are strong on coercive failure.
- Both are useful when punishment does not produce surrender.
- Both can explain why a war expands beyond the political goals that launched it.
- Both are valuable for judging whether a ceasefire or MOU is real or merely temporary.

## Where They Diverge

### Pape

Pape is strongest when we need to know what specific mechanism is operating now.

He is the better first retrieval voice for:

- smart-bomb trap logic
- escalation ladders tied to observable thresholds
- blockade / shortage timing
- Gulf risk premium and material pressure
- MOU / deal fragility with review hooks

He is weaker when:

- the question is primarily about deep structural incentives rather than near-term mechanism
- the daily task needs less forecasting and more stable strategic location

### Mearsheimer

Mearsheimer is strongest when we need to know why coercive success is structurally hard even before the mechanism finishes playing out.

He is the better first retrieval voice for:

- security dilemma logic
- bargaining impossibility / no-offramp arguments
- punishment-does-not-equal-surrender reasoning
- limits of American power as a strategic constraint
- Israel as structural liability
- NATO / order-transition implications of crisis widening

He is weaker when:

- we need a concrete falsifier
- we need a timing mechanism
- we need a bounded daily review hook rather than a structural explanation

## Best Pairings

### Coercive Failure

Start with Pape when the question is "what trap or escalation process is operating?"

Bring in Mearsheimer when the question becomes "why was surrender unlikely even if pressure intensified?"

### Bargaining Space

Start with Mearsheimer when the question is whether real settlement space exists at all.

Bring in Pape when the question becomes whether the apparent settlement is stable, fragile, or likely to widen into a new phase.

### Public Stakes vs Internal Judgment

Use Pape for internal monitoring, forecast hooks, and disciplined daily review.

Use Mearsheimer for the higher-level strategic frame that tells us where the crisis sits in the wider incentive map.

## Do-Not-Collapse Rules

- Do not collapse Pape's smart-bomb trap into Mearsheimer's structural realism. One is a mechanism with thresholds; the other is a constraint model.
- Do not collapse Mearsheimer's punishment-does-not-equal-surrender logic into Pape's escalation ledger. They may point to the same result for different reasons.
- Do not treat a shared anti-coercion conclusion as proof that either voice can replace the other.
- Do not use Mearsheimer alone when the synthesis needs a falsifier.
- Do not use Pape alone when the synthesis needs a durable explanation of why bargaining space is structurally narrow.

## Retrieval Recipes

### If the item is about bombing, strikes, or pressure widening the war

1. Open [Pape](../pape/README.md).
2. Check the smart-bomb trap, escalation ledger, or blockade clock claim family.
3. Add Mearsheimer only if we need the structural reason the pressure was unlikely to compel surrender.

### If the item is about ceasefire, MOU, or negotiations

1. Open [Mearsheimer](../mearsheimer/README.md) if the question is whether an offramp is structurally real.
2. Open [Pape](../pape/README.md) if the question is whether the agreement is stable, fragile, or reviewably failing.
3. Use both when the daily item needs both structural room and mechanism risk.

### If the item is about strategic defeat, overstretch, or failed US leverage

1. Open Mearsheimer first for the constraint logic.
2. Open Pape second if we need observable evidence of how the failure mechanism unfolded.

## Bottom Line

Pape tells us how coercion fails.

Mearsheimer tells us why coercion was structurally unlikely to succeed.

Narrative Geopolitics should use them together when the crisis object is coercive failure, but it should preserve which voice is answering which part of the question.
