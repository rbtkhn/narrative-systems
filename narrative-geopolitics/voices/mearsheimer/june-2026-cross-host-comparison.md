## Host Judgments

### Daniel Davis

Davis is the cleanest host lane for turning Mearsheimer into a usable internal mechanism voice. The June 11 source note centers ceasefire terms, sanctions relief, Hormuz reopening, and the political-economy cost of prolongation. The June 25 Davis appearance broadens that same lane into red-line testing and escalation consequences. This is the best lane when we want to ask what coercion can and cannot buy.

Use Davis first when:

- the synthesis question is whether pressure changes bargaining space
- we need a practical bridge between structural realism and forecast hooks
- we want the least rhetorical version of Mearsheimer's coercive-failure logic

### Judging Freedom

Napolitano reliably pulls Mearsheimer toward the Israel-liability and peace-veto axis. The June 2, June 19, June 23, and June 30 files all push on whether Israeli preferences distort or block US settlement choices. This lane is highly valuable for the structural-liability claim family, but it carries the most ideological host coloration and needs disciplined comparison against regional or mechanism voices before public use.

Use Judging Freedom first when:

- the item turns on whether Israel narrows American bargaining room
- we need the sharpest version of the peace-veto thesis
- we are still staying internal and can pressure-test the framing

### Breaking Points

Breaking Points compresses Mearsheimer into audience-legible balance-sheet language: Trump lost, Iran gains spoils, Russia escalation risks widen. This is useful for translating consequences, but not for first-pass analytic precision. The value here is downstream communication and stakes-clarification after we already know the mechanism.

Use Breaking Points first when:

- we need to summarize visible winners, losers, and immediate political stakes
- we are stress-testing whether a structural claim survives populist compression

### Tucker Carlson

Tucker's June 9 appearance pulls Mearsheimer into a broader decline-and-delusion register: US force resumes, elite strategy remains incoherent, and a clean exit remains unlikely. This lane is strong for limits-of-power mood and civilizational diagnosis, but it can over-amplify collapse rhetoric if used without the Davis or Diesen lanes.

Use Tucker first when:

- the task is to understand how elite failure is being narrated to a dissident-right audience
- we need a stress test of whether the limits-of-power thesis is becoming regime-legibility rather than just academic structure

### Tom Switzer

Switzer elicits a more diplomatic and comparative version of Mearsheimer. The June 21 source is the clearest host lane for "can the deal hold" rather than "who is to blame" or "who is collapsing." That makes it a strong companion source when a daily synthesis needs settlement durability rather than punishment or moral-liability emphasis.

Use Switzer first when:

- the question is whether a ceasefire or MOU has real staying power
- we need Mearsheimer in a less polemical comparative register

### Glenn Diesen

Diesen gives Mearsheimer the widest strategic altitude. The June 13 panel pushes deterrence doctrine, NATO escalation sequencing, European vulnerability, and order-transition risk. This is the best lane for systemic escalation ladders and the worst lane for grounded same-day tractability.

Use Diesen first when:

- the item needs placement on a wider great-power or nuclear ladder
- we are testing whether an event changes the structural balance rather than just the immediate bargaining position

## Best-Use Rules

- For coercive mechanism: start with `daniel-davis`, then compare with `tom-switzer` or `glenn-diesen`.
- For Israel-liability claims: start with `judging-freedom`, then pressure-test against `daniel-davis` or a regional voice before promotion.
- For public-stakes translation: start with `breaking-points`, but only after the mechanism is established elsewhere.
- For limits-of-power or elite-delusion framing: use `tucker-carlson` as a secondary comparison, not the sole retrieval lane.
- For order-transition or nuclear-risk framing: use `glenn-diesen`, then bring the claim back down through `daniel-davis` before daily forecast use.

## Synthesis Guidance

If a daily item only has one Mearsheimer source available, do not treat all host lanes as interchangeable.

- `daniel-davis` is the default internal retrieval lane.
- `judging-freedom` is the sharpest liability lane.
- `breaking-points` is the best compression lane.
- `tucker-carlson` is the best decline-rhetoric lane.
- `tom-switzer` is the cleanest settlement-durability lane.
- `glenn-diesen` is the best systemic-escalation lane.
