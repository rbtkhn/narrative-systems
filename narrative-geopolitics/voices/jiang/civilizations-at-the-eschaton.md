## An Encounter at the Boundary of Geopolitics

Jiang Xueqin's interest in Aleksandr Dugin is best understood as methodological before it is ideological. Dugin gives Jiang a language for joining domains that conventional geopolitical analysis usually keeps apart: national power, civilizational character, religious expectation, historical time, and the moral meaning that societies assign to conflict. Jiang does not approach Dugin merely as a commentator on Russia. He approaches him as a theorist who claims that political communities inhabit distinct historical and spiritual worlds—and that those worlds shape what states perceive as possible, necessary, and worth sacrificing for.

Jiang states the intellectual connection directly. He says that he became deeply interested in geopolitics and eschatology after reading Dugin's *Foundations of Geopolitics*, which he calls the most important geopolitical book of the modern era. More revealing than the praise is Jiang's explanation: behind a society's eschatology lies its "national character" or "national soul." In his account, eschatology is not an ornamental religious layer placed over material politics. It expresses a civilization's sense of where history is going, what constitutes redemption or catastrophe, and what kind of ending gives present sacrifices meaning.

That proposition supplies the center of Jiang's attraction to Dugin. Both men are interested in the possibility that geopolitical behavior cannot be adequately explained by material incentives alone. States pursue security, wealth, and influence, but the purposes attached to those goods differ. A society's deepest stories may determine which dangers it recognizes, which humiliations it cannot tolerate, and which futures it can imagine. Dugin offers Jiang a developed—if highly contestable—example of how to interpret those stories as active forces in world politics.

## From States to Civilizational Worlds

Dugin's importance to Jiang also lies in his insistence on civilizational plurality. In the conversation, Dugin describes Chinese, Russian, Islamic, and American societies as different "universes." His claim is stronger than the familiar observation that countries have different cultures. Each universe, in his telling, possesses its own relationship to time, order, good and evil, sacred authority, and historical destiny.

For Jiang, this opens a route beyond geopolitical analysis that assumes all actors ultimately seek the same liberal-modern ends. If different civilizations do not merely choose different policies but organize reality through different historical grammars, then strategic prediction requires interpretation as well as calculation. The analyst must ask not only what resources an actor possesses, but what story makes the use of those resources intelligible.

This is especially relevant to Jiang's own position between Chinese experience and Western intellectual history. Dugin portrays Chinese civilization as oriented toward balance and harmony rather than a linear, terminal struggle between good and evil. He then presents Jiang as an unusual Chinese scholar because Jiang has seriously studied Jewish, Christian, Islamic, and other eschatological traditions. Whether or not Dugin's broad characterization of Chinese thought is defensible, the exchange identifies the role Jiang seems to want: a translator among civilizational models of historical time.

Dugin is valuable to that project because Russia supplies a particularly strong test case. Dugin explicitly connects Russian geopolitical conduct to Orthodoxy, tradition, imperial memory, and a claimed providential mission. Jiang can therefore examine a worldview in which metaphysical commitments are asserted rather than hidden. The Russian case becomes a laboratory for asking whether civilizational narratives predict behavior, rationalize behavior after the fact, mobilize constituencies, or perform some mixture of all three.

## A Shared Critique of Liberal Modernity

Jiang and Dugin also meet in a critique of Western modernity, although they reach it through different routes. Jiang traces the modern Western order through the Bank of England, public debt, imperial finance, private property, utilitarianism, individualism, and consumer society. His account treats philosophical developments partly as legitimating structures for a financial and imperial system. Dugin pushes the genealogy backward through Calvinism and interprets modernity as a spiritual decline away from sacred tradition.

The convergence matters. Both reject the idea that liberal modernity is a neutral platform upon which politics occurs. They treat it as a historically specific system with its own anthropology, moral psychology, institutions, and implicit account of history. Both are therefore interested in the way concepts such as progress, rationality, individual freedom, and material prosperity function as civilizational commitments rather than universally self-evident goods.

Yet the overlap should not be mistaken for identity. Jiang's explanation is more institutional and genealogical: finance creates political capacity, and philosophy helps legitimate the resulting social order. Dugin's is more theological: modernity represents anti-tradition and spiritual inversion. Their diagnoses can reinforce one another rhetorically while relying on different causal mechanisms. That distinction is crucial for using this source responsibly. Agreement on an object of criticism does not prove agreement on why it arose, how it operates, or what should replace it.

## Dugin as an Intellectual Foil

The clearest evidence that Jiang's interest is not simple discipleship appears near the end of the conversation. Jiang agrees that Western civilization contains ideas he considers antihuman, citing individualism, rationalism, consumerism, and utilitarianism. But he then introduces a problem that Dugin's account can understate: liberal-material modernity is attractive. It has produced wealth, technological power, and dramatic improvements in material life. Many people will defend it not because they have been deceived about its costs, but because they value its benefits.

Jiang applies this challenge directly to China. A society that understands its modern history partly through development and the lifting of vast numbers of people from poverty may not find an apocalyptic struggle against modernity compelling. Dugin's spiritual urgency therefore encounters Jiang's strategic realism about preference formation. If people experience a system as materially successful, what would make them reject it? How much explanatory or mobilizing power does eschatology possess when set against prosperity, comfort, and social stability?

This disagreement makes Dugin more useful to Jiang, not less. Dugin represents the strongest form of the claim that ideas, sacred missions, and providential history ultimately govern events. Jiang probes the boundary of that claim by asking how spiritual narratives compete with material incentives. Their exchange thus stages a larger unresolved question within Jiang's method: are civilizational stories causal engines, elite instruments, retrospective rationalizations, or latent structures activated under crisis?

## The Search for a Moral Grammar

Jiang's interest also has a personal-intellectual dimension. He describes growing up in an atheist society, remaining skeptical of religion for much of his life, and more recently developing what he calls a gnostic framework without adopting a single religious loyalty. His study of eschatology, Kabbalah, geopolitics, and evil appears to be part of a search for a moral grammar capable of explaining phenomena that a purely strategic or material vocabulary leaves unsatisfied.

Dugin is compelling in this context because he combines an explicit Orthodox commitment with comparative study of multiple traditions. He claims one confessional truth while treating other religious systems as objects of serious philosophical and geopolitical inquiry. Jiang does not share that settled confessional position. Dugin consequently offers both a model and a contrast: a thinker who integrates metaphysical certainty into political analysis, encountered by a thinker still exploring whether and how such integration is possible.

This does not validate the empirical claims made in the conversation. Some historical genealogies are sweeping, some causal chains are asserted rather than demonstrated, and some claims about religious or political groups require substantial independent scrutiny. The analytical significance lies elsewhere: Jiang is testing whether a moral-metaphysical account can reveal dimensions of political behavior that game theory and material interest alone cannot capture.

## What the Relationship Means for the Jiang Voice Model

Within Narrative Geopolitics, Dugin should be treated as an influence, interlocutor, and pressure test for Jiang—not as a key that unlocks Jiang's entire corpus. The April conversation was their first direct encounter. It was hosted in a channel environment oriented toward provocative civilizational and religious discussion. It therefore provides strong evidence of intellectual interest but weak evidence of a stable alliance or representative everyday register.

The source supports four bounded conclusions:

1. Jiang explicitly credits Dugin's *Foundations of Geopolitics* with helping initiate his study of geopolitics and eschatology.
2. Jiang finds Dugin useful for connecting national character, civilizational identity, religious expectation, and geopolitical behavior.
3. Jiang shares parts of Dugin's critique of liberal modernity but supplies a more material and institutional genealogy.
4. Jiang preserves a consequential disagreement: prosperity makes modernity attractive, limiting the mobilizing force of Dugin's spiritual opposition to it.

The deeper interpretation is that Dugin helps Jiang move from forecasting events to interpreting the historical worlds that make events conceivable. Dugin's thought proposes that civilizations are not simply players on one game board; they may inhabit different understandings of the game, its stakes, and its ending. Jiang is interested in that proposition because it could enlarge predictive analysis beyond incentives and capabilities.

But it also creates a methodological danger. Once geopolitics is read through eschatological patterns, dramatic events can too easily become confirmation of an unfalsifiable civilizational script. The productive question for Jiang's archive is therefore not whether Dugin's metaphysics is "correct." It is whether this layer yields discriminating, time-bounded expectations that outperform ordinary strategic analysis—and whether contrary evidence is allowed to revise the story.

That is the most useful meaning of Jiang's interest in Dugin: not an endorsement to be inherited wholesale, but an experiment in whether geopolitics requires an account of the sacred stories through which civilizations understand history.
