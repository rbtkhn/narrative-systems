# Dialogue Contract

This contract governs corpus-grounded dialogue with curated intellectual
voices. Its purpose is realistic intellectual continuity, not impersonation.

## Core Boundary

A reconstructed voice answers only from an identified corpus available as of a
stated date. It represents a defensible reconstruction of an intellectual
framework, not the person's current private beliefs and not a prediction of
what the person would literally say.

Every dialogue must identify:

- the exact question and as-of date;
- the participating voices and why each is included;
- the authorized source boundary and important omissions;
- relevant channel conditioning;
- the moderator, if any, as a separate analytical role.

## Epistemic Labels

Use these labels at the claim or paragraph level whenever ambiguity matters:

- `direct_support`: the corpus states the proposition directly; cite it.
- `close_paraphrase`: wording is compressed but meaning remains source-bound.
- `characteristic_inference`: a recurring framework is applied to the question.
- `bounded_extrapolation`: the framework is extended beyond observed cases;
  state the added assumption and reduce confidence.
- `internal_tension`: the voice's own record supports competing readings.
- `unknown`: the authorized corpus does not support a responsible answer.

Never render inference or extrapolation as quotation. Quotations must resolve to
an archive source and retain enough context to preserve their meaning.

## Reconstruction Rules

1. Draft each voice independently before reading the moderator synthesis.
2. Prefer conceptual and causal fidelity over verbal mannerism.
3. Preserve changes across time; do not combine incompatible periods into a
   timeless persona.
4. Preserve contradictions and failed forecasts rather than harmonizing them.
5. Condition claims on host, show, and source modality when those factors alter
   interpretation.
6. Say `unknown` when a novel fact pattern outruns the corpus.
7. Distinguish what the voice claims from what the evidence establishes.
8. Do not let one well-developed voice set the vocabulary for every other
   response.

## Dialogue and Cross-Examination

A multi-voice exchange should expose disagreement, not manufacture theatrical
banter. A reconstructed reply may challenge another response only by identifying:

- the disputed premise or mechanism;
- the responding voice's corpus-supported alternative;
- evidence that would distinguish the accounts;
- any point the responding corpus cannot answer.

Do not invent interpersonal attitudes, emotional reactions, or familiarity
between voices.

## Moderator Boundary

The moderator is the system's analytical voice, not another reconstructed
person. It may map agreements, disagreements, blind spots, and decision
implications. It must cite load-bearing claims, expose unequal corpus depth, and
avoid converting convergence among overlapping channels into independent
confirmation.

The moderator may reach a judgment that no participant holds. It must label
that judgment as synthesis rather than attributing it to the council.

## Fidelity Review

Before promotion, score the dialogue on:

| Dimension | Passing condition |
| --- | --- |
| Source fidelity | Load-bearing voice claims resolve to the authorized corpus. |
| Attribution precision | Quotes, paraphrases, inference, and extrapolation remain distinct. |
| Temporal integrity | The response respects the as-of date and changes across time. |
| Voice differentiation | Participants use materially distinct mechanisms and priors. |
| Contradiction preservation | Relevant tensions and failed forecasts remain visible. |
| Generative restraint | Unsupported questions produce limits or `unknown`, not invention. |
| Reader value | The exchange reveals something an ordinary synthesis obscures. |

Failure on source fidelity, attribution precision, or generative restraint
blocks publication. Public dialogue must state prominently that it is a
corpus-bounded reconstruction and not an actual interview.
