# Strategic Briefings: Iran War and the Middle Game of Escalation

### How Regional Wars Spread Through Systems

### Prof Robert Pape

Wars rarely expand in a straight line. They spread through systems.

The Iran conflict may now be entering its strategic middle game. And the middle game is where the most dangerous escalation traps are set.

The early phase of war is dominated by events: air strikes, retaliation, battlefield damage. The middle game begins when expectations change—when governments, markets, and security institutions begin preparing for a longer and more dangerous conflict.

Those expectations alter behavior long before any formal decision to escalate is made.

Signals of that shift are already visible—if you know where to look.

To understand how a regional war spreads through these pathways, it helps to recall another crisis more than a century ago.

## When Systems Spread Wars

In June 1914 the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand initially appeared to be a localized confrontation between Austria-Hungary and Serbia.

Within weeks it became a continental war.

Russia mobilized to defend Serbia. Germany declared war on Russia and France. German armies marched through Belgium toward Paris. By August, Britain had entered the conflict and the war had spread across Europe and the global empires tied to the great powers.

The mechanism was not a single decision. It was horizontal escalation across interconnected systems.

Alliance commitments tied the security of multiple powers together. Railway mobilization timetables meant that once armies began moving they could not easily stop. Trade and financial networks linked the industrial economies of Europe long before the shooting began.

In effect, the infrastructure of the international system had already connected the major powers into a tightly coupled network.

Local crisis → alliance mobilization → continental war

World War I did not become global because armies suddenly expanded everywhere.

It became global because the systems linking states pulled them in.

Today the architecture of globalization performs a similar role. Energy flows through the Strait of Hormuz. Shipping routes, financial markets, and supply chains bind the world economy together in ways that can transmit disruption far beyond the battlefield.

The Iran conflict may now be entering a comparable phase—not a world war of mass armies, but a systemic escalation spreading through global networks.

Evidence of this dynamic is already visible. Iranian drones and missiles have struck targets or airspace in multiple states across the region, including Kuwait, Jordan, and Oman.

What began as a confrontation centered on Iran is spreading outward across the wider Gulf security system. The early phase of war is about military exchanges.

The middle game begins when the systems around the battlefield start reacting.

## The Oil System: The First Transmission Mechanism

If railways and alliances helped spread the First World War, the modern world has its own tightly coupled system capable of transmitting shock.

That system is global energy flows.

Roughly 20–21 million barrels of oil pass through the Strait of Hormuz each day—nearly one-fifth of global petroleum consumption and about a quarter of all seaborne oil trade. Around 80 percent of those shipments move to Asian economies, making the strait the single most important artery connecting Middle Eastern production to global markets.

Nearly a fifth of the world's oil moves through a waterway only 21 miles wide.

Block that artery and the global economy risks a cardiac shock.

Under normal conditions the system operates with remarkable efficiency. Tankers load at export terminals in Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, and the United Arab Emirates. They pass through Hormuz and distribute supply across refineries from Shanghai to Rotterdam.

But tightly coupled systems transmit disruption as easily as they transmit commerce.

Small attacks = cascading effects: A handful of attacks on shipping, a temporary closure of a port, or even credible threats against tankers can ripple outward through the entire network. Insurance premiums spike. Tanker traffic slows. Traders immediately price geopolitical risk into energy markets.

Local attack → tanker disruption → global price shock

At first such shocks appear temporary.

Governments release strategic petroleum reserves to calm markets, and traders assume the disruption will pass.

But repeated disruptions begin to change expectations.

Early signals of that shift are already visible. Attacks on shipping and energy infrastructure have pushed oil prices above $100 despite emergency releases from strategic reserves. Some exporters have begun rerouting shipments away from the Gulf entirely.

This is not simply supply volatility. It is the first sign that expectations about the duration of the conflict are beginning to change.

The strategic leverage here does not depend on physically sealing the Strait of Hormuz. Because the energy system operates with tight logistical timing and limited spare capacity, even partial disruption can produce ripple effects across the global economy.

The battlefield may remain geographically limited. The economic shock does not.

## The Nuclear System: Visibility Under Stress

Energy shocks are the most immediate signal of escalation.

A second signal appears inside the nuclear system.

For years Iran has accumulated significant quantities of enriched uranium, including material enriched to levels approaching weapons-grade. Current estimates suggest roughly 1,000 pounds enriched to about 60 percent and roughly 10,000 pounds enriched to lower levels around 5–20 percent.

Much of this stockpile has historically been concentrated in a small number of known facilities monitored—however imperfectly—by international inspectors.

In normal circumstances that concentration creates a degree of stability.

A small number of fixed sites can be tracked, inspected, and monitored. The global nonproliferation system rests on a simple assumption: nuclear material remains visible and geographically bounded.

War changes that equation.

Strikes on nuclear facilities = emergency dispersal by Iranian authorities, or deliberate concealment designed to preserve deterrent capabilities can scatter nuclear material across multiple locations inside a country roughly two-and-a-half times the size of Texas.

Recent events suggest that some form of dispersal may already be occurring. Satellite imagery reportedly showed convoys of trucks leaving the Fordow enrichment facility shortly before U.S. strikes in June. Weeks earlier, large areas near the Isfahan complex had been excavated, possibly to create new underground storage sites.

Once dispersal begins, the strategic problem changes fundamentally.

Military strikes → dispersal of nuclear material → global proliferation risk

Instead of monitoring a handful of facilities, intelligence agencies must search for mobile or hidden stockpiles across a vast territory with extensive underground infrastructure.

The danger is not immediate nuclear war. The danger is the gradual erosion of visibility.

Material that cannot be precisely located creates multiple risks: covert movement between facilities, possible transfer to allied groups, or the emergence of clandestine weapons pathways outside international monitoring regimes.

For decades the nonproliferation system has relied on a simple principle: Nuclear material must remain visible.

War, however, disrupts visibility. And once visibility erodes, expectations about the future trajectory of the conflict begin to shift.

## The Expectations Shift

Energy disruption and nuclear uncertainty illustrate how wars spread through physical systems. But the most powerful escalation pathway often operates through something less visible: Expectations.

Wars do not expand only because new weapons are used or new targets are struck. They expand when leaders, markets, and institutions begin adjusting their behavior based on what they expect others will do next.

As those expectations interact across governments, markets, and security institutions, they begin reinforcing one another—escalation can become self-fulfilling.

Economists understand this dynamic in the context of inflation expectations. When firms believe inflation will rise, they raise prices and wages in advance—producing the very inflation they anticipated. Escalation works the same way.

The early phase of the Iran conflict illustrates the mechanism. Initial strikes were widely interpreted as limited operations designed to signal resolve without triggering a broader war. But as retaliation followed and regional tensions mounted, that assumption began to erode. Expectations began to move.

Once expectations shift, preparation follows. Military planners expand operational options. Intelligence agencies broaden surveillance priorities. Governments quietly prepare for longer disruptions.

Markets move in parallel. Energy traders hedge against sustained supply shocks. Shipping insurers raise risk premiums. Corporations begin planning for instability in logistics and energy costs.

Limited conflict → expectation of wider disruption → precautionary actions

Individually these actions appear defensive. Collectively they change the strategic environment.

Governments deploy additional forces to reassure allies. Rivals interpret those deployments as preparation for wider war. Each side then adjusts again in response.

Preparation for escalation → perceived threat → further preparation

At that point escalation no longer depends solely on the intentions of the original combatants. It begins emerging from the structure of expectations inside the system itself.

## Detecting the Strategic Middle Game

If the middle game of escalation is defined by shifting expectations, the most revealing signals will not be battlefield events alone. They will be signs that governments and markets are quietly preparing for a different kind of war.

The earliest signals tend to appear in a few predictable places.

Energy policy often moves first. Emergency releases from strategic petroleum reserves or discussions of export restrictions signal that policymakers believe supply shocks could persist. Such measures are rarely taken for conflicts expected to end quickly.

Military deployments provide another clue. Additional missile-defense systems deployed across Gulf states would suggest governments expect repeated attacks rather than isolated retaliation.

When multiple states expand defensive coverage simultaneously, expectations about the duration of conflict are already changing.

Signals surrounding nuclear material security may prove even more revealing. If enriched uranium is dispersed across multiple locations, the operational challenge changes dramatically.

Air strikes can destroy facilities. They cannot reliably locate mobile stockpiles.

Securing those materials historically requires forces on the ground.

For that reason, one of the most revealing indicators of shifting expectations would be increasing leaks about contingency planning to secure nuclear material inside Iran.

Such discussions rarely appear first as formal policy proposals. They emerge through background briefings, trial balloons in major newspapers, or references to "options under review."

Once the question enters elite policy debate—who secures the material—expectations about the trajectory of the conflict may already be shifting toward a longer and more complex war.

During the Cold War analysts sometimes looked for warning signals in mundane places: lights burning late at night in the Pentagon or sudden surges of pizza deliveries to national security offices in Washington.

Those clues reflected a simple reality.

When expectations shift inside governments, behavior changes before policy announcements appear.

Today the signals are different, but the principle remains the same.

Satellite imagery showing unusual logistics activity, emergency energy measures, or recurring leaks about nuclear security planning reveal something deeper than isolated events. They reveal expectations moving across the system.

And once expectations begin reinforcing one another across governments, markets, and military institutions, the Escalation Trap tightens.

## The Homeland Pathway

The middle game of escalation can also reach far beyond the battlefield. One pathway runs directly into the United States itself.

Recent weeks have already produced a troubling pattern: terrorism.

Investigations are underway following a mass shooting in Austin, an attempted IED attack near Gracie Mansion in New York, a vehicle attack at Temple Israel synagogue in Michigan, and a firebombing at a rally in Colorado. Federal authorities have also disrupted plots involving drones launched from vessels near the California coast and threats against a federal courthouse in Cincinnati.

These incidents remain limited in scale. But they illustrate a broader danger.

The risk is not simply isolated attacks inspired by extremist propaganda. Unlike groups such as the Islamic State, Iran is a state sponsor of terrorism with decades of experience providing technology, training, and operational guidance to proxy networks across the Middle East.

That distinction matters.

State-backed actors can supply capabilities far more sophisticated than those typically available to lone extremists: advanced explosives, drone technology, or coordinated attacks against infrastructure.

Air campaigns against states often trigger asymmetric retaliation.

Regional war → pressure on a state sponsor → activation of proxy networks

If that pathway develops, the Iran conflict would open a new front not in the Gulf but inside the United States itself.

This is how the middle game of escalation unfolds.

The war spreads not because a single decision expands it, but because multiple systems—energy markets, nuclear security institutions, military alliances, and covert networks—begin preparing for a larger conflict at the same time.

And once those expectations begin reinforcing one another, the Escalation Trap becomes much harder to escape.
