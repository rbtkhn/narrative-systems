What It Actually Means to “Open” Hormuz
Every war begins with the same question: What counts as victory?

In the battle for the Strait of Hormuz, there are two different standards.

The tactical standard measures military performance. Have missile launchers been destroyed? Have attacks declined? Have islands been secured? Has freedom of navigation been established?

The strategic standard measures whether the political objective of the war has been achieved. In Hormuz, that objective is straightforward: the return of normal commercial shipping.

Those standards are not the same.

Commercial Victory Is Strategic Victory

The United States is not fighting simply to destroy Iranian military assets. It is fighting to restore the uninterrupted flow of global commerce through the Strait of Hormuz.

That objective is achieved only when commercial shipping returns to roughly prewar levels because tanker operators, marine insurers, commodity traders, and governments conclude that transiting Hormuz has again become a routine commercial decision rather than an extraordinary wartime risk.

Military commanders evaluate battlefield performance.

Markets determine whether strategic success has actually been achieved.

Commercial Markets Require Near-Complete Security

Commercial shipping does not require perfect safety. Tankers routinely operate despite piracy, storms, and localized conflict. But they cannot operate normally when successful attacks remain a recurring possibility.

That judgment belongs to private actors—not governments.

Governments can escort ships. Navies can patrol sea lanes. Military commanders can declare routes secure.

Only shipping companies decide whether to sail. Only insurers decide whether affordable coverage is available. Only commodity markets determine whether supply chains have become reliable again.

One successful strike on a commercial tanker can reverse months of restored confidence almost overnight.

Iran retains tens of thousands of drones and missiles according to US intelligence estimates leaked in April and May -- and may have more now. Only 50-100 are needed to swarm ships successfully, well within Iran’s capacity to produce on a monthly basis.

This Is the Strategic Problem

The United States cannot achieve its political objective until Iran loses not merely the capacity to wage war -- but also loses the capacity to convince commercial markets that shipping through Hormuz remains dangerous.

Everything that follows explains why reaching that far more demanding strategic standard before the November midterm elections is unlikely.

Iran is Simply Too Large to Suppress Quickly
Iran is simply too large to suppress drones and missiles quickly. That—not Iranian ideology, not American military weakness, and not diplomatic failure—is the central military reality driving the next phase of the war.

To reopen the Strait of Hormuz, the United States does not merely need to degrade Iranian forces. It must suppress every remaining capability capable of threatening commercial shipping. That is a fundamentally different military problem. Again, 50-100 drones or missiles would be sufficient to swam ships near Hormuz, and Iran now has many thousands.

The scale of that suppression problem is enormous. Iran covers roughly 1.65 million square kilometers—more than three times the size of Iraq and about four times California. Across that vast territory lie mountain ranges, deserts, islands, thousands of kilometers of coastline, hardened underground facilities, dispersed missile depots, drone production sites, and countless locations from which mobile launchers can operate.

Why There Is No Such Thing as a Limited Landing in Iran
The most dangerous illusion in Washington is that the United States could conduct a limited ground operation to reopen Hormuz.

Iran has every incentive to ensure that no such operation remains limited.

In the media, there are calls to seize a single island, a port, or a narrow stretch of coastline as though these were self-contained objectives. They are not. Once American forces land, they immediately become large, fixed targets that must be defended. Iran does not need to stop the landing. It needs only to make remaining there dangerous enough that the mission expands.

That is precisely when geography favors the defender.

Much of Iran’s coastline rises rapidly into broken hills and rugged mountains overlooking the narrow coastal plain. Those ridgelines provide thousands of concealed firing positions for small drone teams, anti-tank missiles, mortars, rockets, reconnaissance units, and mobile missile launchers. Airpower can destroy identified targets, but it cannot continuously neutralize every cave, ravine, ledge, tunnel entrance, and reverse slope from which defenders can repeatedly emerge.

Iran therefore does not need to defeat a Marine landing outright.

It can simply allow American forces to concentrate ashore before attacking the much larger and more vulnerable force that has now committed itself to remaining there.

That changes the military problem immediately.

Protecting several thousand Marines on a beachhead requires controlling not simply the beach but the surrounding high ground, road network, logistics sites, air-defense positions, and artillery approaches. Every additional mile secured creates another perimeter that must itself be defended. Every new defensive line requires additional troops. Every reinforcement creates additional supply requirements that themselves become targets.

Military planners recognize this dynamic. A Marine Expeditionary Unit of roughly 2,200–2,500 Marines is designed for highly limited areas of control— think 1 mile by 2 mile areas, not occupying and defending an expanding coastal battlespace against an organized state military. Once resistance proves persistent, pressure inevitably builds to commit larger formations capable of holding wider terrain.

That is exactly the escalation in US troop deployments that favors Iran.

Why the Political Clock Matters
The United States is not fighting against Iran’s military alone. It is fighting against America’s political calendar.

Iran does not need to defeat the United States militarily.

It needs only to prevent Washington from reopening Hormuz before November.

If Hormuz remains contested through the election, Congress—not CENTCOM—may become the decisive constraint on American strategy.

Political time – the US midterms in November – matter decisively.

Iran is fighting for regime survival. The Islamic Republic can absorb years of military pressure if its leaders and millions of supporters believe the alternative is political collapse.

The United States is fighting for a far narrower objective: reopening the Strait of Hormuz while avoiding a prolonged war that threatens the global economy and domestic political stability.

Those are fundamentally different wars with different political time.

Supporters of expanding the Iran war often argue that military necessity—not politics—should determine how long the campaign continues. In theory, that is true. In practice, American wars have always depended on continued political support at home because Congress ultimately controls appropriations, emergency military funding, oversight, and the political legitimacy necessary to sustain long campaigns.

Vietnam illustrates the point.

By 1974, American combat forces had largely left Southeast Asia, yet President Gerald Ford still sought additional military assistance to preserve South Vietnam. A Congress that had shifted sharply against the war refused. The following year, further restrictions on military funding effectively ended Washington’s ability to continue the conflict. Military conditions mattered. Political control of Congress ultimately determined whether the war could continue.

In Vietnam, Congress acted because time allowed domestic politics to catch up with military stalemate.

The same institutional reality exists today.

If Republicans retain control of Congress after November, President Trump will almost certainly possess greater latitude to expand military operations if reopening Hormuz continues proving elusive. If Democrats capture either chamber, congressional investigations, funding battles, and legislative resistance will substantially narrow the administration’s options—even if military commanders argue that additional operations remain necessary.

Iran does not have to win the military contest. It only has to keep the contest going until America’s political clock overtakes its military clock. That is the strategic advantage Tehran now possesses.
