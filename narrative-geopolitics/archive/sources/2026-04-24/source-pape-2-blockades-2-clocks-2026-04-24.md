# 2 Blockades, 2 Clocks: Why Neither Side Will Blink

What Washington is dangerously wrong about in the Iran War

Ten days ago, I argued the conflict was entering a new phase. That shift is now visible—but much of the analysis still rests on a flawed premise: that enough economic punishment will force Iran to back down. A century of evidence suggests otherwise. Economic pressure rarely compels states to concede on core security issues. More often, it sets in motion a predictable sequence—pain, resistance, and escalation.

## I. The Myth of Sanctions/Economic Pressure Success

For decades, policymakers have relied on a widely cited statistic: economic sanctions succeed roughly one-third of the time. That estimate comes from the influential dataset compiled by Gary Hufbauer, Jeffrey Schott, and Kimberly Ann Elliott, which examined 115 cases since World War I.

But that number does not survive scrutiny, as I found in my academic research.

When those cases are recoded to control for the use of force—the key methodological step missing from the original dataset—the success rate falls dramatically. Of the 40 “successes” identified, many involve either no actual concession, misclassification, or, most importantly, outcomes driven by direct or indirect military coercion. These include cases where battlefield victories, coup operations, or explicit invasion threats—not economic pressure—produced the outcome.

Once those are removed, fewer than 5 percent of cases show evidence that sanctions alone produced meaningful policy change.

This is not a marginal correction. It overturns the dominant empirical foundation for sanctions as a tool of coercion.

The implication is straightforward: what often appears to be economic success is, in fact, military coercion operating under the cover of economic pressure.

## II. The Logic Problem: Economic Punishment Does Not Translate Into Political Concession

The appeal of sanctions and blockades rests on a simple logic: increasing costs on civilians will pressure governments to change behavior. But this logic mirrors the same flawed assumption underlying coercive bombing campaigns—that suffering translates into political leverage.

It usually does not.

Across cases—from wartime blockades in World War I and World War II to modern sanctions regimes—the mechanism consistently breaks down. Governments facing external pressure rarely respond by conceding core interests. Instead, they prioritize survival and reallocate resources to maintain control.

This is why the most severe cases of economic punishment are often the least successful. Cuba has endured decades of sanctions without abandoning its political system. Iraq in the 1990s experienced GDP losses approaching 50 percent under one of the most comprehensive sanctions regimes in modern history, yet Saddam Hussein did not concede his core strategic position.

Severe economic contraction does not fragment regimes—it strengthens them. As resources shrink, the state gains greater control over distribution. Access to fuel, food, and employment becomes politicized, loyalty is rewarded, and opposition is marginalized.

Scarcity concentrates power.

## III. The Exception: When Sanctions Are Backed by Security Pressure from Allies

There is one important exception—but it reinforces rather than undermines the broader argument.

Sanctions can contribute to limited policy change when they are paired with direct security pressure from a target’s key external military partners. This is not economic coercion alone—it is a transformation of the target’s strategic environment.

During the Cold War, both South Korea and Taiwan abandoned nuclear weapons programs under U.S. pressure. But this outcome was not driven by sanctions alone. It reflected explicit U.S. threats to withdraw military protection, fundamentally altering the security calculus of both states.

A similar dynamic was present in the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. By 2012–2015, Iran faced not only U.S. and European sanctions but coordinated pressure from major powers, including Russia and China, through UN-backed restrictions and diplomatic alignment. Iranian oil exports fell sharply, access to the global financial system was curtailed, and inflation surged. Yet what made negotiations possible was not economic pain alone—it was the convergence of pressure from states that could otherwise offset U.S. leverage.

Even then, the outcome was limited. Iran accepted constraints on enrichment levels, stockpiles, and inspections under a time-bound framework, but it did not abandon its nuclear program, regional posture, or regime structure. This was a negotiated constraint under pressure—not a decisive concession.

The lesson is clear: sanctions can shape bargaining when paired with aligned security pressure. They do not independently compel surrender.

A more recent case reinforces the point. After Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine, the United States and its allies imposed unprecedented sanctions—freezing central bank assets, restricting technology exports, and cutting major banks off from global finance. Early expectations in Washington were that this pressure would cripple Russia’s economy and force a change in policy. Russia did suffer significant shocks: GDP contraction, inflation spikes, and loss of Western markets. But the Kremlin adapted—redirecting energy exports to Asia, stabilizing the currency, and reallocating resources toward regime-supporting sectors. Four years on, there has been no strategic capitulation. Economic pressure imposed costs, but it did not alter core war aims.

## IV. The Escalation Pattern: From Sanctions to Force

If sanctions rarely achieve their stated goals, why do they persist?

Because they serve a political function.

Economic pressure creates the perception that all non-military options have been exhausted. It reassures domestic audiences that restraint has been tried. But when sanctions fail to produce results—as they usually do—they do not resolve the conflict. They narrow the perceived choices.

Escalation begins to look inevitable.

This pattern recurs across cases: sanctions are imposed, expectations rise, results fail to materialize, and pressure builds for more decisive action. In many instances, this leads to the introduction of military force—coercive bombing, ground operations, or militarized regime change efforts.

Economic pressure and sanctions are not an alternative to force. They are often a prelude to it.

## V. Iran Today: Two Blockades, No Short Clock

This dynamic is now unfolding in real time.

The current U.S. blockade aims to impose economic costs on Iran by restricting oil exports and limiting access to critical goods. At the same time, Iran is applying counter-pressure through disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz, targeting global energy flows.

The prevailing narrative frames this as a race—two blockades, two clocks, waiting to see which side yields first.

That framing is wrong.

Both sides are operating under zero-sum security stakes. For Iran, the issue is regime survival and strategic autonomy. For the United States, it is regional influence and the credibility of its commitments. These are not negotiable margins; they are core interests.

In such conditions, economic pressure alone does not produce concession. It produces endurance.

A decisive shift would require something far more extreme: alignment of Iran’s key external partners against it, including active participation by Russia and China in enforcing the blockade with their own naval forces. That scenario would fundamentally alter Iran’s strategic environment.

It is also extraordinarily unlikely.

Absent that kind of alignment, the structure of the conflict remains unchanged. Economic pressure will impose costs, but it will not resolve the underlying dispute.

## VI. The Timeline Ahead: Persistent Disruption, Not Quick Resolution

If sanctions do not compel concession, the timeline of the conflict changes.

It becomes longer—and more volatile.

Rather than a rapid resolution driven by economic pressure, the more likely outcome is sustained disruption: intermittent supply shocks, continued maritime tensions, and periodic escalation. Markets may adapt at the margins, but the underlying instability persists as long as core interests remain in conflict.

At the same time, the absence of decisive results will increase pressure in Washington to escalate further—to seek through military means what economic tools cannot achieve.

This is the trajectory that sanctions historically produce.

## Conclusion: What the Framework Gets Wrong

The “two blockades, two clocks” framework assumes that time and pressure will force a decision.

The historical record suggests otherwise.

Economic sanctions rarely compel states to abandon core security objectives. When they appear to succeed, it is usually because military force—or the credible threat of it—has done the decisive work. When they fail, they do not end the conflict; they shift it to a more dangerous phase.

The current confrontation is not a short-term test of endurance. It is a structural conflict between actors with incompatible objectives.

That is why neither side is likely to “blink” in the coming weeks or months.

And it is why the expectation that economic pressure alone will resolve this crisis is not just optimistic.

It is empirically wrong.
