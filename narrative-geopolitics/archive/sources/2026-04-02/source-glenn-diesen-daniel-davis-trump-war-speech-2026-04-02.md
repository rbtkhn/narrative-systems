# Daniel Davis × Glenn Diesen — Trump war speech, Hormuz, NATO (operator transcript)

**Interview title (YouTube):** Daniel Davis: Trump's War Speech: Iran Escalation & Death of NATO  
**Recorded (YouTube description):** April 2, 2026  
**Aired / published (operator):** 2026-04-02  
**Watch:** [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KqD5LfmcCEE](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KqD5LfmcCEE)

**Speakers:**

- **Glenn Diesen** (host)
- **Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis** (guest) — *Daniel Davis Deep Dive* (YouTube)

**Glenn Diesen:** Welcome back. We are joined today by Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis, a four-time combat veteran and host of the Daniel Davis Deep Dive YouTube show. As always, I will leave a link in the description. Thank you very much for coming back on the program.

**Daniel Davis:** It’s always my pleasure to be here. Thank you very much for having me.

**Glenn Diesen:** Donald Trump just gave his address to the nation — essentially a war speech. We got mixed messages: on one hand, Iran has been defeated; on the other, if they don’t capitulate, he will bomb them back to the Stone Age. NATO doesn’t want to help, but we don’t need them. The Strait of Hormuz has to be opened, but it’s not our problem — other people should open it. It was all over the place, with a lot of uncertainty covered up by bluster. How did you make sense of this speech? It was announced in advance. Did you hear anything new?

**Daniel Davis:** I certainly didn’t hear anything new. The previous afternoon around 5 PM, he had already talked about the war being over in roughly two to three weeks and was kind of wrapping it down. When he announced the address to the nation a few hours later, people assumed he must have something new to say. But he really didn’t. He just reiterated the same two-to-three-week timeline, that we’re talking with them, but if they don’t do what they’re supposed to, we’ll basically walk away — at least from the Strait — and said it’s somebody else’s problem now. “We’ve done the hard work. Why don’t you guys get some courage?”

He repeated phrases from his Truth Social posts the day before. So nothing new. What we did hear was continued statements showing he remains disconnected from reality and doesn’t realize the desperately bad situation the United States has been plunged into by this war of choice.

The Strait is not going to open easily, and it cannot be opened by force of arms — not by us, not even if the entire NATO navy joined in. Sending warships through would just turn them into a shooting gallery, which is why no nation has done it and why our own Navy hasn’t. Nevertheless, the Strait remains closed. Oil prices continue to spike. Unlike previous comments where markets calmed, this time the stock market dropped and oil prices jumped immediately because people worldwide recognize that what he’s saying has no connection to reality.

The war is not winding down. After five weeks, the Iranian government is still firmly in place despite the assassinations of many leaders. Their missile capabilities remain intact, as they demonstrated again yesterday and today against Israel. Their navy — the parts that matter: speedboats, drone boats, and submarines — is largely untouched. What they hit on the surface barely matters. What matters is what’s hidden in the thousands of tunnels and underground facilities.

Estimates before the war put the number of those speedboats at up to 3,000. Their proxies — Kata’ib Hezbollah in Iraq, Lebanese Hezbollah, the Houthis — have all stepped up and joined the fight at varying levels. There’s nothing we can do about that.

When President Trump says he’s going to bomb them into the Stone Age, it exposes a lack of understanding of two major things. First, we don’t have enough ammunition to bomb a country four times the size of Iraq with 93 million people spread across difficult terrain. Even attempting it will only solidify and steel the resolve of the Iranian people.

Just an hour and a half ago, Trump posted on Truth Social bragging about destroying the biggest bridge in Tehran — a civilian bridge with no military value — and said much more is to follow. It’s time for Iran to make a deal before there’s nothing left. This is exactly what he threatened last night, and it looks like a war crime. I’ve spoken to Iranian expats who are strongly opposed to the regime, and even they call this a war crime.

If Trump thinks this will cow the Iranian people or accomplish anything militarily, the exact opposite is likely. It will firm up their control of the Strait of Hormuz, drive prices higher, tank markets further, and create problems worldwide.

**Glenn Diesen:** Trump seems to realize he’s backed into a corner. He said himself he’s running out of military targets. So the only strategy now appears to be inflicting massive pain on civilian infrastructure, hoping that forces Iran to make a deal. But when a country faces what it reasonably sees as an existential threat, it can’t simply accept a bad deal that makes the threat worse in the future. It seems that’s all he has left.

He keeps saying we’re close to ending the war in two or three more weeks, while also threatening massive escalation. Do you see this as him believing he can force a deal soon, or is there another plan?

**Daniel Davis:** You said he doesn’t have any more cards to play. There is one left: ground forces. The air campaign and navy haven’t worked. We’ve seen a significant escalation in the last week — A-10 ground attack jets, Apache helicopters, elements of the 82nd Airborne, Marine Expeditionary Units, Army special forces, Rangers — roughly 20,000 ground troops either already deployed, in place, or en route.

It looks like he may be preparing to play that card. But this is like sitting down at high-stakes poker on the opponent’s home turf with a weak hand (a two and a six) while the other side holds face cards. The Iranians have mobilized reserves and have volunteers who know the terrain intimately. They’ve openly said “bring it on.”

The chance of this succeeding tactically — let alone operationally or strategically — is almost zero. I could lay out in detail why even a tactical ground operation would be near-suicidal. But that seems to be where things are heading. If he doesn’t use those troops, then what? Just walk away? That’s what I would advise. Don’t sacrifice Americans in pointless deaths that will only make the situation worse. Trying and failing will hurt more than not trying.

**Glenn Diesen:** Sending in 10–20,000 soldiers — you can’t completely dismiss the possibility it might work, but the risks are enormous: supply lines, support, potential for heavy casualties or captures. It would be an unmitigated disaster, and swallowing such a defeat could lead to even more reckless escalation. It’s strange he’s willing to play this card when the outcome is so uncertain.

In the speech, Trump also listed people who disappointed him and blamed others, particularly the Europeans. He said NATO hasn’t done much for us. On one level, NATO wasn’t supposed to do this, and he didn’t even consult the Europeans. On another level, the US has provided security for Europe for decades, and now they’re closing airspace, denying bases, and Poland refused to send Patriots. I can see frustration on both sides. What does this mean for NATO? Is it dead, or just another nail in the coffin?

**Daniel Davis:** I might differ slightly here. Up until February 2022, I was a strong advocate for NATO winding itself down in an orderly way. There was no longer a need to keep expanding eastward. It was militarily irrational. At the end of the Cold War, 16 members had stood against the entire Warsaw Pact. After the Soviet collapse, expanding it could only be seen as antagonistic by Russia. It was poking a stick in their eye — unnecessary and provocative.

Once this mess with Russia started (which the US led), the Europeans joined enthusiastically. Now Trump wants them to treat NATO as his personal force: pay more, and send troops even into wars of choice they weren’t consulted on. To me, NATO is dead. It won’t survive this. Even if the Iran war ends miraculously soon, the damage is done. Trump has made clear he views NATO as an extension of American forces he can command at will. The Europeans will have to figure something else out. The pieces are in motion.

This will be catastrophic for Ukraine. They must be regretting not taking the Istanbul deal in 2022. They may now face outright military defeat, with almost nothing coming from the US and even Europeans holding back equipment for their own defense.

The biggest winner so far is Russia: flush with cash, some sanctions relieved, and Putin looking like the reasonable adult offering stability in the energy sector.

**Glenn Diesen:** I used to prefer gradual reform of NATO, but now I agree — it can’t be reformed. It has to go, like the Soviet Union.

I saw interesting comments from Lindsey Graham. He briefly suggested the war objectives could be met and we might wind it down — something I’ve rarely heard from him. Then after Trump’s speech, he was back to full cheerleading. Do you think panic is setting in because there’s no clear exit?

**Daniel Davis:** That brief moment of realism from Graham and even Netanyahu (who reportedly said Iran no longer poses an immediate threat) suggested some people were looking for an offramp. But it quickly reversed. Now it looks increasingly likely there will be some kind of ground operation or raid. Anyone in Iran who might favor negotiations seems to get killed. Trump is back on board with threats to bomb Iran back to the Stone Age while still demanding total surrender.

My bigger worry is that if this heads toward abject military failure, Trump or Netanyahu might consider a so-called “tactical” nuclear weapon. There’s no real difference — it’s still a nuclear detonation with fallout.

**Glenn Diesen:** The rhetoric — “bomb them back to the Stone Age” — is so over-the-top and vile. Putin comes off as reasonable by comparison, and the Iranian foreign minister sounds civilized, saying they have no problem with the American people, only the political leadership. It feels like the information war is being lost. On the nuclear issue, do you see this as Trump trying to escalate to force Iran to back down, knowing they have nuclear material and could potentially improvise a device?

**Daniel Davis:** It has to be part of Iran’s calculation. That’s why they keep the door open to negotiations, but on their terms. They’re willing to open the Strait (which isn’t fully blocked — they’re controlling passage) in exchange for security guarantees. It would require profound humility from the US, which we haven’t shown in decades.

I don’t think Trump is thinking several moves ahead like chess. He’s more action-reaction: throw something at the wall, see if it sticks. He still mentions Venezuela, which suggests he thinks this can become “Venezuela 2.0” with control over their oil. That’s a dangerous mindset, especially with the people around him.

**Glenn Diesen:** Last question — where do Israel’s interests lie right now? Do they want to end this soon or keep pushing to break Iran?

**Daniel Davis:** I see no evidence the Israeli leadership is wavering. They calculated the pain in advance and believe they can outlast Iran. A country of 93 million with deeply protected facilities versus tiny Israel in a war of attrition seems irrational to me, but they appear committed. They will keep pressuring Trump not to end it, saying the sacrifices would be for nothing. That’s why I think a ground operation remains likely.

**Glenn Diesen:** Sounds like a strategy based on hope — not a good one. Thank you for taking the time and sharing your insights.

**Daniel Davis:** Always my pleasure, Glenn. Thanks for having me on.
