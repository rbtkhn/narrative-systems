# Situation Report: Pakistan's Troops in Saudi Arabia

### The Quiet Deployment Changing the Iran War

Prof Robert Pape

Jun 30, 2026

Over the past 48 hours, attention has focused on the obvious developments.

U.S.–Iran talks in Doha failed to launch. Speaker Mike Johnson is floating a new 60-day congressional authorization for military operations. Commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains well below prewar levels, while the latest exchange of strikes has further undermined confidence in the memorandum of understanding.

All of that is important.

But it may not be the most important development.

Over the past weeks, Reuters and other regional and defense outlets have reported a substantial Pakistani military deployment to Saudi Arabia, including thousands of troops, fighter aircraft, drones, and advanced air-defense systems. Neither Riyadh nor Islamabad has publicly confirmed the full scope of the deployment. At the same time, neither government has denied it. Instead, both have continued to emphasize their long-standing strategic defense partnership, including military training, advisory missions, and mutual defense commitments.

Taken together with the consistency of independent reporting, the strategic implications are significant enough to warrant close examination. If these reports are substantially correct, they point to a major shift that has received remarkably little attention: the next phase of the Iran War may be shaped less by what Tehran or Washington decide than by what America's regional partners are willing—or unwilling—to support.

That has profound implications for the future of the war.

Below, I examine:

What the reporting actually says—and what remains uncertain.

Why the reported deployment coincided with Saudi Arabia's decision to restrict U.S. military access, and how that affected American operational options.

Why this development could increase Iran's strategic leverage without Iran firing another shot.

Why the current memorandum of understanding should not be compared with the JCPOA—and why treating them as the same kind of agreement risks misunderstanding where this conflict is heading.

Pakistan's Troops in Saudi Arabia: The Timeline That Changes the Story
Most reporting over the past week has focused on Doha, the latest exchange of strikes, and the apparent collapse of the memorandum of understanding. Yet a quieter sequence of reported events may prove far more important.

According to Reuters and other regional and defense reporting, the sequence unfolded as follows:

April 11. Pakistan reportedly deployed a substantial military contingent to Saudi Arabia under the long-standing Saudi–Pakistan Strategic Mutual Defense Agreement. The reported deployment included thousands of troops, fighter aircraft, drones, and advanced air-defense systems. Although neither Riyadh nor Islamabad publicly confirmed the full scope of the deployment, neither government denied it. Instead, both continued to emphasize their longstanding security partnership and mutual defense cooperation.

May 3. President Trump announced Project Freedom, a U.S.-led initiative intended to escort commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and restore confidence in Gulf maritime traffic.

Immediately afterward. Multiple reports indicate that Saudi Arabia, joined by Kuwait, declined to permit the United States to use their airspace and key military facilities—including Prince Sultan Air Base—in support of the operation. According to those reports, Riyadh's decision reflected concern that direct participation could draw the Kingdom into a broader regional war at precisely the moment it was seeking to preserve diplomatic channels and avoid Iranian retaliation.

Within roughly 36 hours. Project Freedom was reportedly shelved. Following subsequent discussions between President Trump and Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, routine U.S. military transit reportedly resumed, but Saudi Arabia is also reported to have maintained its refusal to support that specific operation from its territory or airspace.

Taken individually, each of these developments might appear manageable.

Taken together, they point to something much larger.

Allies have disagreed before.

The important question is whether America's regional partners are beginning to place operational limits on the military options they are willing to support.

If so, the strategic balance has shifted in a way that has received remarkably little attention.

For months, analysts have focused almost exclusively on whether the United States could coerce Iran.

The emerging question may be whether the conflict – and Iran's growing alliance network -- is now beginning to constrain Washington through its own partners.

That is a fundamentally different strategic problem—and one with implications that extend well beyond this week's fighting.

Why Airspace Matters More Than Airpower
For the United States, the critical issue is not simply access to another military base. It is access to operational geometry.

Modern air campaigns depend on secure basing, predictable flight corridors, aerial refueling, intelligence collection, and rapid sortie generation. Saudi Arabia sits at the center of that network. Restrictions on Saudi airspace or facilities do not make American air operations impossible, but they make them slower, more complex, and substantially more expensive to sustain.

Aircraft must fly longer routes, require additional tanker support, carry less ordnance, and spend less time over their targets. Fewer sorties can be generated in a given period, while operational flexibility declines precisely when commanders value it most. Every additional logistical hurdle reduces the credibility of threats to conduct sustained coercive operations.

Saudi Arabia is geographically huge and denial of its airspace matters more than any other single country for US air operations.

This is a lesson that extends well beyond the current crisis. In Bombing to Win, I argued that successful coercion depends not simply on possessing overwhelming military power but on translating that power into sustained political pressure. Geography—and allied cooperation—are essential parts of that translation.

If regional partners begin limiting how American military power can be employed, the balance of coercive leverage shifts even if the underlying military balance does not. The United States may still possess overwhelming combat capability, but its ability to apply that capability rapidly, repeatedly, and at acceptable political cost becomes increasingly constrained.

That is why allied access is not a logistical detail. It is a strategic variable.

How Iran Gains Leverage Without Firing Another Shot
The strategic significance of these reported developments is not that they make the United States incapable of using force. They change the bargaining environment in which force is used.

Throughout this conflict, Washington's strategy has rested on the assumption that additional military pressure could compel Iran to make greater political concessions. That assumption becomes more difficult to sustain if America's operational options become progressively narrower while time increasingly favors Tehran.

Every week that commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted places additional pressure on global energy markets. Oil inventories gradually decline, shipping costs remain elevated, insurance premiums stay high, and governments face growing political pressure to restore stability. Iran does not need to close the Strait completely to benefit from this dynamic. It needs only to preserve enough uncertainty to keep economic costs accumulating while avoiding actions that provoke a decisive military response.

If, at the same time, key regional partners become less willing to facilitate expanded U.S. military operations, Washington confronts a widening strategic dilemma. The coercive tools available become more costly to employ precisely as the economic costs of waiting continue to rise.

This is the essence of the Escalation Trap. Iran does not have to defeat the United States militarily. It has to convince Washington that the cost of preventing Iran's preferred political outcome is becoming greater than the cost of accepting it.

That is what I mean by Iran's period of maximum leverage. Military pressure can continue to increase even as America's bargaining position gradually weakens.

The Wrong Historical Analogy
Much of the commentary surrounding the memorandum of understanding assumes the relevant comparison is the nuclear negotiations that produced the JCPOA. That is the wrong framework.

The JCPOA was primarily an arms-control agreement designed to limit Iran's nuclear capabilities through negotiated constraints and verification. The current memorandum is something fundamentally different: an attempt to stabilize an unresolved balance of power after a major regional war. Those are not the same strategic problem.

When the underlying distribution of leverage remains contested, agreements are less likely to produce lasting stability than repeated bargaining backed by military and economic pressure. That is why ceasefires repeatedly give way to renewed crises and why temporary pauses are so often mistaken for durable peace.

The central question is no longer what the memorandum says.

The central question is whether it reflects a balance of power that all sides are ultimately prepared to accept.

That is the framework I will continue to use in The Escalation Trap—because it is the one most likely to identify the next escalation before it becomes tomorrow's headline.
