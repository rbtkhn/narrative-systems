# Getting the Framework Right: Why Iran Is Seeking Power, Not Just Money

## Escalation Trap This Week: Staying Ahead of the Risks that Matter

Prof Robert Pape

Jun 22, 2026

Last Monday, I argued that the greatest risks surrounding the new memorandum were uncertainty itself and Iran's growing leverage from collapsing oil inventories.

Events quickly validated both. The talks stalled, Iran walked out after they resumed, talks briefly continued, and President Trump himself acknowledged that inventories had become central to the crisis.

Many assume that the negotiations are fundamentally about money and economic concessions. The Trump administration clearly thinks so.

However, if the current framework is wrong, then many of the expectations surrounding the negotiations may also be wrong.

This week goes deeper.

What if Iran is pursuing something larger than economic relief?

What if the negotiations themselves are helping accelerate Iran's rise?

And if so, what does history tell us about how rising powers use negotiations after wars to lock in their gains?

Most importantly, if the prevailing framework is wrong, what does the correct framework imply about the risks facing the United States, Israel, and the global economy over the next thirty to sixty days?

Because getting the framework right is often the difference between understanding events—and being surprised by them.

When the Battlefield Shifts to Negotiations
Most discussion of the current negotiations begins with the assumption that Iran wants money.

President Trump and Vice President Vance have repeatedly framed the negotiations in terms of sanctions relief, reconstruction funds, and economic incentives. Under that framework, the central question becomes straightforward:

How much economic relief is necessary to keep Hormuz open?

In effect, this framework treats diplomacy like a business deal. Both sides ultimately want economic gain, and the central challenge is simply finding the right price. Direct negotiations facilitate compromise. Conflict merely gets in the way. Lebanon is a sideshow. Want Iran's enriched uranium, buy it.

But this may be the wrong framework.

Thinking about negotiations primarily through inducements is the mirror image of thinking about bombing primarily through punishment. Both assume that coercive leverage comes mainly from how the target calculates immediate costs. No doubt, money matters. But states have higher priorities.

History suggests that high-stakes international politics work differently.

The reason is power.

At the highest levels of international politics, states rarely think primarily about how to divide an economic pie. They think about the balance of power—the relative position each side will occupy not only today, but for years into the future.

This distinction becomes especially important after wars. Postwar negotiations are rarely about settling past damage. They are efforts to shape future security.

History repeatedly shows that postwar negotiations are rarely about repairing damage. They are efforts to shape future balance of power. States that emerge stronger seek to convert temporary military advantages into lasting political influence. States that emerge weaker seek to preserve enough strength to avoid permanent subordination.

The Treaty of Versailles after World War I was not fundamentally about reparations. It was an effort to prevent Germany from again dominating Europe. Like many postwar settlements, it attempted to lock in a new balance of power. The current negotiations should be understood in the same way.

The MOU Recognizes Iran's Rising Position
Viewed through a power framework, the structure of the memorandum becomes easier to understand.

Many observers focus on the future negotiations over Iran's nuclear program and the promise of roughly $300 billion in eventual economic normalization. Under an inducement framework, those long-term economic benefits appear to be the center of the agreement.

The provisions that take effect immediately, however, directly reinforce the rising power of Iran, whether the long-term ones occur or not.

The memorandum commits the United States to preserving Lebanon's "territorial integrity" (art 1), which effectively places Washington in the position of containing Israeli operations against Hezbollah. It requires the US to "immediately" issue "waivers" to sanctions on Iran that would block oil-related investment in the country (art 10). It permits renewed oil sales and access to frozen assets "upon implementation of the MOU" (art 11). And Iran promises "no charge for 60 days only" for shipping via Hormuz, after which "arrangements" will be set between Iran and Oman (art 5). Finally, the US promises to respect the "sovereignty" of Iran and to refrain from "interfering" in its internal affairs (art 2), far different from a "rogue" or "terrorist" state.

Each provision strengthens Iran's power. None weakens it.

Taken together, these measures provide Iran with three things every rising power seeks.

· A secure strategic base: Iran + Hormuz.

· Greater financial resources: oil sales, unfrozen funds, Gulf investments.

· And a recognized sphere of influence: an arc from Lebanon to Gulf.

The significance of these provisions becomes clearer when compared to the American position only months earlier.

In April, Washington rejected Iranian proposals remarkably similar to many of the provisions now contained in the memorandum.

The contrast is striking.

In April – before the US Blockade -- Iran proposed a ceasefire framework built around ending military operations, removing sanctions, releasing frozen assets, recognizing enrichment rights, establishing future arrangements for Hormuz, and codifying the settlement through international institutions. Washington rejected those proposals because American leaders believed military pressure would eventually force Tehran into concessions.

At that time, American leaders believed military pressure would force Tehran into concessions.

The blockade produced the opposite result. Iran survived. Iran controlled Hormuz. And many of the same principles that Washington dismissed in April reappeared in the June memorandum.

The United States now accepts discussions over enrichment, commits itself to sanctions relief, agrees to release frozen assets, promises large-scale reconstruction assistance, recognizes future negotiations over Hormuz administration, and ultimately supports codifying the agreement through the United Nations.

The significance is not that every Iranian demand was granted.

The significance is that the direction of movement was overwhelmingly toward Tehran's original position rather than Washington's.

Wars shift bargaining power. Negotiations reveal who gained it.

This is why the memorandum represents something larger than a simple ceasefire or strategic defeat for the United States.

It acknowledges that the regional balance of power is shifting toward Iran -- and that Washington is willing to accommodate that change. From the perspective of a state seeking regional primacy, these are extraordinary gains achieved through diplomacy rather than war.

And Day 60 is coming, when oil inventories fall to operational limits, the MOU allows Iran to charge Hormuz fees, Iran's period of maximum leverage begins, and Trump's maximum political vulnerability approaching the mid-terms will be undeniable.

In sum, the memorandum is a roadmap to Iranian regional hegemony in the most economically important region of the world – with all the tensions such a transformation inevitably creates. And a roadmap to more impossible dilemmas for Trump.

This is precisely why the stability of the agreement itself is fragile now and more fragile with the passage of time.

The Three Tripwires
If the memorandum is understood through a balance-of-power framework, then the next question becomes straightforward:

What red lines would convince Iran that the agreement is no longer serving its interests?

The answer matters because the future of Hormuz ultimately depends on Iran's willingness not to use force.

Three red lines stand out.

Red line #1: Israel Israeli Military Operations in Southern Lebanon, not just Beirut

Article 1 commits the United States to preserving Lebanon's territorial integrity and ending "military operations on all fronts, including Lebanon." From Tehran's perspective, this provision is not about managing the level of violence. It is about restoring Lebanese sovereignty.

Iran views continued Israeli military operations in southern Lebanon as a direct challenge to the sphere of influence it has spent decades constructing through Hezbollah and its broader network of regional partners. From Tehran's perspective, accepting continued Israeli military freedom in southern Lebanon would amount to accepting a postwar order that leaves Hezbollah permanently exposed and undermines the regional position Iran emerged from the war seeking to consolidate.

Red line #2: US Delay in Releasing Iran's Assets

Article 11 promises access to frozen assets and immediate economic relief "upon implementation of the MOU." If billions fail to arrive quickly—within say, 10 days after Thursday's signing -- Tehran may conclude that Washington is again using delay as leverage. A power framework predicts that Iran would interpret this not as a technical dispute, but as an attempt to weaken its newly improved position.

Red line #3: US Interfering in Iran-Oman Negotiations on Hormuz Fees

Article 5 leaves future arrangements over transit fees to Iran and Oman. Any effort by Washington to pressure Oman or undermine those negotiations would strike directly at what Tehran increasingly regards as its most important strategic gain.

Friday offered a reminder that Iran controls the redlines. According to the Wall Street Journal, "After an uptick in the number of tankers sailing through the strait in both directions Thursday, traffic dwindled Friday …. ships attempting to pass through the waterway said they were turned back by the Iranian navy."

The reality now confronting the world is uncomfortable but unmistakable:

Hormuz remains open because Iran permits it to remain open.

The Ultimate Red Line: Ending Enrichment
Article 8 reveals perhaps the deepest contradiction inside the memorandum. The text commits Iran not to develop nuclear weapons, but it deliberately postpones the most important question: enrichment itself. Iran agreed to "discuss the issue of enrichment" but makes no hint of a commitment to end it. President Masoud Pezeshkian reinforced that position over the weekend, declaring that Iran would never abandon enrichment and insisting enrichment is a sovereign right, even while reaffirming that the country does not seek nuclear weapons.

From Tehran's perspective, enrichment is no longer simply a technical issue. It has become a symbol of sovereignty and great-power status. Accepting the US position of zero enrichment after surviving the war would transform military endurance into political surrender. For Washington and Israel, however, accepting continued enrichment risks acknowledging Iran as a threshold nuclear state with growing regional influence. Pezeshkian's speech reportedly triggered Trump's new outburst of threats.

This is why enrichment represents the ultimate red line. Unlike disputes over sanctions, frozen assets, or Lebanon, the enrichment question goes directly to how both sides define victory itself. If either side attempts to impose its preferred outcome, the negotiations could collapse regardless of progress elsewhere. In that sense, Article 8 does not resolve the nuclear issue. It postpones the most dangerous argument until later.

Why the Trap Continues
If the memorandum is strengthening Iran's position rather than limiting it, then the forces driving escalation have not disappeared. They have merely shifted from the battlefield to diplomacy.

From Tehran's perspective, negotiations offer the opportunity to convert wartime survival into regional influence. From Israel's perspective, those same negotiations risk legitimizing the emergence of an Iranian sphere of influence stretching from Lebanon to the Gulf. Washington increasingly finds itself caught between the two.

This is the strategic problem. Stability ultimately requires all three actors to accept the same balance of power. But Iran seeks greater influence, Israel seeks to prevent it and continue its own rise, and the United States seeks stability without conceding Iranian primacy.

Those objectives are not yet aligned.

That is why the future of the memorandum – and the peace -- remains uncertain. Ceasefires do not create peace when the underlying balance of power remains contested.

The central question is whether the emerging balance of power is one the principal actors themselves are willing to accept.

And that is why the Iran War has entered the middle game of the Escalation Trap—not the endgame.
