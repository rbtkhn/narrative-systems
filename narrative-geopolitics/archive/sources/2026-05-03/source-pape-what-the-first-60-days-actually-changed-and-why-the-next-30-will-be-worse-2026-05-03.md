# What the First 60 Days Actually Changed-and Why the Next 30 Will Be Worse

The war is no longer about defeating Iran. It is about the United States trying to prevent a global system from moving beyond its control-and failing

Prof Robert Pape
May 03, 2026

Sixty days into the war, none of Washington's core objectives-regime change, nuclear rollback, military degradation, proxy collapse, or restored deterrence-has been achieved, as oil spikes and allies begin acting independently.

That failure is no longer contained. It is now driving decisions the United States cannot shape, only react to.

Iran's regime is more aggressive than before the war, while the country retains its core nuclear assets, its missile and drone forces remain active - and dangerous to the point that US naval ships remain 200 miles from Hormuz to avoid their targeting range - and its regional influence-from Iraq to Lebanon-has not collapsed. In important cases, it has hardened under pressure.

At the same time, credible reporting with photographic evidence indicates that Iranian strikes on U.S. bases caused much more damage than initially acknowledged, including rendering significant infrastructure unusable and degrading operational capacity beyond anything Saddam Hussein's military achieved against US bases during the 2003 US invasion of Iraq.

This is not a stalemate.

It is a failure to translate military pressure into strategic outcomes.

And when major powers fail at the outset of a war, this does not stabilize the situation.

They often pursue more escalation.

## Iran Is Turning Survival Into Power

Iran is no longer fighting simply to endure the war.

It is using the war to shape the strategic environment that follows it.

Control over the Strait of Hormuz-through selective military attacks, continued risk of ongoing disruption, and selective bargaining with Gulf and other states-has become Iran's central lever. Roughly one-fifth of globally traded oil - and much more -- moves through that corridor. The US blockade has only added to Iran's global leverage. This "partial" oil disruption drove gas prices sharply higher in America by 30 cents a gallon in a matter of days.

This shift matters because it changes the structure of global bargaining power. Iran is well on its way to becoming the 4th center of world power, and a year or so from now could buttress its rising economic leverage with working nuclear weapons.

At the same time, Iran is expanding its diplomatic influence with Pakistan, Russia and Oman - often operating with timelines and channels that exclude Washington. In Pakistan, Iranian officials recently left talks before American negotiators could even arrive, signaling not desperation but control and growing confidence over the diplomatic pace of great power politics.

Economically, Iran continues to exert pressure on the Strait of Hormuz, reducing Saudi Arabia, UAE and other energy exporting Gulf states' gross domestic product 20-30 percent and exerting powerful damage on the world's economy. Even just two months' loss of shipping through Hormuz has already driven prices from around $95 per barrel to $110, and then to $117 within hours.

This is not symbolic resistance.

Iran is practicing great power politics at the highest levels - and its growing power is felt from the Gulf to Asia, Australia, Europe, the United States, and in virtually every corner of the world's economy.

US gas prices are one important indicator, rising from approx. $2.80 a gallon pre-war to nearly $4.40 today - and Americans must now accept that $5/gallon is a plausible near-reality.

Bottom line: Iran is moving from surviving the war to shaping the global political and economic system that emerges from it - all to the advantage of Iran, Russia, China and the growing list of countries moving diplomatically in Iran's direction such as Pakistan and Oman.

## The Geopolitical Price of Failure: U.S. Authority Is Being Publicly Ignored

In previous crises -- and even the US war against Iraq in 2003 -- U.S. policy preferences structured the behavior of allies and adversaries alike.

That is no longer reliably true.

Requests from Washington for allied military support have gone unanswered or been explicitly rejected. European governments have instead restricted access, limited cooperation, or pursued independent positions. The leaders of Canada, Britain, and France have made numerous public statements directly questioning America's reliability.

This week Germany's leader Merz publicly said the United States was being "humiliated" by Iran. This called harsh public attention to the hard truth: America is losing the war militarily to Iran, a second ranked power - and has no apparent strategy to reverse the outcome.

Far from granting U.S. requests to participate in the Iran war, Germany's leader declared "this is not our war," and NATO itself declined formal involvement-while Washington responded by withdrawing 5,000 troops from Germany.

Loss of US authority is nowhere more evident than with Iran.

U.S. calls for direct negotiations with Iran have failed to produce compliance. Tehran is choosing when, where, and with whom it engages-and increasingly, that excludes Washington or appears to merely string it along.

Iran is not buckling, quiet quitting, or nervously hedging.

Iran is downgrading US power and showing the world that America is no longer the indispensable great power that commands authority, respect - or fear.

When major powers ignore your demands in real time, influence falls rapidly.

## The Global Economy Is Entering the Second Phase

The first phase of the war raised price.

Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz became slower and more expensive, with war-risk insurance premiums rising sharply. Energy flows were rerouted, inventories tightened, and alternative supply chains were pushed to their limits and still proved insufficient to offset disruption.

This week, oil rose from the mid-$90s to over $115 in days. This reflects more than panic. It reflects that the second phase -- actual shortages and initial economic contractions - has begun in earnest.

The news is full of evidence for this. Air travel - always a leading indicator of energy shortages - is now feeling real pain. Skyrocketing jet fuel prices and actual shortages have led to major cancellation of tens of thousands of flights across Europe, Canada, and the United States as well as yesterday's announced corporate death of Spirit airlines, and loss of over 17,000 jobs.

Historically, this is where lagged effects of energy shortages begin. Physical disruption comes first. Economic contraction follows weeks later.

If current conditions persist, late May and June are likely to bring a more visible combination of inflation pressure and slowing output across major economies.

That is the moment when war moves from markets into changes in daily life. That combination is politically as well as economically destabilizing.

And it is now baked into the trajectory of the war and will be the subject of future articles on our Substack.

## Asia Is the Next Arena of Consequence

The effects of the Iran war are not confined to the Gulf, Europe, or world economy.

They are beginning to shape expectations in Asia, where President Trump has a planned meeting with China's President Xi, already once postponed due to the war, on May 14 in China.

As U.S. military resources and attention are drawn into the Iran conflict, America's ability to project power credibly in Asia is weakening. The longer the Iran war continues without clear U.S. success, the more leverage shifts toward China-not only on trade, but on broader regional questions, including US security commitments.

And this places Trump at a decided disadvantage in discussions with Xi. Should the meeting occur, China's leaders may well expect serious US concessions on future US assistance to Taiwan and other US allies in Asia - with profound consequences for the security architecture in Asia and the US role in Asia in the coming year.

Accordingly, just as the Iran war is effectively leading to the disintegration of NATO, so too could similar consequences occur in the near term in Asia.

The bottom line: It is difficult to overstate what it means for U.S. alliances to fracture simultaneously across the Gulf, Europe, and Asia. When the Soviet Union lost the Cold War, it did not just lose a contest-it watched global power reorganize without it. The United States now risks a similar outcome in the Iran war: not simply defeat, but a declining ability to shape the system that follows.

## Another Desperate US Gamble?

Washington now confronts the classic escalation trap.

Regime-change bombing, strikes on military targets, and the blockade have not reduced Iran's nuclear stockpile or missile operations. Diplomatic channels-from Oman to Pakistan to Russia-are operating on terms Tehran controls. U.S. requests for allied military support have been rebuffed, leaving unilateral escalation as the primary remaining instrument. Preparations for expanded strikes on infrastructure and discussion of securing nuclear sites point in that direction.

But escalation at this stage comes with reduced surprise, higher regional risk, and uncertain operational payoff. When coercion fails, great powers rarely step back.

They double down-often at worse odds.

## Why Would Trump Take the Gamble?

Because great powers rarely accept total loss when high-risk options remain.

The classic case is Japan's decision to attack the United States at Pearl Harbor. As political scientists-most notably Bruce Russett-have shown using captured Japanese government records, Tokyo's leaders understood the enormous risks. They knew the United States could retaliate with overwhelming force.

They attacked anyway.

Why? Because U.S. oil sanctions were strangling Japan's war effort. If trends continued, Japan faced near-certain strategic defeat and likely regime collapse. Faced with that trajectory, leaders chose the least bad option: a high-risk gamble that might break U.S. resolve rather than accept certain failure.

That logic is not unique to Japan.

It is how states behave when they see the future closing against them.

Trump now faces a structurally similar choice. Escalation risks deeper failure. But the costs of inaction-strategic defeat, political damage, visible loss of control-are already mounting and may be irreversible. Under those conditions, a high-risk escalation can become the least bad option-especially when many of the immediate costs fall on others: Gulf states, allies, and the global economy.

That is the trap.

To be sure, escalation is not locked in. Trump could muddle through and continue the blockade - but this means the world's economy continues its precipitous fall and the world's major countries continue to ignore Washington's precipitously falling authority. Trump could also march US forces out of the region - which would allow Iran to open Hormuz selectively and on its own terms, and still US authority would continue falling.

These are Trump's real choices - all bad - and why failure creates pressure to escalate.

Escalation under unfavorable conditions risks further failure - but once that cycle begins, it is extraordinarily difficult to break.

## The Next 30 Days

The next 30 days will not reset this trajectory. They will clarify it.

Three indicators will matter most:

Whether energy flows through Hormuz stabilize or tighten further

Whether allied coordination strengthens or continues to fragment

Whether U.S. military deployments and logistics to the Gulf expand

These are not abstract signals - they are decision points.

Paid subscribers will receive real-time analysis as these thresholds are approached and crossed.

Because the core issue is no longer what has happened.

It is what happens next-and whether the United States can still shape it.

The first 60 days set the direction.

The next 30 will determine whether that direction becomes outcome.
