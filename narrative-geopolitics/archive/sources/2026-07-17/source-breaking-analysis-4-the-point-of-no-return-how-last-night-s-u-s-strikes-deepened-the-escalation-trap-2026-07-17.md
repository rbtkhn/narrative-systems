Prof Robert Pape

Breaking Analysis is a new Escalation Trap series providing immediate assessments of major developments-not simply explaining what happened, but identifying the strategic logic that points to what is likely to happen next.

July 17 BLUF
Last evening, U.S. Central Command conducted multiple waves of airstrikes against Iran, targeting key bridges, railway junctures, and power lines connecting key cities, particularly in the Hormozgan province and around the major port city of Bandar Abbas. These strikes significantly raise the risks vertical and horizontal escalation that would intensify and widen the Iran war by attacks against infrastructure targets in Gulf states with US bases and further encouraging the closure of the Bab el-Mandeb oil gateway. Risks are accelerating while global energy inventories continue running down.

Why this deepens the Escalation Trap
The US strikes are classic "shaping" operations in preparation for a ground assault, consistent with the air strikes over all six days in the last week. Last night's strikes disrupt the logistics and mobility of Iranian military forces in the south, while the previous strikes degrade coastal missile storage and launch sites, air defense systems, and surveillance facilities from Chabahar to Bushehr - along hundreds of miles of coastline that complicates the defender's problems in stopping any landing or seizure of specific islands.

This latest round of strikes also deepens the US Escalation Trap.

Once the United States begins shaping the battlefield for possible ground operations, stopping becomes politically harder than continuing. Having already accepted the risks of sustained escalation, Washington faces growing pressure to demonstrate that those costs will produce tangible strategic gains. Every additional strike therefore raises the political price of restraint while making a larger military commitment appear increasingly necessary to justify what has already been done.

The strikes also deepen Iran's own Escalation Trap.

Having survived six days of U.S. attacks while still contesting Hormuz, Tehran now faces mounting pressure to demonstrate that American escalation cannot succeed. Standing down after these strikes would risk signaling weakness both domestically and across the region. As a result, the political incentives for Iranian leaders increasingly favor further escalation, even as the military and economic costs continue mounting.

Expect multi-faceted escalation in the near future.
