# Within 10 Days, Shortages Are Already Here

Not just higher prices—now the global economy is running into physical constraint, and most analysis is still behind

Ten days ago, I argued that shortages—not just price increases—would begin to hit the global economy. That call is now being confirmed in real time. What matters is what comes next: not just higher prices, but the accelerating absence of critical goods—and the first irreversible steps toward economic contraction.

The shift is no longer theoretical. It is underway, and it is arriving faster than most major institutions predicted.

At the time, that claim ran ahead of the consensus view from financial markets and international organizations. Today, the first indicators are visible across multiple regions. Jet fuel shortages are emerging in parts of Europe. South Korea has reported disruptions in plastics production tied to constrained petrochemical inputs. Across several economies, early supply constraints are appearing—not as isolated anomalies, but as the first stage of a broader systemic shift.

This is not a typical energy shock. It is the early phase of a blockade dynamic that follows a historically consistent pattern—one that unfolds in stages and becomes progressively more difficult to reverse.

The critical mistake now is to continue interpreting events through the lens of prices.

What we’re seeing now is the moment every blockade reaches—when price stops mattering and access becomes the constraint.

That transition marks the dividing line between a market disruption and a system shock.

## The Logic of Blockade: Why This Was Predictable

For three decades, I have studied economic coercion—sanctions, blockades, and the use of economic pressure to achieve strategic goals. The central finding is consistent across cases: economic pressure rarely compels major concessions on its own, but it reliably produces a specific internal dynamic within the targeted system.

That dynamic unfolds in stages.

The first stage is price shock. Supply is disrupted, prices rise sharply, and states draw on reserves and inventories to cushion the impact. This is where most analysis remains focused, because it is the most visible and familiar phase.

The second stage is physical shortage. Inventories begin to deplete, replacement flows are insufficient, and critical inputs fail to arrive. At this point, the system no longer adjusts through prices alone. Production itself begins to contract.

The third stage is economic and political reorganization. Governments intervene directly—rationing fuel, prioritizing sectors, and determining which parts of the economy continue to function and which do not.

We are now entering the second stage. Prices are still rising—but now shortages are emerging alongside them, and their effects accumulate over time.

## Iraq: Severe Economic Pain Without Surrender

The historical record makes clear why this matters.

After the 1991 Gulf War, Iraq was subjected to one of the most comprehensive sanctions regimes in modern history—effectively an oil blockade. Over the course of the 1990s, Iraq’s GDP declined by roughly 40 to 50 percent. Oil exports—the backbone of its economy—were sharply curtailed. Civilian infrastructure degraded, and shortages of basic goods became widespread.

By any economic measure, the pressure was severe.

It did not produce the intended political outcome.

Saddam Hussein’s regime did not collapse. It did not concede its core strategic position. And despite years of economic isolation, the United States and its allies were never confident that Iraq had fully abandoned its weapons programs.

The result was not coercive success. It was escalation—to the 2003 ground invasion.

The mechanism is straightforward but often misunderstood. As the economy contracts, the population becomes more dependent on the regime for access to scarce resources. Opposition groups, cut off from those resources, weaken first. The regime consolidates control over a shrinking pie.

Bottom line: when core issues of power and security are at stake, states absorb extraordinary economic costs rather than concede.

## Iran: The Limits of Repeated Economic Pressure

The same pattern is visible in the case now unfolding.

Iran has been under varying levels of sanctions for decades, with particularly severe restrictions reimposed during the 2018 “maximum pressure” campaign. Those measures sharply reduced oil exports—from roughly 2.5 million barrels per day in 2017 to below 500,000 at points in 2019—and contributed to a GDP contraction of approximately 6–8 percent annually during the peak pressure years.

The economic impact was real.

It did not produce decisive strategic change.

Iran did not abandon uranium enrichment. It did not dismantle its regional network. It did not cease development of missiles or drones. Instead, it adapted—shifting trade patterns, expanding informal networks, and sustaining core capabilities.

Sanctions imposed costs. They did not compel surrender.

The current U.S. naval blockade operates on the same logic. It will likely reduce Iran’s oil exports at the margin and further constrain imports of key goods and technologies. This will impose additional economic pressure on an already strained economy.

But the political effect will not be capitulation.

It will be consolidation.

As in Iraq during the 1990s, shrinking the overall economy strengthens the regime’s relative control over society. Scarcity increases dependence. Access to fuel, food, and basic goods becomes more tightly mediated by the state. Political loyalty is rewarded; opposition is marginalized. The regime does not weaken as the economy contracts—it becomes harder.

Making Iran poorer will not halt its nuclear trajectory. It will not stop enrichment. It will not dismantle its strategy for expanding regional influence. It will not open Hormuz and allow the Gulf countries to avoid vast declines of their gross domestic product by 20-30%. It will not save Asia, Europe, and the US from vast economic paid. And it will not prevent the regime from allocating remaining resources to sustain its core power base and project influence outward.

Economic pressure changes distribution inside the state. It does not change the state’s fundamental priorities -- and the fact that the regime is in charge.

That is the structural limit.

And it is why blockades and sanctions, on their own without direct military pressure, rarely produce the outcomes their architects expect.

## U.S. Domestic Politics and the Escalation Pathway

This dynamic does not unfold in isolation from domestic politics.

Recent polling shows declining public support for the current U.S. approach, reflecting how sensitive advanced economies are to sustained economic disruption. As costs accumulate—through higher prices, shortages, and slowing growth—political pressure intensifies.

A recent AP poll places presidential approval near 33 percent, underscoring the growing domestic strain.

This creates a reinforcing escalation dynamic.

Economic pressure is applied to compel concessions. When those concessions do not materialize, the choice narrows: accept the adversary’s growing position, or intensify pressure.

Each step up the escalation ladder is taken in search of a decisive effect that remains elusive. Each failure increases the pressure for the next move.

This is not new. It is the same logic that drove escalation in Vietnam.

And it is now taking shape again.

## What Comes Next: From Shortage to Contraction

The progression from shortage to contraction follows a historically consistent timeline.

The first 30 to 45 days were dominated by price increases and early constraints. That phase is now behind us.

Between 45 and 60 days, shortages become visible. Production disruptions begin to emerge across sectors dependent on continuous inputs.

That is where we are now.

Airlines are already cutting routes to conserve fuel, while several Asian economies—including the Philippines and Sri Lanka—have introduced reduced workweeks and energy-saving measures. India has drawn down fuel reserves while facing localized supply gaps. These are early indicators of system-wide constraint.

Between 60 and 90 days, the effects broaden into measurable economic contraction. Industrial output declines, supply chains fragment, and governments intervene more aggressively to allocate scarce resources.

Beyond 90 days, the system enters a deeper adjustment phase. During the 1973 oil crisis, by early 1974, U.S. inflation exceeded 10 percent, gasoline shortages led to rationing measures and station closures, and industrial output fell measurably as energy-intensive sectors curtailed production.

Today’s system is more interconnected and more tightly coupled.

Which means the adjustment could be more severe.

## The Structural Reality

The underlying dynamic is not difficult to identify.

States value power and security more than wealth.

When forced to choose between economic cost and strategic position, they absorb the cost. That is why economic pressure alone rarely produces decisive outcomes on issues of core importance.

It does, however, produce escalation. Because the absence of results does not reduce pressure. It increases it.

## Conclusion: Ahead of the Curve

Ten days ago, this community was ahead of where broader analysis is only now beginning to converge.

That gap remains.

What matters next is not whether shortages will occur—they already are. It is how those shortages evolve into economic contraction, and how that contraction feeds back into political and strategic decisions.

That is the phase most analysis still does not capture.

And it is the phase that will determine how this crisis unfolds over the next 30 to 60 days.
