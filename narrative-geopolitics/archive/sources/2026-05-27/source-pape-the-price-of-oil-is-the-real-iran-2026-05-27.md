# The Price of Oil Is the Real Iran Deal

Why the most important negotiation now may have nothing to do with uranium

Prof Robert Pape
May 27, 2026

If oil remains above $90 even after Hormuz reopens, then the central strategic outcome of the Iran war may already be decided.

The real issue is no longer whether ships can physically pass through the Strait. It is whether markets now believe the Gulf has become permanently dangerous.

And if they do, then Iran may have discovered something far more powerful than closing Hormuz entirely:

That controlled instability can reshape the global balance of power without winning a conventional war.

For weeks, negotiations over Iran have revolved around uranium enrichment levels, inspections, missile constraints, and the precise meaning of "freedom of navigation" through Hormuz.

But beneath all the diplomatic language sits a much simpler strategic question:

Will the price of oil return to where it was before the war?

The answer directly impacts everyone reading this.

Before the February escalation, Brent crude hovered close to the mid-$60s range. Today, despite partial de-escalation signals and renewed diplomacy, prices continue holding substantially higher - near $100 -- amid persistent fears of renewed disruption across the Gulf. Major shipping insurers still price significant war-risk premiums into Gulf transit, while tanker-routing costs and charter rates remain elevated relative to prewar baselines. Energy traders increasingly assume the region has entered a structurally more unstable phase rather than a temporary crisis.

That distinction matters enormously because modern geopolitical power increasingly turns not simply on military control, but on the ability to shape the long-term price of energy.

## The Strait Is No Longer the Point

The critical strategic reality is that Iran no longer needs to close Hormuz completely to achieve leverage.

That may be the single most important lesson emerging from the war.

A total shutdown of the Strait would invite overwhelming retaliation and risk isolating Iran diplomatically, including from China. But persistent instability is different. Limited attacks, periodic disruptions, shipping uncertainty, drone incidents, insurance volatility, and the constant possibility of escalation may already be enough to sustain a large geopolitical risk premium in global energy markets.

That creates a fundamentally new strategic environment.

Iran may have discovered that controlled instability is more profitable than total closure.

And from Tehran's perspective, even a sustained period of oil prices remaining 25-40 percent above prewar levels for one to two years could solve multiple strategic problems simultaneously. Higher prices generate sharply larger revenues for the regime, strengthen its regional leverage, increase pressure on Western economies already burdened by debt and inflation, and weaken the political cohesion of U.S. alliances during a critical transition period in global power politics.

The irony is profound. The war intended to weaken Iran may instead have increased the strategic value of Iran's core geopolitical position.

## The OPEC Precedent

There is also a major historical reason to take this possibility seriously.

The 1973 Arab oil embargo officially ended in March 1974. But the era of cheap energy did not return when the embargo ended. Before the crisis, oil traded near $3 per barrel. After the embargo, prices stabilized not back at pre-crisis levels, but closer to $11-14 as OPEC maintained production discipline and markets embedded a permanently higher geopolitical risk premium into global energy pricing.

And instead of normalizing, prices rose again after the 1979 Iranian Revolution and the Iran-Iraq War, climbing above $35 per barrel by 1981. Oil did not truly collapse back toward pre-1973 levels until the supply glut and OPEC breakdown of 1986.

The strategic lesson was not simply that oil prices could spike during crises.

It was that geopolitical leverage over energy flows could redistribute global economic and political power long after the shooting stopped.

Critically, this happened not because Arab states physically blocked oil for twelve straight years. They did not. What changed was the psychology of global markets. Traders, insurers, governments, and corporations no longer believed Middle Eastern energy could be treated as politically secure in the same way again. Once that psychological shift occurred, a much higher pricing structure became self-reinforcing.

That is why the current negotiations matter so much.

Even if Hormuz technically reopens, markets may already believe the Gulf has entered a structurally more dangerous era.

## The New Oil Weapon

The most important question now is how Iran might sustain elevated prices without provoking an overwhelming military response.

There are several plausible mechanisms.

Iran could periodically harass shipping under the justification of "security inspections" or retaliation for sanctions violations. It could impose selective transit fees on certain categories of commercial traffic moving near Iranian-controlled waters. It could tolerate deniable proxy attacks against energy infrastructure while formally denying responsibility. It could periodically suspend maritime cooperation during moments of political tension, forcing insurance markets to repeatedly reprice Gulf transit risk upward.

Most importantly, Tehran could frame all of this domestically and internationally as compensation for war damage inflicted during the American and Israeli bombing campaigns.

That political framing matters.

The leadership would not present higher oil prices as aggression. It would present them as reconstruction financing, sovereign compensation, and economic recovery after attacks on Iranian territory.

And markets would respond not to legal arguments, but to risk.

Even intermittent instability may prove sufficient. Tanker markets operate on expectations as much as actual disruption. If insurers believe even a small percentage of voyages face elevated danger, premiums rise across the entire shipping network. That alone can sustain materially higher global energy prices without any formal blockade ever occurring.

Iran also would have a powerful deterrent to discourage US military escalation in response to a policy of maintaining higher energy prices through intermittent instability.

In response to Iran intermittently and indirectly disrupting shipping through Hormuz, the United States could certainly inflict additional punishment on Iran through air and naval strikes. But Tehran can threaten a qualitatively different escalation: a full closure of Hormuz that could send oil prices sharply higher still and trigger a far larger global economic shock.

Washington may possess escalation dominance militarily, but Iran increasingly possesses economic escalation leverage -- and that matters enormously.

Large financial institutions understand this dynamic well. Oil markets remain unusually sensitive to persistent uncertainty because global spare production capacity remains relatively tight while inventories in many consuming economies are not dramatically above long-term averages. Even modest disruptions can generate outsized price movements when shipping confidence deteriorates simultaneously.

The same logic now applies to Hormuz.

Yes, oil markets are ultimately elastic. Sustained high prices eventually generate new production, conservation, fuel substitution, and technological adaptation. But, large-scale supply expansion from offshore drilling, LNG infrastructure, pipeline systems, refining capacity, and new shale investment cycles typically require years rather than months. Even the American shale revolution took several years after the 2008 price spike before production growth materially changed global pricing dynamics.

That delay matters enormously in geopolitical terms. Iran does not need permanently high prices to generate major strategic effects. It only needs a sustained period of elevated prices long enough to deepen inflation pressures, strain Western alliance politics, weaken investment confidence, and widen the relative economic advantages of states better positioned to absorb energy volatility.

## America's Inflation Trap

A sustained period of controlled instability lasting twelve to twenty-four months would not merely raise gasoline prices in the United States.

It would strike directly at some of the weakest structural points in the American economy simultaneously.

The United States remains deeply dependent on diesel-intensive freight systems, aviation fuel markets, petrochemical production, globally integrated manufacturing supply chains, and energy-sensitive transportation networks. Even though America produces large quantities of domestic oil, prices inside the United States still move largely with global benchmark pricing. That was demonstrated clearly after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, when U.S. gasoline prices surged despite record American production.

Even a prolonged period in which oil prices remain 30 percent above prewar baselines could materially intensify inflation pressures just as the Federal Reserve continues struggling with elevated interest rates, rising federal debt-service costs, slowing commercial real estate markets, and weaker consumer demand. Trucking, airlines, agriculture, shipping, plastics, fertilizer production, and industrial manufacturing would all face renewed cost pressures simultaneously.

And politically, sustained energy inflation historically produces broad public exhaustion with incumbent political leaders.

That is one reason the strategic implications could become so profound.

The real vulnerability may not be military at all.

It may be the growing inability of Western democracies to absorb prolonged economic strain without deepening internal political fragmentation.

## China's Window

This does not mean China would tolerate permanent instability indefinitely.

Beijing does not want uncontrolled Gulf chaos, $90 oil forever, or a prolonged collapse of global trade flows.

But China may not need permanent instability to benefit strategically.

A sustained period of elevated energy prices lasting even twelve to twenty-four months could significantly weaken Western economies, deepen inflation pressures inside democracies, strain alliance cohesion, worsen debt burdens, and accelerate broader shifts in global industrial and technological power already underway.

Great powers rarely need permanent advantage.

They need advantage during decisive transition windows.

China is also far better positioned for a temporary energy shock than many observers assume. Over the past decade, Beijing has quietly expanded one of the world's largest strategic petroleum reserve systems while simultaneously locking in long-term supply relationships with Russia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and multiple Central Asian producers. Chinese refiners and state planners therefore possess substantially greater capacity than most Western economies to smooth short-term energy volatility across industrial sectors.

Tehran could, of course, offer Beijing price concessions, but these may not be required for China to benefit strategically. Even a moderate but sustained period of elevated global prices could disproportionately weaken Western economies already struggling with debt, inflation fatigue, political polarization, and slower industrial growth. From Beijing's perspective, that may be strategically sufficient to accelerate relative gains to lock-in China's current trajectory as the rising power in a coming era of American decline.

That is a coherent geopolitical tradeoff. And it fits China's historical strategic behavior remarkably well: patient, opportunistic, transitional, and focused on long-term relative gains rather than immediate confrontation.

## Tactical Victory, Strategic Defeat

This is why even "successful" negotiations may ultimately prove far less favorable to Washington than many assume.

Even if a diplomatic agreement stabilizes military escalation temporarily, the deeper economic structure created by the war may remain intact.

Markets already understand that Iran possesses the ability to threaten one of the world's central energy arteries repeatedly and unpredictably. That understanding alone changes long-term pricing behavior.

And once markets begin to see structural instability deeply embedded into energy pricing, the negative consequences will spread fast -- and beyond the Gulf itself.

Higher energy prices raise industrial costs across Europe and Asia, intensify inflation pressures inside the United States, strain transportation and food systems globally, and complicate efforts to restore long-term economic stability.

In effect, the Iran war may have shifted the geopolitical balance without requiring Iran to win militarily in any conventional sense.

That is the real Escalation Trap.

The United States may have succeeded tactically while helping create a global economic environment that increasingly favors its long-term rivals.

## The Real Negotiation Should Focus on Prices

That is why the most important unanswered question in the negotiations is also the simplest:

Does Iran intend to help return oil prices toward prewar levels?

Because if the answer is no, then the strategic outcome of the war may already be visible.

Not in uranium stockpiles.

Not in bombing damage assessments.

But in the repricing of global power itself during a critical geopolitical transition period over the next 12 to 24 months.

Which means the real strategic challenge for Washington is no longer simply preventing Iran from building a nuclear weapon.

It is preventing a prolonged rise in the cost of energy - not an abrupt new shock so much as a sustained plateau at substantially higher prices than before the Iran war -- capable of weakening Western economies and alliance systems during one of the most consequential shifts in global power since the Cold War.

That requires US-Iran negotiations explicitly tied to restoring energy prices toward prewar levels - similar to how OPEC has traditionally operated - including understandings that, depending on circumstances, this may mean suspension of shipping fees and acceptance of higher levels of export from some Gulf states to offset lower levels in others due to actions by non-state actors and other future disruptions.

Without that broader framework explicitly tied to prewar energy prices, the negotiations risk producing a deeply misleading form of stability.

Hormuz may reopen and shipping may resume. But the world economy could still remain trapped inside a significantly more expensive and strategically unstable energy system shaped by the war's aftermath.

And if that happens, historians may ultimately conclude that the most important consequence of the Iran war was not military at all.

It was the beginning of the end of the post-Cold War assumption that the United States could reliably keep the world's energy arteries open at acceptable long-term cost.
