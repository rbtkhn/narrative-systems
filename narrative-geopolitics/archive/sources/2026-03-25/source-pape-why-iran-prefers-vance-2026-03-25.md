# Why Iran Prefers Vance

### Weak states exploit divisions inside strong states to win on the political battlefield

### Prof Robert Pape

Why would Iran prefer JD Vance over figures like Jared Kushner or Steve Witkoff in negotiations?

At first glance, the answer appears personal or tactical—style, tone, or trust. But that interpretation misses the deeper logic. States in conflict do not choose interlocutors randomly; they choose them strategically, based on how those choices shape the broader balance of power. What looks like a personnel decision is often a political move.

Iran's preference is not about diplomacy alone. It is about politics inside the United States.

## Weak States Do Not Confront Strength Directly

The United States is vastly more powerful than Iran by any conventional measure. It possesses superior military capabilities, global reach, and alliance networks. In a direct contest of force, Iran cannot win.

That reality dictates strategy.

When weaker states face stronger ones, they rarely confront them symmetrically. Instead, they look for indirect ways to offset power—raising costs, stretching timelines, and, most importantly, shaping the political environment in which the stronger state operates. Military weakness pushes strategy into the political domain, where outcomes are often determined long before the battlefield decides them.

Power asymmetry → indirect strategy → political warfare

## The Political Battlefield Matters More Than the Military One

Wars between unequal powers are rarely decided solely by firepower. They are decided by whether the stronger state can sustain the political will to continue.

This is where internal divisions matter.

A unified state can absorb costs and escalate when necessary. A divided one becomes constrained, uncertain, and slower to act. For a weaker adversary, exploiting those divisions is not opportunistic—it is essential. Political cohesion is the foundation of sustained power, and once it weakens, military superiority becomes harder to translate into outcomes.

The most effective way to weaken a stronger state is not to defeat its army. It is to divide its politics.

## Why Vance

This is the context in which Iran's preference for Vance should be understood.

Vance is not simply another negotiator. He represents a distinct position within the American political spectrum—one more skeptical of military escalation and more cautious about prolonged conflict. Choosing him over figures associated with prior negotiations and harder-line approaches is not incidental; it reflects a deliberate assessment of where leverage lies.

**It is selection.**

Iran is not choosing a person. It is choosing a position inside the American system.

## Exploiting Divisions Inside the United States

This choice maps onto real and widening divisions within the United States.

On one side is a faction that views the conflict with Iran as necessary, strategically justified, and aligned with broader regional objectives. On the other is a faction that sees the war as costly, misaligned with core U.S. interests, and potentially driven by external priorities rather than national ones. These differences are no longer abstract—they are visible in public debate and elite disagreement.

Figures associated with restraint—including Vance and commentators like Tucker Carlson—have raised concerns about escalation. Others within the administration and policy community argue for a more forceful approach. What matters is not who is right, but that the divide exists—and is growing.

These are not marginal disagreements. They are competing visions of how American power should be used.

## From Division to Leverage

Iran's strategy is to turn these differences into leverage.

By elevating one faction over another—by signaling a willingness to negotiate through channels associated with restraint—it increases the political weight of that faction inside the policy process. It creates pressure to pursue talks, avoid escalation, and reconsider objectives. Over time, that pressure can shape decisions at the highest level.

This is not about persuasion.

It is about structure.

Select interlocutor → legitimize faction → increase internal pressure → constrain escalation

## The Israel Dimension

These divisions do not exist only within the United States. They intersect with differences between the United States and Israel over the conduct and purpose of the war.

Within the United States, a growing segment of opinion sees the conflict with Iran as serving Israeli strategic interests more directly than American ones. That perception has been reinforced by public debate and internal dissent, including the resignation of figures like Joe Kent, who raised concerns about the direction of policy after engaging with Vance. Whether one agrees with that view or not, its presence matters.

The debate is no longer just about how to fight the war. It is about whose interests the war serves.

When internal divisions align with external tensions, political cohesion becomes harder to sustain—and easier for adversaries to exploit.

## The Strategic Logic

None of this requires Iran to win militarily.

It requires only that it prevent the United States from acting cohesively and decisively over time. By shaping the political environment—amplifying divisions, selecting favorable interlocutors, and reinforcing competing narratives—it can reduce the effectiveness of superior power without ever matching it directly. This is how weaker states level the playing field.

This is not new. During the five years of the 1968 to 1973 Paris Peace Talks, North Vietnam prolonged negotiations, signaled reasonableness, and aligned messaging with anti-war sentiment—helping fracture U.S. political cohesion and ultimately constraining Washington's ability to sustain the war. The talks did not change the outcome – US ignominious strategic defeat in Vietnam.

Weak states survive not by defeating strong states, but by outlasting their unity.

## The Strategic Signal

Iran's preference for Vance is not a diplomatic curiosity. It is a strategic signal.

It reflects an understanding that the outcome of this conflict will not be determined solely by military capability, but by political cohesion. The key question is not whether the United States can escalate further. It is whether it can do so with a unified purpose.

Because in wars like this, the decisive battlefield is not only in the region. It is inside the stronger state itself.
