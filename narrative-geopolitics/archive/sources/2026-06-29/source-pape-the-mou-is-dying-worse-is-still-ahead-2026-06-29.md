# The MOU Is Dying — Worse Is Still Ahead

### The Escalation Trap This Week – Staying Ahead of the Risks

Prof Robert Pape

Jun 29, 2026

Less than two weeks after it was signed, the U.S.–Iran Memorandum of Understanding is beginning to collapse.

That should not surprise anyone.

The agreement stopped the shooting for a while.

It never resolved the incentives for war.

Over the past week, Iran attacked commercial shipping exactly where I argued it would. The United States responded with new military strikes. Israel continues pressing for broader military action. Commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains far below normal.

The question is what happens next.

The Escalation Puzzle
The ceasefire is collapsing. Here's why.

The biggest mistake this week is believing the latest attacks are isolated violations of a fragile agreement.

They are not.

They are the predictable result of a deeper strategic problem.

The Iran War fundamentally changed the balance of power in the Middle East. Iran survived with enough military capability to deny American control of the Strait of Hormuz. The United States accepted an interim memorandum that stopped large-scale fighting but never resolved who would control the Gulf after the war. Israel, meanwhile, never accepted the emerging settlement and continues pressing for military action in Lebanon despite the broader framework.

That strategic disagreement has now become kinetic.

On Thursday, Iran struck the Singapore-flagged cargo vessel Ever Lovely with one-way attack drones near the Strait of Hormuz, forcing the International Maritime Organization to suspend evacuation operations for commercial shipping. On Friday, the United States retaliated by striking Iranian radar, missile, drone, and surveillance sites supporting operations around Hormuz. On Saturday, Iran attacked another commercial vessel and launched drones toward U.S.-linked facilities in Bahrain. Hours later, U.S. aircraft again struck Iranian military targets after Washington concluded Tehran had violated the ceasefire, prompting an ongoing surge of naval firepower and logistical support in and around the Strait of Hormuz.

These are not random escalations.

They reflect competing interpretations of the memorandum itself.

Washington and several Gulf partners have encouraged commercial shipping to use routes that reduce Iran's practical control over Hormuz, while a separate U.S.-brokered framework would allow Israeli forces to remain in parts of southern Lebanon far longer than Tehran appears willing to tolerate. Iran is responding not simply with words, but with military actions designed to force acceptance of its interpretation of the postwar order.

Iran's strategic logic is becoming increasingly clear:

Keep Hormuz operating under persistent pressure.

Force commercial traffic to decline.

Increase pressure on American energy inventories and global markets.

Build leverage before final negotiations.

Force Washington toward Iran's interpretation of the memorandum.

Ultimately raise pressure for reduced long-term U.S. military presence in the Gulf.

That is why the ceasefire is failing.

Not because diplomacy broke down.

Because the agreement never settled the central strategic question:

Who controls the new Middle East?

This Week's Strategic Shift:
Iran Demonstrating It Is Not Deterred
For months, analysts have measured this conflict tactically, by counting airstrikes, missiles, and bomb damage. That is the wrong metric to understand the future of the war.

The more important question is whether military punishment is changing Iran's strategic behavior.

This week's answer is increasingly clear: it is not.

Since the war began, Washington has repeatedly tried to convince Tehran that the costs of continued resistance would become unbearable. Each major escalation was expected to restore deterrence. Instead, Iran has responded by finding new ways to preserve—or even expand—its leverage.

The pattern is impossible to ignore.

US leadership decapitation, culminating in the death of Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, did not produce strategic capitulation. Instead, Iran tightened its grip over Hormuz, demonstrating that command continuity mattered more than any individual leader.

Weeks of sustained U.S. and Israeli bombing did not compel Tehran to retreat. Rather than stepping back, Iran increased pressure on the Gulf states, signaling that regional economic stability would remain hostage to the conflict as long as Iran's own security concerns went unmet.

The subsequent U.S.-led maritime blockade was likewise intended to force concessions by tightening economic pressure. Instead, after nearly two months, negotiations produced a memorandum of understanding that left Iran with many of its core strategic objectives intact while easing immediate pressure on the regime. Whatever tactical compromises Tehran accepted, the broader message was unmistakable: sustained coercion had not broken Iranian resolve.

Taken together, these developments validate the central prediction of The Escalation Trap: when an adversary's core political objectives remain intact, escalating military punishment is more likely to produce strategic adaptation than strategic capitulation—a conclusion consistent with more than three decades of my research on coercion and the limits of bombing and economic blockades.

Now the same mechanism is appearing again.

The memorandum itself is proving less durable than many hoped because the underlying balance of leverage continues to shift. As global and American oil inventories continue to run down, the global economic risks of prolonged disruption in the Gulf rise steadily. Every additional week of constrained energy flows increases pressure on consuming countries while improving Iran's bargaining position. On June 19, US Strategic Petroleum Reserve fell to 331 million barrels, the lowest since 1983. Tehran does not need to win on the battlefield. It needs only to convince adversaries that time is working in Iran's favor.

This is the strategic mechanism that matters.

Deterrence fails when punishment does not change the adversary's political objectives or strategic behavior. By continuing to escalate pressure despite repeated military strikes, economic coercion, and presidential threats, Iran is demonstrating that it still believes the path to a more favorable outcome lies in continued resistance rather than concessions.

That—not today's bombing—is the most important strategic development of the week. It suggests the conflict is entering a phase in which each new round of military pressure yields progressively smaller political returns while strengthening Tehran's confidence that it can outlast its opponents. That is precisely the dynamic that makes renewed escalation increasingly likely.

President Trump is left with remarkably few short-term options. Accepting an Iran that effectively dictates the terms of shipping through the Strait of Hormuz—and collects transit fees from the world's energy trade—is politically untenable at home. Yet there is no new coercive instrument ready to replace air power. That makes renewed bombing more likely not because it promises success, but because it is the only instrument Washington can employ quickly while avoiding the appearance of backing down.

As I argued two weeks ago, this means the conflict is likely to become more dangerous before it becomes more stable.

Iran is now entering its period of maximum leverage. As U.S. and global oil inventories continue to decline, every week of sustained disruption increases the economic and political pressure on Washington while strengthening Tehran's bargaining position. That is the strategic reality now driving events—and the reason the next phase of this conflict is likely to be defined by escalation rather than resolution.

The Pape Test
Every week brings a new barrage of headlines: airstrikes, drone attacks, diplomatic statements, ceasefire violations, and threats of retaliation. Most are treated as isolated events.

That is a mistake.

The ceasefire did not resolve the underlying balance of power. It left every major actor dissatisfied. Iran survived and now wants more power. The United States stopped short of imposing decisive military defeat. Israel continues to seek a more favorable strategic outcome. That means the MOU is not a stable settlement—it is a continuing contest over who shapes the postwar order.

To separate genuine strategic change from daily political theater, I will apply the same three questions every Monday.

1. Who Controls Hormuz?

Not who patrols it. Not who sails through it.

Who can determine whether global energy flows normally or under constant threat?

Today, the answer is Iran. Tehran has repeatedly demonstrated that it retains the ability to disrupt commercial shipping, raise insurance costs, and force governments and markets to react. As long as Iran possesses that leverage, it remains central to every major strategic calculation in the region.

2. Who Sets the Red Lines?

Red lines belong to the actor whose threats consistently change the behavior of others.

This week, Tehran again demonstrated that it intends to enforce its own limits on military operations, commercial shipping, and implementation of the memorandum of understanding regarding Israeli military action in Lebanon and the disposition of its unfrozen assets. Washington and its regional partners continue to react to those boundaries rather than simply imposing their own. That is a significant shift from the assumptions that prevailed before the war.

3. Who Controls Escalation?

This is the most important question.

The actor controlling escalation is not necessarily the one with the largest military. It is the one that can impose disproportionate costs with limited actions while forcing its adversaries to choose between accepting surrender or escalating further.

This week, that remains Iran. Relatively inexpensive attacks and continued pressure on maritime traffic continue to shape decisions in Washington, Jerusalem, Gulf capitals, and global energy markets. The United States and Israel retain overwhelming military superiority, but Iran continues to influence the pace and direction of the conflict at comparatively low cost.

That is why the strategic balance has shifted. The headlines may change every day. The Pape Test reveals whether the underlying balance of leverage is changing—or remaining exactly where it was.

What I Expect Next
Over the next several weeks, I expect three developments to shape the next phase of the Iran War.

1. Another pause—but not another ceasefire. The current burst of violence is likely to give way to a lull lasting roughly four to seven days, perhaps a little longer if U.S.-Iran talks resume. That should not be mistaken for de-escalation. Wars like this rarely move in a straight line. They advance through recurring cycles of burst, pause, and renewed burst as each side reassesses, bargains, and tests the other's resolve. The pauses are part of the escalation process—not evidence that it has ended.

2. Lebanon becomes the next pressure point. I expect the next significant increase in violence to come through Lebanon, as Hezbollah seeks to demonstrate that it has not been strategically deterred by Israel's recent agreement with the Lebanese government. Whether through drones, rockets, or other limited attacks matters less than the broader signal: the postwar balance remains contested across multiple fronts.

3. Shipping continues to yo-yo—but at a much lower level than before the war. Commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz will likely recover unevenly, while inbound crude tankers remain well below prewar levels. They have the least commercial incentive to enter the Gulf and the greatest exposure if shipping is suddenly disrupted.

These forecasts all follow from the same mechanism. Iran is entering its period of maximum leverage. Even if violence temporarily subsides, strategic pressure will continue to build as energy inventories decline and the economic costs of prolonged uncertainty steadily increase.

And that is why the Iran War remains in the middle game of the Escalation Trap—not the endgame.

These are forecasts for the next several weeks, not the next several days. Next Monday, we'll see whether events are moving in the predicted direction—and adjust the scorecard as the evidence accumulates.
