# Jeffrey Sachs: U.S. Attacks Venezuela & Kidnaps President Maduro

Welcome back. We are joined by Professor Jeffrey Sachs to discuss the US attack on Venezuela and the kidnapping of its president, Maduro. So in a way, Trump seems to be continuing where he left off in his first term with Venezuela. Why is this happening? What does Trump have against Venezuela?

I don't know the answer. This is a hard one. I don't think there's much on it. I really don't. I think the US has a long history of arrogance, political manipulations, incompetence in Latin America. It dates back basically 200 years to the Monroe Doctrine, which viewed Latin America as the US backyard, and the US could do with Latin America what it wanted. It has, over the years, overthrown dozens of governments, installed puppet governments, tried to manipulate countries. When that doesn't work, then sanctioned those countries, blockaded them, fomented coups. The whole history is sordid. But I don't think any of it is because Venezuela really matters. I think it is a mishmash of arrogance, inertia, power, and the lingering imperial mindset.

Now of course Venezuela has oil, and the US certainly has been interested in Venezuelan oil for a century. But the US does not need Venezuelan oil, nor would Venezuelan oil change any significant dynamic in the US economy at this point. So I don't think that's really the issue either. I think part of it has been politics in south Florida, and that is to say electoral politics within the United States, because there has been a strong anti-Castro, anti-Chavista, anti-left constituency in south Florida, and US politicians have pandered to that for decades. But even that is too small an explanation. So my answer is that this is the continuation of a long imperial habit combined with some domestic political incentives and a lack of any serious strategic thinking.

Because the language is very strong. They speak of narco-terrorism, they speak of kidnapping Americans, they speak of all kinds of criminal activity. But when you look at what the US is actually doing around the world, this looks more like projection than analysis. How do you view the legal aspect of this?

From the standpoint of international law, it is lawless. It is straightforward lawlessness. The United States is not the world's policeman, despite what many people in Washington seem to believe. It cannot seize another country's head of state. It cannot decide who is legitimate and who is not and then impose sanctions, blockades, and even extraterritorial measures on the basis of its own unilateral determinations. This is completely outside the UN Charter framework.

If the United States had genuine legal claims, they would go through proper international institutions. They would go through the Security Council, international courts, or other recognized legal channels. Instead, what the US does is issue indictments in its own courts, weaponize the dollar system, threaten third parties, and then claim that all of this is somehow law enforcement. It is not law enforcement. It is coercion. It is power politics dressed up in legal language.

And that matters because when the world's most powerful country acts this way, it erodes the international system for everyone else. It says openly that the rule of law applies to others but not to the hegemon. And then Washington wonders why so many countries want alternatives, whether in payments systems, reserve assets, or geopolitical alignments. This is exactly why.

You mentioned the Monroe Doctrine. A lot of people in Europe perhaps don't understand just how deeply embedded this is in American strategic culture. Could you explain why Latin America has so often been treated as if it were not fully sovereign?

Yes. The Monroe Doctrine was basically an assertion by the United States that the Western Hemisphere was under its special purview. Over time, especially as the United States became more powerful, that transformed into the idea that Latin America was not really a collection of sovereign states in the full sense, but rather an area in which Washington had deciding rights. Of course that is not how international law works, but it is how the imperial mentality worked.

So throughout the twentieth century and into the twenty-first, the US repeatedly intervened in Latin America through coups, invasions, covert operations, debt diplomacy, and sanctions. It supported dictatorships when convenient. It undermined elected governments when they pursued policies Washington did not like. And in every case there was some justification offered at the time: anti-communism, anti-drugs, democracy promotion, anti-corruption, anti-terrorism. The labels changed. The underlying mentality did not.

Venezuela is one more chapter in that long story. What is striking is how uncreative it is. It is the same repertoire of pressure and demonization that Washington has used again and again, even though it has usually produced suffering, instability, migration, and resentment rather than any constructive outcome.

What do you think this says about Trump's foreign policy in broader terms? Because he often presents himself as anti-war or against the foreign policy establishment, yet here we see continuity.

Trump is not anti-imperial. He may be less doctrinaire in some settings than parts of the US foreign policy establishment, and he may have instincts against certain long wars, but he is not anti-imperial. He believes in coercion. He believes in domination. He believes that the United States should be able to dictate terms. So in some places he says, let's not spend so much on this war. In other places he says, let's bully more directly.

That is not a coherent peace strategy. It is simply a different style of power politics. And because he is erratic, because he personalizes policy so much, because he likes spectacle, it can become even more dangerous. Not necessarily because every move is bigger in military terms, but because it is less bounded by process, law, or diplomacy.

So yes, there is continuity. Different rhetoric, some different methods, but the same deep assumption that the United States can decide outcomes far from its own borders with little regard for international legality or the actual welfare of the populations affected.

And for Venezuela itself, what are the consequences of this kind of policy?

The consequences are devastating. Sanctions are not abstract. They are not clean instruments. They disrupt imports, health systems, access to medicines, infrastructure maintenance, financial transactions, and ordinary life. They intensify hardship for millions of people. They also tend to harden political positions internally because when a country is under siege, politics becomes securitized.

So the United States claims to be helping democracy, but in practice it often weakens the social and economic conditions under which normal democratic life could even function. This is why so many sanctions policies are self-defeating even on their own declared terms. They are cruel, they are illegal in many respects, and they do not produce the political outcomes their advocates promise.

At the same time, they send a wider message to the world: if you are not aligned with Washington, your economy can be targeted, your leaders can be criminalized, your international transactions can be blocked, and your sovereignty can be treated as conditional. That is why the rest of the world increasingly sees the need for a more multipolar order and for institutional alternatives to US dominance.

So if one steps back, is this best understood as a Venezuela story, a US domestic politics story, or a story about the crumbling of the unipolar system?

It is all three, but most fundamentally it is a story about the long decline of the unipolar illusion. The United States still has enormous power. It can still do great damage. But it is not able to command the world as it once imagined. And because it cannot command as before, it often resorts to increasingly blunt and lawless forms of coercion.

That does not restore legitimacy. It further undermines it. And it pushes more and more countries to seek distance from the United States in strategic, financial, and diplomatic terms. So from Washington's point of view this may feel like strength, but in the longer run it is a symptom of weakening authority and weakening legitimacy.

And for the countries on the receiving end, including Venezuela, the task is survival, diversification, and building relations outside the US orbit to the extent possible. That is one of the great geopolitical facts of our time.

Professor Sachs, thank you as always.

Thank you very much.
