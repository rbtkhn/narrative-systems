# The Gambler's Conceit in War

### Why Trump Started the Iran War—and Why He Keeps Doubling Down

### Prof Robert Pape

## The Winning Streak That Becomes a Trap

It starts the same way it always does. A gambler sits at a table after a run of wins—nothing dramatic, just enough to feel in control. The bets get larger. The risks feel manageable. Then the streak breaks. A loss. Then another. Instead of pulling back, the gambler leans in. One more hand will restore the pattern. One more bet will recover the losses.

But the streak does not return.

The bets grow. The losses compound. And by the time the gambler recognizes what is happening, the winnings are gone—and the house is next.

This is not just a story about casinos. It is a pattern of behavior. And it is the clearest way to understand the most urgent question in the Iran war: how a U.S. president who prides himself on dealmaking chose a path of escalating risk.

The answer is not confusion. It is not impulsiveness.

It is something more dangerous.

This isn't a strategy. It's a losing streak disguised as control.

## Success, Not Failure, Drives Escalation

Most analysis still looks for the wrong cause—miscalculation, ideology, or pressure from allies. But those explanations miss the mechanism that actually drives escalation in powerful states.

Leaders do not take extreme risks only because they are losing.

They take them because they believe they are winning.

The early phase of this war reinforced that belief. U.S. and Israeli strikes hit key targets. Iranian retaliation, while serious, appeared containable. The tempo of operations suggested escalation could be managed. At multiple points, Donald Trump emphasized these successes—highlighting precision, control, and limits.

This is the critical moment.

Success does not reduce risk. It increases it—because it creates the expectation that success will continue.

And when reality begins to diverge from that expectation, leaders don't pull back.

They double down.

The U.S. isn't escalating by accident—it's escalating because it thinks it's winning.

## The Gambler's Conceit: Why Winning Makes Losing More Likely

Behavioral economists have long documented a pattern known as the "house money" effect, most closely associated with Richard Thaler at the University of Chicago. After gains, individuals take greater risks, treating prior winnings as a cushion.

More recent research shows something even more dangerous: when outcomes begin to turn, people escalate their bets to recover losses, even when the odds have worsened. Losses trigger risk-seeking behavior, not caution.

This is the gambler's conceit: the belief that success reflects control—and that control can be restored by increasing the bet.

In markets, this leads to ruin.

In war, it leads to escalation. The mechanism is simple:

Perceived success → illusion of control → expanded risk tolerance → doubling down when control slips

What looks like confidence is often something else entirely. A refusal to recognize that the game has changed.

## When Leaders Gamble With Other People's Lives

In a casino, the gambler risks his own money.

In war, leaders risk something else: Other people's lives.

This difference is central to why escalation becomes so dangerous. Political leaders do not experience the battlefield directly. The costs are distributed outward—to soldiers, civilians, and entire economies.

This creates a powerful distortion.

Losses feel abstract. Gains feel immediate. Confidence compounds faster than caution.

Each successful strike reinforces belief in the next. Each contained escalation suggests further escalation will remain contained. And when setbacks emerge, the instinct is not to reassess—but to act more forcefully.

- What begins as precision becomes hyper-confidence.
- What begins as disruption becomes control.
- What begins as a limited operation becomes a sequence.
- And sequences are harder to stop than decisions.

## The Historical Warning: Success Before Catastrophe

History shows how this pattern unfolds—and how it ends.

In the 1930s, Imperial Japan achieved rapid military successes across the mainland and more in East Asia. Each operation reinforced the belief that decisive force could reshape the strategic environment at acceptable cost.

Then came constraint. U.S. economic pressure, culminating in an oil embargo, threatened Japan's position. Faced with a narrowing window, leaders escalated.

The strike—Attack on Pearl Harbor—was tactically effective. It was also strategically catastrophic, bringing the United States fully into the war and eliminating any path to victory.

The key is not simply that the decision was wrong. It is why it felt right.

Earlier successes made the gamble seem reasonable.

This pattern is not unique—Germany before World War I, the United States in Vietnam, the post-9/11 wars.

Winning makes escalation feel safe—until it isn't.

## Why Trump Keeps Doubling Down

The present trajectory fits this pattern with unsettling precision. Early successes created a perception of control. That perception now shapes each decision, making escalation appear justified and manageable.

At the same time, the limits of air power are becoming clearer. Infrastructure can be damaged but not secured. Military assets can be degraded but not eliminated. Iran is adapting—shifting toward economic disruption and regional pressure.

This is the inflection point.

It is where expectations begin to break—and where the pressure to double down becomes strongest.

Trump isn't managing this war. He's chasing it.

Ground operations do not appear as a new risk. They appear as the necessary step to restore control. This is how wars expand.

## The Endgame: From Strategic Bet to Systemic Loss

The greatest danger is not one bad choice. It is the structure of choices that follows.

Each step narrows options. Each escalation raises stakes. Each commitment makes reversal more difficult. What begins as a limited operation becomes a trap.

In gambling, the endpoint is personal bankruptcy.

In war, the stakes are far higher.

The potential losses are systemic: disruption of the global economy through sustained energy shocks, long-term instability across the Gulf, and erosion of U.S. strategic position as costs mount. Disruption evolving into damage – and permanent harm to people.

The next phase will not be decided by what leaders say—but by whether ground forces begin to battle for physical control of geographic space – this is Stage 3 of the Escalation Trap. And the indicators for that shift are now visible.

By the time the illusion breaks, the losses are no longer recoverable – and measured in the growing instability of the global economy and declining security of Americans.
