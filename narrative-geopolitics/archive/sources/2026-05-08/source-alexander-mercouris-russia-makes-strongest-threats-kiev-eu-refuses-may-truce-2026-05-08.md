### Show Open - Russia Ceasefire Ukraine

# Russia Makes Strongest Threats, Tells EU Diplomats to Leave Kiev; EU Refuses; Kiev Violates May Truce

**Cleaned Transcript (Condensed Ingest Note)**

**Core sequence**
- Mercouris frames the date as 8 May 2026, the day before Russia’s Victory Day celebrations on 9 May.
- He says Putin’s Victory Day ceasefire is already in effect, running 8–10 May, but only as a brief holiday truce.
- He argues the ceasefire is being undercut by Ukrainian drone strikes on Russia and by Kiev’s public messaging.
- He stresses that the real flashpoint is the 9 May Victory Day parade in Red Square, where foreign diplomats and leaders are expected to be present.

**Threats and escalation risk**
- Zelensky’s ceasefire proposal is described as deliberately mismatched to the Russian one, effectively rejecting it.
- Mercouris says Zelensky has been hinting at threats toward parade attendees without explicit wording.
- He recalls earlier Zelensky remarks and drone activity near Putin-linked targets as evidence that threats should not be dismissed.
- The Russian Defense Ministry’s warning is described as unprecedented and severe: if Red Square is attacked, Russia may strike central Kiev, likely with Oreshnik missiles.
- The Russian Foreign Ministry backs that warning and tells civilians and diplomats to leave Kiev if needed.

**Mercouris’s judgment**
- He says he thinks Ukraine will probably not strike Red Square, but he treats the risk as real and unusually dangerous.
- He argues the West is trying to manufacture a crisis narrative around Russia, portraying Putin as isolated, weak, and paranoid.
- He says that narrative is threadbare and disconnected from realities on the front and in Russian domestic politics.

**Russia’s internal position**
- Mercouris says economic indicators and front-line reports point the other way: Russia’s position is still strong, not fragile.
- He cites rebound in Russian business activity, higher oil revenues, and continued public support.
- He says most Russians follow the war closely and understand it better than Western publics.
- He treats attacks on Putin’s reputation and leadership as a Western information campaign rather than a reflection of Russian reality.

### Segment 2 - Iran Russia Europe

**Negotiations**
- He says the diplomacy track is effectively dead.
- He points to Yuri Ushakov’s remarks as meaning: the Americans already know the terms from Anchorage, the Ukrainians know what they need to do, and there is no reason for further talks unless Kiev takes a first step.
- He interprets this as a Russian closing of the negotiation door while still leaving wording deliberately polite.

**Ukraine battlefield picture**
- He says the Ukrainian army is short of trained manpower, armor, shells, artillery, rockets, and logistics depth.
- He says drones remain the Ukrainians’ main remaining asset, but they are fragile and increasingly limited.
- He says drone warfare has been over-relied upon and cannot by itself sustain the front.
- He expects the front to deteriorate further, eventually with Donbass falling first, then possible Russian pressure toward Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, Odessa, Kharkov, and eventually Kiev.

**Drone and air-defense discussion**
- He says Russia and allies are adapting to the drone era and that the era of drone dominance is nearing its end.
- He mentions reports of Russian air-defense and drone-tracking improvements that will likely change battlefield conditions by summer.
- He also discusses Baltic-state complicity in allowing Ukrainian drones to overfly their airspace, especially Latvia.

**Europe**
- He says Europe is increasingly aware that the war is going badly and that an energy shock from the Persian Gulf crisis is approaching.
- He describes European diplomatic efforts as incoherent and too little, too late.
- He says Europe has missed the bus on diplomacy and has no coherent proposal for the Russians.

**Persian Gulf overlap**
- He briefly returns to the Iran / Hormuz crisis, saying that another flare-up in the Persian Gulf is occurring at the same time.
- He says the United States and Iran remain in an unstable equilibrium, with the Strait of Hormuz firmly under Iranian control.
- He cites a British naval view that the US is unlikely to sustain operations against Iran for long because of inventory depletion.

**End-state**
- Mercouris concludes that the war in Ukraine and the crisis in the Persian Gulf are both heading toward larger systemic strain.
- He says the fundamental issue remains unresolved: the US wants Iran to have no nuclear weapon, Iran says it does not want one, and in principle a negotiated solution exists, but trust and political will are absent.
- He closes by saying the diplomatic and military situation is likely to get worse before it gets better.
