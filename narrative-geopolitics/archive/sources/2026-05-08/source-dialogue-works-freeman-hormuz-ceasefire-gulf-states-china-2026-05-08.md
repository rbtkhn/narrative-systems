Let me give you a little history here. In 1763, which was the end of the so-called Seven Years' War between France and Great Britain, the British decisively defeated French sea power. They consolidated that victory in 1805 at Trafalgar, the famous battle where Horatio Nelson prevailed over the French and Spanish. Ever since 1763, as the song goes, Britannia has ruled the waves.

The day I was born, March 2nd, 1943 - 83 years ago - was the date of a three-day battle in the Bismarck Sea north of Australia. That marked the farthest advance of Japanese power in World War II. There were six Japanese troop convoys which were sunk - a huge loss to the Japanese forces. That began a counterattack which eventually defeated Japan. That moment is cited by historians as the moment in which the hegemony which had been British passed to the United States Navy. And the United States Navy ever since then has made and enforced the rules internationally.

Now, a number of things happened which are interesting in the context of the war with Iran. In the 18th century, when laws of war began to be formulated - mainly Dutch thinkers like Grotius and others - they began to codify international law. The range of a cannon was three miles. So that meant that a ship that came within three miles of the shore could theoretically be struck by a shore battery, and that led to a three-mile limit for territorial sea.

As time went on, the range of cannons, missiles, and everything expanded, and we now have missiles with terminal guidance that can strike ships at a distance of 2,000 km or 1,500 miles. That is a pretty big distance. In American terms, it's the distance between Boston and Winnipeg or Boston and Omaha, Nebraska. So the three-mile limit became absolutely meaningless, and there was an arbitrary compromise on 12 miles. Some countries like Peru were arguing for a 200-mile limit. I think Brazil joined that same argument. That ended up not as a territorial limit but as a limit for seabed resource exploitation. So the so-called salt oil that Petrobras produces is in that zone.

Anyway, all this is history. And what it illustrates - and what the war with Iran illustrates - is that one of the main provisions of the rules was passage through straits is unimpeded - freedom of navigation through straits. Well, that's gone, and so has the rest of it. So basically we're in a new era of anarchy. There are no rules.

We see American warships kept at a distance of 300 km because that is the range of Iranian cruise missiles and drones; 200 miles for aircraft carriers at a farther distance of 1,000 km or 600 miles.
