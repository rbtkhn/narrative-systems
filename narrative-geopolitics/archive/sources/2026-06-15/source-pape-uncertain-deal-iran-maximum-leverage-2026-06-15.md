# Uncertain Deal—And Iran Certainly Enters Its Period of Maximum Leverage

Escalation Trap This Week: Staying Ahead of the Risks that Matter

Prof Robert Pape
Jun 15, 2026

Despite the headlines, this deal remains highly uncertain.

President Trump announced that a memorandum of understanding would be signed Friday in Geneva. Initial reports suggested the agreement would reopen Hormuz permanently and begin sixty days of negotiations between Washington and Tehran.

Within hours, however, Iran's view of the agreement is very different.

According to Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister – the most senior to comment on the Friday signing after Trump's announcement -- Tehran expects three conditions before any long-term process begins: immediate release of $12 billion in frozen assets, an immediate and complete end to Israeli military operations in Lebanon, and significant movement toward reducing the American military presence in the Gulf. He also said Iran begins charging fees in 60 days.

Those demands tell us something important. Negotiations are no longer occurring because Iran fears defeat. They are occurring because Tehran believes time increasingly favors Iran. Iran is no longer negotiating to stop losing. It is negotiating to win more.

States gaining leverage rarely hurry to end wars.

Below I explain:

• why this agreement may actually strengthen Iran's leverage rather than reduce it;

• why August could become the most dangerous month for the global economy;

• why Iran's growing leverage may eventually weaken the American military position in the Gulf and set the stage for Iranian nuclear weapons; and

• the three indicators on whether the war is ending or moving to a new phase on ongoing violence.

These are the risks that matter now.

## Iran's Growing Leverage

For most of the war, Tehran negotiated because it feared defeat. Leadership strikes, attacks on nuclear facilities, economic pressure, and the threat of internal instability created strong incentives to seek relief.

Survival changes those incentives. Once defeat no longer appears imminent, the central question shifts from how to avoid collapse to how to transform survival into long-term advantage.

Increasingly, Iran appears to have entered that second phase.

For Trump, no question he is under enormous pressure to end the war at virtually any price. When he started it, he promised to destroy Iran's missiles and proxies, and "to the great proud people of Iran…take over your government." None of these goals will be achieved. Even in Trump's interpretation of the deal, Iran's regime remains and retains missiles, drones, proxies and no limits on expanding these tools of regional influence.

The critical source of Iran's advantage hinges on what's changing for the world's economy, not on the battlefield per se. The world's economic cushions are running out and smaller than many assume.

The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve began the war with just under 400 million barrels. Today it stands near 348 million. Energy specialists increasingly argue that the operational floor lies closer to 270 to 300 million barrels, the operational minimum after which the salt walls in the storage caverns start to physically collapse. At current draw rates, that leaves only six to eight weeks before Washington faces harder choices.

In other words, time is not neutral. Time itself is becoming a source of Iranian leverage.

For months, the administration successfully reduced immediate pressure by talking down oil prices. Politically, that strategy bought time. Economically, however, it postponed adjustment. High prices normally encourage conservation and substitution. Suppressed prices delay those responses. Delayed adjustments frequently become abrupt adjustments. If inventories continue shrinking, Iran's leverage will likely be significantly greater in August than it is today.

An indicator of Iran's growing leverage is the size of the concessions now reportedly under discussion. In 2016, President Obama faced intense domestic criticism for transferring roughly $1.7 billion to Iran after implementation of the JCPOA. Today, Tehran is reportedly demanding roughly $12 billion immediately and as much as $24 billion overall simply to begin a sixty-day negotiating process and temporarily reopen Hormuz.

Whatever one thinks of either agreement, the comparison itself is revealing. States receiving larger concessions are usually states whose bargaining position has improved, not deteriorated.

## For Iran, Negotiations Are Becoming a Power Maximizing Strategy

The reality of growing Iranian leverage creates an obvious question. Why should Tehran settle today if its leverage is likely to be greater six weeks from now? From Iran's perspective, negotiations make sense if they increasingly produce rewards.

For Iran, those rewards are geopolitical rather than economic. Iran's objective is not permanently high oil prices. Over time, extreme prices destroy demand and weaken influence.

Negotiations themselves are becoming a power maximizing strategy.

Iran is now stronger than at the beginning of the war. The regime survived. The enriched uranium survived. And Iran gained Hormuz – a source of leverage that no paper deal can diminish since it is built on new military realities of the power of mass drones. Those outcomes alone leave Tehran in a stronger position than it occupied before the opening strikes.

But states rarely stop once survival and initial gains are secured. Success creates new objectives. The question facing Iran is no longer how to restore the status quo that existed before February or to freeze the status quo of today. The question is how to gain still more power – assuring its long-term survival – and end the war stronger than it is today.

Iran's larger objective is regional primacy, becoming the dominant power in the Persian Gulf and expanding its sphere of influence beyond its borders – the same ambitions of all powers that control large fractions of the world's wealth.

The next step in achieving that objective increasingly points toward reducing American military presence in the Gulf states itself. For decades, roughly fifty thousand American personnel and a network of bases across the Gulf formed the foundation of U.S. influence in the region. And there is a straight line between Iran's growing leverage with oil and this geopolitical goal that involves changing the balance of power in the region.

From the perspective of geopolitics, Iran's strategic objective has never been simply higher oil prices or short-term gains from temporary disruption. Oil leverage is valuable because it creates political leverage – specifically opportunities to separate Gulf states from Washington, including crucial leverage for American forces to gradually withdraw from the region. Damaged bases can be rebuilt. Withdrawn bases are harder to restore.

From Tehran's perspective, therefore, the next great victory would be convincing Gulf governments that hosting American forces creates more danger than protection.

Recent diplomacy likely strengthens Tehran's confidence rather than weaken it. Mounting credible reports show that Gulf governments are increasingly willing to pay substantial economic and political funds to Iran simply to restore a degree of stability. According to Reuters, the UAE agreed to release between $10 billion and $20 billion in exchange for Iran halting missile and drone attacks. The report claimed the UAE had already transferred an initial $3 billion. Separately, Qatar reportedly offered Iran $12 billion to help secure a broader de-escalation deal.

That Gulf state reaction itself represents leverage. If temporary relief from Hormuz disruptions requires concessions today, Iranian leaders have reason to believe that even larger concessions may become possible tomorrow. From Tehran's perspective, the crisis has already demonstrated that Gulf states fear instability more than Iran fears punishment.

These developments have implications beyond oil. If Gulf governments increasingly conclude that American military deployments attract danger rather than provide protection, they may gradually begin encouraging a smaller U.S. footprint.

## The Strategic Prize

The strategic prize for Tehran is not simply reopening Hormuz or collecting economic concessions. The larger objective is changing the political calculations of the Gulf monarchies themselves. Once Gulf governments begin quietly nudging American forces outward, Iran's wartime leverage would have produced something far larger than temporary disruption. It would have altered the regional balance of power.

Oil leverage matters not because it generates revenue, but because it generates political influence. As Gulf governments increasingly conclude that instability accompanies the American military presence, pressure inevitably shifts toward the forces and bases that underpin Washington's position in the region. Once the American military footprint begins shrinking, even gradually, opportunities for Iranian hegemony expand. From Tehran's perspective, that outcome would represent a strategic transformation far more valuable than any short-term economic gains. For Iran, that is an objective worth pursing aggressively—and why its period of maximum leverage may only be beginning.

## The Withdrawal Window

If weakening American influence is the objective, then political pressure inside the United States becomes a strategic asset. The same oil leverage that pressures Gulf governments also pressures Washington. Rising prices, shrinking inventories, and growing public frustration create incentives for American leaders to reduce commitments abroad rather than expand them. The ideal outcome for Tehran is not a dramatic American retreat. It is gradual convergence, where Gulf governments quietly prefer fewer American forces and Washington quietly prefers to send fewer forces.

That objective helps explain why Iran's deputy foreign minister repeatedly emphasizes military withdrawal alongside economic demands. High on Tehran's list are likely to be the forces deployed during the war itself: one or both carrier strike groups, many of the destroyers shifted into the region, roughly 10,000 Marines and airborne troops deployed as reinforcements, and many of the aircraft and refueling tankers transferred from Europe and Asia. Beyond those additions, Tehran almost certainly seeks eventual reductions in the roughly 50,000 American personnel who formed the prewar military presence. Washington may focus on uranium. Iran is likely focused on something larger: changing the military balance of the Gulf.

For Iran, the late summer and fall may represent a rare strategic opportunity. Inventory shortages create leverage. Leverage creates political pressure. Political pressure creates opportunities for military withdrawal. And military withdrawal creates opportunities to fill the vacuum and gain power. From Tehran's perspective, that sequence is already beginning to emerge.

By late summer and fall, Iran will attempt to convert growing oil leverage into gradual American military withdrawal from the Gulf.

## Negotiations as a Weapon

In Tehran's eyes, negotiations are valuable not because they end the war, but because they help shape the political order that emerges afterward. If Iran's objective is regional primacy, then separating Gulf states from Washington becomes more important than defeating American forces directly. Recent mediation efforts by Qatar, the UAE, and other Gulf governments already suggest that regional actors increasingly prefer accommodation to confrontation.

Future negotiations are therefore likely to focus less on grand settlements and more on practical regional arrangements. Expect more Gulf diplomacy, more economic understandings, more calls for "regional solutions," and greater efforts by Gulf governments to avoid becoming battlefields in a prolonged U.S.-Iran confrontation. If those trends continue, the political center of gravity in the Gulf will gradually shift away from confrontation and toward coexistence with Iran—the postwar order Tehran increasingly appears to be seeking.

## The Ultimate Question

History will ultimately judge this war by a single question: did the war—and the agreement designed to end it—actually prevent Iran from becoming a nuclear weapons state? Ironically, the very dynamics that have strengthened Iran's bargaining position may also strengthen Tehran's incentives to seek the one form of leverage that cannot be blockaded, sanctioned, or negotiated away. The regime survived. The leadership survived. Oil leverage worked. And if growing leverage now creates pressure for American military withdrawal and regional accommodation, Iranian leaders may reasonably conclude that an eventual nuclear deterrent would lock those gains into place permanently.

Nothing in the current framework appears to require Iran to alter behavior on the nuclear issue, except a generic pledge not to obtain a weapon. If anything, the combination of survival, bargaining success, and declining confidence in outside protection creates incentives pointing in the opposite direction.

For Tehran, nuclear weapons would not simply provide security. They would become the ultimate foundation of regional primacy.

## Three Indicators I'm Watching This Week

### 1. Lebanon

Lebanon remains the easiest way for this agreement to unravel. Iran has repeatedly linked broader de-escalation to events there, and Israel continues striking Hezbollah targets. If Israel can continue operations without provoking Iranian retaliation, the agreement has a chance to survive. If Lebanon reignites, everything else quickly becomes secondary.

### 2. Insurance Rates

Insurance markets often reveal danger before governments do. Even if ships continue moving through Hormuz, elevated premiums would signal that commercial actors still expect future disruptions. Markets care less about diplomatic statements than about risk. If insurance rates remain high, the world economy will continue paying a war premium even during a ceasefire.

### 3. American Military Withdrawals

What matters most is not what leaders say. It is where forces actually go. Wars end politically only after deployments begin reversing. If carriers, destroyers, aircraft, and ground forces start leaving the region, the endgame may be finally beginning. Until then, we remain in the middle game of the Escalation Trap.

### Bonus Indicator

#### 4. Nuclear Proliferation

Watch whether countries such as Saudi Arabia, the UAE, South Korea, and Japan begin discussing nuclear deterrence more openly. One lesson increasingly visible to leaders around the world is that states without nuclear weapons—Ukraine and now Iran before the war—can see their homelands devastated, while states possessing nuclear weapons—North Korea above all—avoid direct attacks aimed at regime destruction. If more countries begin concluding that nuclear weapons are the only reliable guarantee of sovereignty and regime survival, the long-term consequences of this war will extend far beyond the Persian Gulf.

The headlines change. The logic does not. The purpose of The Escalation Trap is not to predict every event, but to identify the forces that make certain outcomes increasingly likely. That is why understanding the mechanism matters more than following the headlines.
