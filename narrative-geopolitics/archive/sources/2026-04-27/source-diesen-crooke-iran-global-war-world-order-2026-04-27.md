# Alastair Crooke: Iran War Is Now a Global War for World Order

**Diesen:** Welcome back. Today is April 27th, 2026, and I have the great pleasure of being joined by Alastair Crooke, who is a renowned British diplomat and negotiator who worked for decades on addressing conflicts in the Middle East. He is also the founder and director of Conflicts Forum, and the link to his great Substack can be found in the description.

So thank you very much for coming back on the program.

**Alastair Crooke:** Thank you. Thank you. Always a pleasure joining you.

**Diesen:** So it seems that we are at risk of a greater war with both Iran and Russia. But I thought we could start with Iran.

First of all, there’s a lot of confusion here. That is, are the peace negotiations still going on? Is the ceasefire actually still here?

But overall, I also see the problem of how we can end this war. That is, is the United States seeking an end to the Iran war? And if so, what do you think is the possible off-ramp or exit strategy of the United States?

**Alastair Crooke:** Well, there is a lot of propaganda and there’s a lot of confusion. Most of it is propaganda. A lot of it is propaganda.

So, I mean, okay, because there has been a substantive change take place, and we ought to try and understand that.

But first of all, to say that there was never a proposal for Araghchi to meet with Kushner or Witkoff in Islamabad. This was a fantasy created by Trump. It doesn’t refer to any plans or proposals.

Trump has said very clearly, well, you know, there was really no point to having any talks with them until they get their act together and produce a plan of what they want. We don’t know what they want.

But of course he does, because what Iran wants was spelled out very clearly in the 10-point plan that was given to the Americans for the Islamabad talks, and which Trump said was a realistic basis for discussion, if you recall at that time.

So they have put out very clearly in those 10 points their negotiating position, and they haven’t changed from it, except now, and for some time, I’ve been saying — and I looked through and reread the JCPOA, even though I’ve been, one way or another, dealing with it for more than 20 years. I reread it and I looked at that and I saw, why on earth would Iran want to go back to that?

It’s like being on parole from prison. You know, you’ve been on parole for a few weeks’ leave, and then the prison gate is opening and you’re going to go back into your cage. I mean, not a literal cage, but a cage of being surrounded by American military bases everywhere; the cage of sanctions, of tariffs, of UN resolutions, of IAEA inspections.

I mean, would you want to go back to that?

And what’s happened over the last few days, and what Araghchi has been busy with, what he went to Pakistan to do, was to tell them their new negotiating principles, which are basically simply not that different except one bombshell difference.

They’re not prepared to discuss a nuclear issue. They’re not prepared to discuss it until they have resolved the points about the war, about the blockade of Hormuz, about the attacks on them and on Iranians, and also the sanctions that are being imposed, that tankers are being seized, or arrested and seized, by U.S. officials.

So they said, this is it for the moment. These are the things we’re going to discuss. We’re going to discuss Hormuz, and we’re going to discuss ceasefire — or not, maybe not — and we’re going to discuss the ending of the attacks on us and on others like Hezbollah in Lebanon.

So we’re going to deal with that. Maybe later we’ll talk about nuclear issues. That’s what they say.

And Qalibaf did a tweet, I think, last night, and he said: Iran, you know, Trump keeps saying he has all the cards, but listen to our cards. One is we control, and will continue, we are able to control Bab al-Mandab and the Red Sea, and we can control pipelines.

And what have they got on the other side? Well, they’ve got a naval blockade.

And the question really — he didn’t put it in these terms, but I’m saying — which is the shorter timeline? The timeline between the Iranian blockade, which is porous, it’s open to specific vessels, but what is the timeline between that creating serious damage in the Western economy? And the timeline, the Trump American naval blockade, which is not very much observed, which is really quite porous. Vessels have been passing through, out to China.

How long before that puts real pressure on Iran?

And the answer is, contrary to what Trump believes, that after the 26th of April, yesterday, no, Iran’s oil supplies, it’s all going to crash and they’ll be broke. It’s not true.

First of all, Iran is continuing to get tankers out by following the coast very closely into territorial waters, getting their loaded tankers out. They have been earning more in a month of the war. They’ve been earning twice what they ever earned in the best years beforehand.

Just one day, four tankers leaving Kharg Island a little while ago, produced an income — with 72 million barrels — or produced an income of just under $1 billion, $815 million.

They have oil at sea. Before the war, they put 200 million barrels at sea. They have 130 left, I think, 100, well, 100 to 130. So they can go on for months, really.

And in any case, it’s a misunderstanding of the nature of Iran and the nature of their strategy to think they’re about to capitulate or surrender and come on their knees and beg for your indulgence.

Iran is not just the usual nation-state that you take out a baseball bat and you bash them and you take away all their money, and then they’ll come on their knees.

Iran is, in many ways, a very different vehicle. It started as a revolt against Western modernity, with the influence of Sayyid Qutb and others. But it was always a civilizational state — civilizational and revolutionary — and not just a sort of 19th-century linear, autocratic nation that tried to enforce a national identity.

They deliberately didn’t enforce a national identity, and they felt Islam was a civilizational element in it.

So I don’t think — I think that the calculation the Iranians feel is in their favor. And I don’t know — I admit it clearly, I don’t know whether they intend ultimately, if they’ve got the right deal, to talk about the nuclear issue.

I don’t think there’s any chance of Iran handing over 430 kilos of enriched uranium to the Americans ever.

So we’re in a slightly new era, which is why I think there’s mischief going on in Washington, because Trump’s hoped-for quick solution to this conflict is evaporating.

I know there are people who feel that the solution could be put together. I don’t see a path to the solution, and I can explain why, if you would like.

**Diesen:** Actually, yeah, that’s my next question. I was going to ask what prospects there are for a negotiated settlement.

First, you described returning to the JCPOA as almost a prison, and it does feel like this is the war objective of Iran: a prison break, in which control of Hormuz is essentially the main instrument towards this end.

Because if they have the Strait of Hormuz, where they can set up a toll and set conditions for transit, essentially the countries who put sanctions on Iran, who threaten it with war by hosting bases or actually attacking — all of them can be, well, to some extent disciplined if they want to have proper access.

So it seems very critical for Iran, if they don’t want to go back to the past decades of crippling sanctions and the threat of war all the time, that the Strait of Hormuz is non-negotiable.

But I want to ask how you see the possibility, therefore, of an actual negotiated settlement. And to make matters more complicated, you can throw in Israel’s territorial ambitions and, of course, the desire to crack Lebanon like it did Syria.

So how do you see the possibility of actually meeting halfway somewhere in a negotiation?

**Alastair Crooke:** Well, going back to what you just said, I would just add to it. I’m sure you intended it, but we’ve seen supply lines are beginning — there’s a sort of rumbling of catastrophe already apparent because various crucial elements to supply lines pass through Hormuz, as well as oil and gas: helium, acetic acid, gallium, all of these things pass through.

And what happens is that they go on getting slightly, slightly worse, and then they break. And when they break, we in the West are going to feel the consequences directly: that there won’t be diesel in the petrol stations or gasoline stations. There will be difficulty getting your children to school. As a consequence, there will not be the food that you want. You will not have aluminum foil in the shops any longer. Aluminum comes through Hormuz, overwhelmingly.

So, I mean, we are in this moment that we all have heard that it’s going to be so serious, yet it hasn’t fully impacted. In Australia it has. In Ireland it has, where there are protests about not being able to get cars fueled.

But it’s about — all the economic experts of the West say this.

What I’m saying, that I don’t think there’s a path through this, certainly not one that’s going to be very easily achieved, is that there are two major roadblocks in this process.

And the first major roadblock is Trump. I’m not just talking about his mental state, which we all know is contributory. I mean, he’s dominant because he still runs the show at the White House. He hasn’t been like Biden, largely removed from it. He’s very much in control of it. In control, I say loosely in control, but nonetheless, he likes to present himself as being there.

But more than that is that the roots of this, in many ways, his feeling about Iran, has been his dislike of Obama and the sense of rivalry that he’s always felt with Obama.

So what he’d always said was that Obama’s JCPOA was terrible. It was awful. It was imperfect, and I can do much better.

But he needs something like that, a better JCPOA than Obama, to prove that he, Trump, was the better negotiator. There’s a long history to this sort of bitter rivalry.

And when you look at this, I mean, even before what I said at the beginning about the change in tactics, there wasn’t going to be a better JCPOA coming out of this. If there was a JCPOA 2, it would be more favorable for the Iranians than the first one. Otherwise there would be none.

So it would have to be more favorable, and that would not go well with Trump, nor would it go well with Netanyahu.

So the first thing is, you know, the only thing that could really assuage him — and he in advance recognized that — was the one thing that could do it: the Iranians would suddenly hand over 430 kilos of enriched uranium, and he would hold it up like a trophy, a football trophy at the end of a game, and say, look, I won, I won, we succeeded, there’ll never be a nuclear weapon, etc.

Well, I don’t think Iran is going to do that, because they realize this is their great asset in these negotiations, and they’re not going to hand it over.

But also, Trump didn’t like it when Vance started talking from Islamabad. By all the reports we hear, Vance was saying, yes, they will continue to charge a fee on Hormuz, and they will have control over it, and they’ll share it with Oman, etc., but then they will become more powerful. Iran will become one of the big states because, if they control Hormuz and get fees for the next half-year or year, they’ll have money rolling in.

So supposing Vance went back to these talks with his two minders, Kushner and Witkoff, and he came back and said, look, we’ve got a deal on 3.67 enrichment, and the timeline would be extended, and there would be monitoring and all of that.

Would Trump say okay, or would he say, but this isn’t a win for me because this is just JCPOA, this is Obama’s JCPOA, and this doesn’t show that I’ve achieved anything?

So I think there’s a real problem there. And of course there’s still the other problem of his mental condition.

I think various people have spoken of late who’ve got more direct knowledge — Robert Barnes and others — explaining how it’s more than just being erratic. It’s a sense of confabulation taking place, of huge anger, and being unable to take criticism, unable to really understand what’s going on. I mean, very serious.

And so one question is, well, how would the team manage this if things get worse? How would they manage Trump? Not the conflict, but how would they manage Trump?

It seems from the reporting that, not this Saturday but the Saturday before, General Caine said no. And it seems even on this last Saturday, when there was the meeting about Iran, there was some sort of agreement by the team, when there was a proposal for further mass bombing of electricity and infrastructure. All of them, maybe bar one, said no, this is a war crime. This is not a military mission. We cannot accept this.

Can this continue? I don’t know. You’d have to ask someone better equipped in the American field for how long this can go on like this, because there’s no real way I can see for them to change that so easily.

I mean, the 25th Amendment is unlikely to be possible. Are we going to, should we be prepared to go down the route of some sort of indictment? I don’t know.

But this is the second reason I say it’s extremely difficult.

This is the first barrier, if I describe it, to coming to some sort of agreement. If it’s not about the nuclear, what would it be? The only next stage then would possibly be another attempt to go into Isfahan and take out, if it’s already in Isfahan, the enriched material from Isfahan.

After what happened, I mean, it was — and Joe Kent keeps telling him, well, if you go on like this, you end up being like Jimmy Carter after Jimmy Carter’s disastrous attempt to rescue the hostages from Iran, the hostages in the embassy there, when the helicopters crashed. You remember, it was a disaster. You lost the presidency.

And he keeps spelling it in. I think it hurts.

So I don’t know whether they’ll do that. Well, that only leaves what? Bombing, carpet bombing. And it hasn’t succeeded in destroying Iran’s missile capabilities.

So we’re talking about either just repeating bombing mountains, pretty pointlessly, or it will be more likely just bombing civilian infrastructure, killing people, knocking down apartments, which — you have to ask the lawyers if that’s going to be considered a war crime or not.

Anyway, maybe he will double down and say, well, I’ve got to put pressure on. They’ve got to agree, because otherwise my polling, my chances for the midterms, are fading very, very far. We’ve really got to put pressure.

And so I don’t really see what it is that Trump could obtain from these events.

Iran is not about to fully open. I was listening to some of the Gulf states, and they said, we can’t have tolls, and they must be open, it must be without tolls. We can’t accept that. It’s not possible.

Which is one of the reasons why the Pakistanis — Pakistan, of course, is very close to Saudi Arabia — and Saudi Arabia says, oh, it can’t have tolls on it. It’s got to be as it used to be before on the 27th of February.

Come on. I mean, what do you — you don’t like tolls. I mean, nor do I. I used to be able to read newspapers without paying anything. You went online and you could read them, and then suddenly there was a subscription and a toll posted on it. And of course I got used to reading them for free, and it’s always a painful adjustment when you have to pay it.

If I drive a car on a motorway here, I have to pay a toll. I mean, grow up. This is how things are.

And that proposal to put a toll is — I’m not a lawyer — but I think is legal under the UN Treaty on the Law of the Sea, because all of Hormuz, the actual Strait of Hormuz, is within Iran’s territorial water or Oman’s territorial water on the other side. There’s no international part at all. It’s too narrow.

So they will continue with this, I believe.

And so this doesn’t offer a very easy solution for Trump to deal with his blockade. His naval blockade is poor. Iranian vessels are going in and they’re coming out. And we’ve seen that Lloyd’s shipping thing, put in the Financial Times, and I think Trump was rather angry that some 30 Iranian ships in the last few days — this was a few days ago — had traversed the Strait at this time.

So where is the solution, other than bombing, which won’t be a solution because it won’t cause the Iranians to come back?

One of the things that is so clear is the change in atmosphere in Iraq — Iran. I mean, there is a new energy, a new sense of the revolution updating. It’s the young, more than the older generations who participated in the revolution.

No, these are the youngsters. These are great columns of young women, armed to the teeth, going out onto the streets every night in support of the Iranian state.

I mean, it’s a real — it’s something that is going to have long-term impacts, I believe, beyond Iran, in places like Russia and China too.

That’s what I get, that this sort of robust expression of a rejection of one reality and a determination to find and achieve their own sovereignty — and that mystique is also acting against agreement with America.

You ask the young — I mean, you can see it on many of the videos. You ask them about what they see in the picture and they say, why are we in negotiation with America? Talking? Why are our leadership talking to — why, when they bomb our houses and our schools and our hospitals? No.

So the mood is hardening at another level as well, which is important. It’s not reflected in the Western media and their understanding. But you’ve only got to look at the videos, and the evidence is there.

I mean, you see these columns of women going out to defend bridges and power stations and so on that they feel are threatened.

And then you come to the second barricade. Supposing you find a solution with Trump. How do you find a solution with Israel?

And I wrote a paper for the Substack about how Israel had changed. Some 15 years ago I said you cannot go understanding Israel through sort of secular, rational, Enlightenment-type structures. You have to understand that it’s becoming a conflict of religious poles in the Middle East.

And it really has changed further in that direction, because what we’re seeing, Israel has now become much more messianic, apocalyptic, eschatological. Seventy percent, basically, of Jewish Israelis support this.

And the most important thing, I think, was, it’s not some passing moment that is going to come and go with the elections, and then we’ll have back an Ashkenazi secular regime.

Because, as the great Jewish historian Gershom Scholem has written, messianism has been a very important impulse in the Jewish faith over the years, from the 1600s, in the 19th century, with the Sabbatean branch and others.

And he predicted some years ago that religious Zionism which, he said, he predicted in recent decades, is aligned with Likud and the settlement movement and operates as a militant, apocalyptic, and radical messianic movement that tries to force the end by demanding that the state engage in, for example, massive territorial control.

This is heaven. They demand territorial conquest for end-of-times reasons. Forcing it is about forcing redemption and the messianic age coming into being.

So my point about this was, if you go back to the Reagan administration, when they tried to, and to a certain extent did, impose restraint on Israel, is this likely in the new atmosphere of Israel? Are they going to accept restraint because that might be in America’s interest?

And it is in America’s interest. Let’s be quite clear. Of course it’s in America’s interest. They don’t want the whole Middle East set alight in a move towards Armageddon. They don’t want that.

So they call for restraint. But look, is it reasonable to think that that’s going to happen? I think not.

Because I think, if you listen to the latest statement by Katz threatening everything against — that Israel will attack this disgusting country of Iran — the language was extraordinary. It was purely apocalyptic language coming out from the defense minister of Israel.

So I don’t think restraint is going to be possible. And it’s not possible because, in Israel, Netanyahu is under huge pressure, because the people are saying, well, your war on Iran failed. It didn’t achieve any of its objectives. And your war on Hezbollah, which you told us was all successful, so successful, had been eliminated — now we find it was never true. And you tell us, therefore, that the war on Iran is going to be a great success. It’s not true.

So he is in trouble. So he needs a success.

And so what you see in the press, in the Israeli press, even today, this morning, is: we have to get the Americans to continue the war.

Is this in the American interest? I doubt it. But this is in the, if you like, messianic interest of Israel, to get the U.S. to continue the war.

And so what’s going to happen? Well, when you look at it, when there is a divergence of interest to the United States — who, I would argue, actually, the interest of the Republican Party, the interest of Trump personally, is to find a solution to an end to the war. And that is the American interest largely. Not everyone in America agrees with that. Mark Levin won’t.

But if you put that aside, I would say, actually, at the end of the day on this, Israel doesn’t give a damn about America, and it doesn’t give a damn for the Gulf states either. It has something which surpasses all those rational, mechanistic strategies of saying, but surely it’s not in Israel’s interest to go to a major war with Iran.

Well, I remember Smotrich six or seven years ago in a video to Likud, which I watched then, and he said, listen, this was the plan. We’re going to get rid of all the Arabs from the land of Israel. But I have to tell you, to do this, to complete the plan, we have to have — we need a major crisis or a big, big war, and then we will finish the plan.

In other words, to tell them that if you do something silly, you’ll get Armageddon — they want Armageddon. This is the path towards salvation, redemption.

So I find it very difficult to see a simple path. I don’t think the idea that Iran can be persuaded by Russia and China to go back to a JCPOA-type prison is now on the cards.

And now Iran has said on the record publicly: no, we’re not talking the nuclear issue now. We’re talking economic cost and we’re talking about sanctions and tariffs. This is going to be the discussion. Maybe, if all that’s resolved, who knows? We can talk the nuclear issue again.

**Diesen:** Yeah. Well, this is not the United States, but I saw von der Leyen from the European Union making the point that sanctions will not come off Iran because of values. The regime has to change this and that.

It’s intended to sound very principled, but it also comes off as delusional at times.

But they do the same with China and Russia. That is, if they fail to achieve anything, I think part of it is because there’s difficulty in transitioning to a multipolar world. Because they know what they want to get, but it prevents them from seeing what they actually can get, or how they want the world to be. They fail to see how the world actually is, and they often frame it in morality.

**Alastair Crooke:** Yeah, that’s my point about mechanistic, sort of secular thinking that is so embedded in their particular grasp of reality that they’re completely incapable of seeing that others have different ways of seeing reality from a different perspective.

And they can’t understand that.

And of course, this is why I said I can’t see Iran going back to this JCPOA agreement, because they know — I mean, we’re talking 47 years since the first sanctions were put on — they’re never going to be lifted, not while Israel has any say.

And of course, von der Leyen is representing Israel as much as anyone else in this process.

Of course the containment, sanctions, siege on Iran, to break the Iranian spirit, gets also some support from Gulf states, but principally from Israel and from elements within the United States.

So this is why I keep saying Iran’s aim in this war is to break the whole paradigm — the paradigm of putting Iran, as you put it, in a jail.

**Diesen:** Exactly.

Just a final question, then. It seems like this is then a war of attrition, both in the military sense but also economic. That is, who can absorb the most pain and, of course, inflict pain on the other side.

Do you think it’s clear, though, that time is on the side of the Iranians? Or how do you see it? Because it seems all sides are under great pressure now.

**Alastair Crooke:** Oh no, time is on the side of the Iranians in an unqualified way.

They have been making more money during this war. I mean revenues — I’m not talking about businessmen, but money revenues to the state have come in far higher since they’ve had control of the Strait of Hormuz and charged payment.

And Iran isn’t short of money. It has probably 130 million barrels of oil still at sea that they will sell gradually. They can keep going for a long time. They may start charging fees for passage to the Bab al-Mandab Strait through.

Whereas we in the West are closer — and as I say, I don’t think people have appreciated that we have about two weeks before it’s going to really hit. And when it hits, people won’t be able to afford to fly. They won’t be able to easily get their cars refueled. Cost of electricity and things are going to go shooting up.

And Europe is going to be in dark pain.

I live here in Italy, and there’s a huge cost-of-living crisis. There has been no increase in real wages since Italy joined the euro. No increase in real incomes since then. That’s a long, long time to sort of squeeze and squeeze and squeeze down.

And when there are protests, and the train drivers say, how do you expect people to go on when the income that they’re getting has run out by week three of a month, and they have four weeks in a month? I mean, it’s just not working.

So I think the pain is going to come very much to the economic elites, the ones that are trading.

But I just want to say one thing, if you’ve got a minute before — I know you want to finish — but I will just say that this is also something much bigger.

It is, and this is why it’s part of a global war. The other thing that Iran is fighting in this war is, if you like, the financial architecture, not just the dollar hegemony, not just the petrodollar, but the whole structure by which the Gulf states were sort of set up in ’73: keep the oil price high and transfer the enormous excess savings that came from it into Wall Street.

And this underpinned the financialized world that we live in, which incidentally is the source of much of our problems, because it’s come at the price of, if you like, depressing or suppressing the real economy in favor of an economy that is all about trading, about making instant money on markets, but not by actually producing things.

In the real world, it’s much quicker. You can make a few hundred million in an afternoon by insider trading on the markets.

And one of the things I think is going to be a long-term consequence of this war is a consequence of the complete destruction of markets and pricing that’s taking place as a result of all of this insider trading, of this complete disparity that people can see for themselves.

You know, we pick up the paper and they say, oh well, oil has just gone down from 98 to 93. Try buying a barrel of oil at that price. It’s just completely manipulated.

If you want to buy a barrel of oil, you’re going to have to pay over 200. Sri Lanka has just been paying well over 200 to get oil just to keep going.

So what’s pricing? What are markets? What is this market that says the price of oil is 90-something when you actually have to pay double that price to get a real barrel, physical oil, that you can put into the system and keep it running?

It’s going to have a huge impact.

So that’s why the Iranians are saying, I’m sorry, but if you want to have relations with Iran, you’ve got to get rid of Microsoft and Amazon and all of your links. We don’t want that type of economic umbrella which has suffocated us and been antagonistic to us for 47 years.

The umbrella of the hegemony of the dollar and the hegemony of the military, coupled with a hegemony of a financialized world, coupled with, if you like, the sense of the control of the UN, the IAEA, and all these structures that are employed against Iran.

So I think it has this global aspect.

**Diesen:** No, that’s interesting. Well, I don’t think Trump expected that this war, which was supposed to be a quick Venezuela-like operation, would turn out into a war which could bring down the American world order. It’s quite a dramatic development.

**Alastair Crooke:** Always a pleasure to join you.
