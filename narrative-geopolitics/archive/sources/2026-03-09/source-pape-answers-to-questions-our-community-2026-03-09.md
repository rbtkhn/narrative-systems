# Answers to Questions Our Community is Asking

From Yesterday's Live Briefing, March 8

Our first live briefing was a great success.

More than anything, I was struck by the quality of the questions. The discussion ranged from escalation dynamics and air power to the risks to global energy markets and the prospects for de-escalation. That level of engagement is exactly why I wanted to start these briefings in the first place.

### Next live briefing

Sunday, March 22 — 4:00 PM CT / 5:00 PM ET

I’ll continue analyzing the war and answering questions live. If the last session was any indication, the conversation should be lively.

Below are five of the most important questions from the briefing, along with my answers.

## 1. If the U.S. military understands the risks of escalation, why do leaders still fall into the escalation trap?

Political leaders face powerful incentives to escalate that the military does not.

In the American system, we have civilian control of the military—which means political leaders ultimately make the decisions. The military provides professional advice, but elected officials set strategy. Historically, the U.S. military has handled this arrangement with professionalism, even when it disagrees with the political leadership.

Politicizing the military would not solve this problem—it would make it worse. If military advice becomes shaped by political loyalty rather than professional judgment, the risk of escalation increases. That concern is especially relevant in today’s political environment, where some voices emphasize loyalty to individuals over institutional responsibility.

The military can and often does propose risk-mitigation options—backup strategies designed to limit escalation if the initial plan fails. But in conflicts like this, the political incentives pushing leaders to escalate are often stronger than the mitigation strategies designed to contain them.

This dynamic is exactly what I call the Smart Bomb Trap. Early tactical success with precision strikes can create the belief that escalation will remain controllable. Historically, however, those early successes often mask deeper strategic risks that eventually overwhelm the original plan.

## 2. What outcome would cause you to reconsider your thesis about the limits of coercive air power?

The clearest test would be rapid political concession by Iran.

If Iran were to quickly agree to the core U.S. demand—for example, transferring its enriched uranium, or a substantial portion of it, out of the country under peaceful arrangements—that would challenge my expectations.

The key issue in coercion is whether military pressure produces political compliance quickly. If the air campaign alone produced that outcome, it would suggest that the strategy is working in a way that historically has been rare.

If instead we see continued resistance, gradual escalation, and growing pressure for additional military steps, then we are likely seeing the familiar historical pattern in which air power alone struggles to produce decisive political results.

## 3. What is the probability of U.S. ground forces entering the conflict?

At this point I would estimate roughly a 75% probability of some form of limited U.S. ground deployment.

When coercive air campaigns fail to produce rapid concessions, leaders often feel pressure to add new tools to restore momentum and credibility. Limited ground operations are one of the most common next steps.

The problem is that even a small ground presence can change the political dynamics of the war.

Once foreign troops enter the territory of a rival state—even in limited numbers—it often triggers nationalist mobilization. People who may not strongly support their government can still rally against what they perceive as foreign occupation.

That mobilization usually begins slowly. It may take weeks or months to gather strength. But once it builds momentum, it becomes very difficult to reverse.

Importantly, political messaging about the mission—whether it is described as limited, defensive, or temporary—rarely changes this dynamic. What matters most is the presence of foreign troops on the ground.

## 4. Could Iran close the Strait of Hormuz?

Iran would not need to permanently close the Strait of Hormuz to create major disruption.

If Iran were to deploy 100 to 300 naval mines in the strait, that alone could effectively halt commercial shipping for a significant period of time.

Even under favorable conditions for the United States—where U.S. forces conduct mine-clearing operations without interference—the process would likely take weeks to months.

Mine clearing is one of the most time-consuming and technically demanding naval operations. Each suspected mine must be located, investigated, and neutralized carefully. During that time, insurance costs for shipping surge and many vessels avoid the area altogether.

If Iran were able to complicate clearing operations with drones, small boats, or missile threats, the disruption could last even longer.

In other words, Iran does not need to seal the strait permanently. Temporary uncertainty alone can produce major global energy consequences.

## 5. What would signal real de-escalation?

The clearest signal would be withdrawal of U.S. combat forces from the conflict.

Diplomatic statements alone rarely change the trajectory of wars. What matters are observable changes in military posture.

As long as combat operations continue or expand, the incentives pushing leaders toward escalation remain in place.

Given the current dynamics, it is difficult to imagine that kind of disengagement in the coming weeks. Once a war begins and political stakes rise on both sides, leaders often feel pressure to continue until they can claim some form of strategic success.

That is one reason wars frequently last longer than originally expected, even when their initial goals seemed limited.
