# Trump May Be Approaching the Biggest Smart Bomb Trap Yet

Why Tactical Intelligence Could Trigger Strategic Disaster

Prof Robert Pape
May 23, 2026

Over the past forty-eight hours, the signals coming out of Washington have become increasingly difficult to ignore.

Trump canceled plans to attend his son’s wedding this weekend, scrapped his New Jersey trip, and remained in Washington for emergency consultations as senior intelligence and military officials reportedly canceled Memorial Day leave. Commercial air traffic over Iran has thinned dramatically amid growing regional warning notices, while multiple reports suggest the White House is again actively considering new military strikes.

This does not necessarily mean bombing is imminent.

But it does suggest the administration may be approaching a dangerous decision point.

And the real danger is not simply another round of strikes on Iran.

The real danger is that Trump may be approaching the biggest Smart Bomb Trap yet.

## Tactical Intelligence and the Decapitation Temptation

If the strikes come, the most likely trigger may not be a missile launch or tanker attack.

It may be intelligence.

More specifically, intelligence placing Iran’s new supreme leader or senior regime leadership in a known location vulnerable to immediate attack.

That pattern would fit the history of modern American precision-war thinking almost perfectly.

The 2003 Iraq War effectively began when U.S. intelligence believed Saddam Hussein was meeting senior aides at Dora Farms outside Baghdad. The opening “shock and awe” strike was launched immediately in an effort to kill Saddam at the start of the war. He survived.

The February 28 strikes on Iran reportedly followed a similar logic: intelligence indicating a high-level leadership meeting that created a narrow window for decapitation targeting. The Supreme Leader and other leaders were killed.

This is one of the most seductive dynamics in modern airpower.

The possibility that one precise strike could suddenly end the crisis, collapse the regime, restore deterrence, and produce a dramatic political victory.

For any president under pressure, that possibility becomes extraordinarily difficult to resist.

Especially for Trump, whose instinct in crisis has repeatedly been to search for decisive demonstrations of strength through precision force.

## The Logic of the Trap

The basic mechanism is now becoming visible.

The United States can inflict enormous tactical damage on Iran quickly. American stealth aircraft, cruise missiles, cyber capabilities, and intelligence systems remain vastly superior to anything Tehran can match conventionally.

But modern wars are not decided simply by tactical destruction -- They are decided politically. That is where the trap begins.

Iran’s strategy has never depended on defeating the United States militarily. Its strategy depends on surviving politically long enough to make escalation itself become the problem.

That is exactly what happened in Vietnam, Iraq, and Afghanistan.

And it is increasingly what is happening now in the Gulf. The main difference is that Iran is not merely surviving – it is using its asymmetric capabilities to gain significant power over the global economy.

## Tactical Success, Strategic Failure

A new American air campaign would likely attack Iran’s leadership and destroy major Iranian military infrastructure rapidly. Missile facilities, drone launch areas, IRGC bases, coastal strike systems, and command nodes would suffer enormous damage in the opening days. A new three day air campaign meant to shift the bargaining advantages back toward the United States is most likely.

The Pentagon would almost certainly declare major operational success almost immediately.

But none of that solves the central strategic problem.

Iran would still retain dispersed missile capability, underground infrastructure, asymmetric escalation pathways, and — most importantly — the ability to widen economic disruption across the Gulf faster than Washington could stabilize it.

Especially if the US attacks Iran’s leaders, retaliation could well include Saudi, UAE, and Kuwaiti leaders – the leadership of countries that Iran would surely like to weaken decisively as US allies crucial to future basing of more military power against Iran in the future.

That is the key asymmetry in this war.

The United States is fighting primarily with military leverage.

Iran is fighting with its own version of escalation “leverage,” able to threaten to move up rungs on the ladder.

And escalation leverage increasingly favors the side willing to prolong instability rather than rapidly resolve it.

## The Economic Front

This is why the Hormuz issue matters so profoundly.

Even limited disruption inside the Gulf now threatens shipping insurance, diesel markets, LNG flows, aviation fuel systems, and Asian manufacturing supply chains simultaneously.

The longer the crisis lasts, the greater the political pressure on Washington becomes.

And that is where Smart Bomb Traps become historically dangerous:
when tactical military success and worsening strategic conditions begin happening at the same time.

At that point, leaders face growing pressure for broader escalation simply to restore the credibility and stability the original strikes failed to produce.

That is how limited bombing campaigns become larger wars.

The next few days may therefore become extremely important — not simply because Trump may strike Iran again, but because the United States may be approaching the point where overwhelming military superiority no longer guarantees politically sustainable control at acceptable economic cost.

That is the real lesson emerging from the Iran war.

And Beijing, Moscow, Riyadh, and every major capital in the world are watching carefully.
