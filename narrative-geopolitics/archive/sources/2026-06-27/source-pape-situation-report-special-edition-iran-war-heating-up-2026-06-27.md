# Situation Report — Special Edition

### The Iran War Is Heating Up Again

Prof Robert Pape

Jun 27, 2026

For much of the past week, attention drifted away from the Middle East. That was a mistake.

Over the past three days, Iran, the United States, and Israel have taken a series of actions that suggest the current ceasefire is far less stable than many assumed. Commercial shipping has again become a target. U.S. forces have resumed direct strikes. Maritime security organizations are raising threat levels. The incentives for another round of escalation are growing rather than shrinking.

Below is what happened, why it matters, and what to watch next.

What Happened
Over the past three days, the Persian Gulf experienced its most serious escalation since the U.S.–Iran memorandum was signed.

Day One: Thursday (Gulf Time)
Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps attacked the Singapore-flagged cargo vessel Ever Lovely with 4 drones, one of which hit the ship while it transited the Strait of Hormuz along the Omani coast. The vessel suffered bridge damage but no casualties. Following the attack, the International Maritime Organization suspended its evacuation operation for hundreds of stranded commercial vessels while governments reassessed maritime security.

Day Two: Friday
The United States responded with limited air strikes against Iranian coastal radar, missile, and drone facilities supporting operations around the Strait of Hormuz. Washington described the strikes as defensive enforcement of the existing memorandum rather than the beginning of a new campaign.

Day Three: Saturday Morning
Iran retaliated again. A second commercial vessel came under attack near Hormuz, while Iranian drones targeted U.S.-linked facilities in Bahrain. Maritime security alerts increased across the Gulf, insurers reassessed risk, and shipping organizations warned that confidence in the waterway remained fragile. Commercial traffic through Hormuz remains well below normal levels despite the formal reopening of the strait.

Why It Matters
The conventional interpretation is that these are isolated incidents testing a fragile ceasefire.

I believe something more important is happening.

The Iran War ended without resolving the underlying balance of power. Iran survived. It retained leverage over Hormuz. The United States accepted an agreement rather than imposing decisive military defeat. Israel remains dissatisfied with the outcome.

That means all three actors still possess incentives to change the postwar settlement.

The full strategic implications—and the policy choices now facing Washington—were the focus of last Monday's edition of The Escalation Trap This Week.

The central argument was straightforward:

Iran survived. That changed everything.

The biggest mistake is focusing on who won individual battles. The strategic fact is that Iran survived the war—and once it survived, the balance of power in the Middle East began to shift.

The conventional wisdom says this war is ending. The evidence points in the opposite direction. The incentives for renewed escalation are actually growing because the outcome left all sides dissatisfied.

This week's events fit that logic almost perfectly.

What to Watch Next
The biggest mistake media, investors and policymakers can make is watching oil prices instead of geopolitical incentives.

The critical question is whether commercial confidence continues to deteriorate, not just whether another ship is attacked.

Watch four indicators closely over the coming days:

Commercial shipping traffic into Hormuz. Fewer inbound vessels signal declining confidence regardless of whether the waterway technically remains open. They have already fallen by about half since Thursday.

Maritime security advisories and insurance premiums. Rising war-risk premiums often precede larger economic effects.

Israeli-Lebanese tensions. Further escalation along the northern border could quickly spill back into Gulf security.

The memorandum itself. Every action that tests its boundaries increases the probability of a broader breakdown.

The Iran War is no longer just a military conflict. It is becoming the first test of a new Middle Eastern balance of power.
