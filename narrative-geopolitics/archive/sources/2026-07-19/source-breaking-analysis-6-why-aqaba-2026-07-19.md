Breaking Analysis #6: Why Aqaba?
July 19 BLUF
Prof Robert Pape
Jul 19, 2026

Breaking Analysis is a new Escalation Trap series providing immediate assessments of major developments—not simply explaining what happened, but identifying the strategic logic that points to what is likely to happen next.

July 19 BLUF

Although no direct Iranian attack on the city of Aqaba (Jordan), the U.S. Embassy in Amman issued an emergency security alert advising Americans to avoid Aqaba’s international airport and seaport due to a specific and credible threat. The security advisory follows a wider escalation in the region. Recent Iranian missile and drone attacks targeting U.S. forces in Jordan—which resulted in the deaths of American service members—have led to retaliatory U.S. strikes on Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) coastal facilities.

Update as I am about to push send: Air raid sirons are going off in Jordan, warning that Iran missiles are incoming against Aqaba.

Aqaba is Jordan’s only coastal city and serves as a major resort destination. Located on the northern tip of the Red Sea, it functions simultaneously as a luxury tourist getaway, a deep-water commercial port, and a gateway to Jordan’s desert attractions.

The threat to Aqaba widens the war, but why? As a new threat to luxury tourism, simple incremental expansion of infrastructure targeting, or something else?

My assessment is below.

Why Aqaba?
Although multiple reasons may be at work, the threat to Aqaba and especially the main transportation hub for business executives and seaport come on the heels of last week’s meeting between Iraq’s PM and Jordan’s foreign minister in Washington. Reportedly, the purpose was to speed up and break ground for the long-delayed Basra-Aqaba pipeline, which runs 1,600 km from southern Iraq to Jordan’s Red Sea coast, built to carry 2.25 million barrels a day for $18 billion.

Why now?
The Iran war cut southern Iraqi exports roughly 70%, exposing Baghdad’s dependence on Hormuz. Jordan gains transit fees and 150,000 barrels per day for its Zarqa refinery. For Iraq, this is about security not profit. Aqaba costs about $6 a barrel versus 60 cents through the Gulf.

Why this deepens the Escalation Trap
For Iran, the Basra-Aqaba pipleline would undercut Iran’s vision of a “security resistance belt” stretching from the Mediterranean through the Red Sea to the Gulf—a network intended to deny rivals exactly these kinds of permanent energy bypasses and preserve Tehran’s long-term leverage over global oil flows.

If that is Tehran’s strategic objective, the implications are clear.

Under the Escalation Trap framework, Iran has powerful incentives to widen the conflict beyond Hormuz itself and strike any infrastructure that could permanently reduce its coercive leverage.

A functioning Basra–Aqaba pipeline would not simply bypass one waterway; it would weaken Iran’s long-term ability to threaten global energy flows. That makes projects such as Aqaba increasingly attractive military targets—not because of their immediate economic value, but because they challenge Iran’s broader strategy of preserving leverage through control of regional energy chokepoints.

This elevates the risk to anyone involved in these projects and associated locations in the region.
