If this story is true — and it seems so bizarre to me that I did wonder about it — then it merely confirms my point that those whom the gods destroy they first make mad.

There is no sign so far of the Europeans preparing themselves for this energy shock.

The Russians, for their part, are now increasingly talking about finding new suppliers and apparently they have doubled their supply of oil to India, though even that does not make up for India's shortfalls.

President Zelensky and the Ukrainians continue to launch attacks on Russian energy facilities. There is no word of criticism about this in Europe. There are scant words of criticism about it in the United States either. One leader, one NATO leader who is becoming angry is President Erdogan of Turkey — until very recently a staunch ally of Ukraine and a sometime friend of President Zelensky. Erdogan has had a lengthy telephone call with President Putin.

I read the Russian readout. There was a thorough exchange of views on the ongoing escalation of the military and political confrontation in the Persian Gulf. It was noted that the heavy hostilities there are leading to severe consequences for energy, trade, logistics and other sectors not just regionally but globally as well.

The leaders — that is to say Putin and Erdogan — noted that they share the stance in favour of achieving a ceasefire as soon as possible and working towards compromised peace agreements that would take into account the legitimate interests of all the regional parties.

In a recent programme I discussed the importance of these words that are now appearing regularly in Russian readouts with Middle East leaders — specifically in the case of my programme in the conversation between Putin and MBS, the Crown Prince of Saudi Arabia — about the importance of taking into account the legitimate interests of all the regional states, which of course includes Iran.

But then even more interestingly, the readout continues: When discussing the situation surrounding Ukraine, Vladimir Putin thanked Erdogan for his unfaltering willingness to facilitate the relevant negotiations. In light of the Kiev regime's attempts to target transportation infrastructure linking Russia and Turkey as well as commercial ships in the Black Sea, the leaders emphasised the importance of taking coordinated measures aimed at ensuring all-round security in and around the Black Sea.

In other words, the Turks and the Russians are going to work together in the Black Sea against the Ukrainians. Erdogan has been friendly to Ukraine. Turkey has sold weapons to Ukraine. Erdogan has spoken of his support for Ukraine one day joining NATO. But he has now been so angered by Ukrainian attacks on shipping in the Black Sea and the way in which this potentially disrupts energy cooperation between Turkey and Russia, and he has been so infuriated by Ukrainian actions threatening the integrity of the undersea TurkStream pipeline, that he is now publicly signalling that the Turkish and Russian navies are prepared to work together to ensure security against the Ukrainians in the Black Sea.

That is Erdogan taking steps, in the midst of this energy crisis in which Turkey by the way is seriously affected, to protect Turkish interests. He does that even if so far these are rhetorical rather than actual statements. I haven't seen any actual Turkish warships coming out to patrol the Black Sea together with the Russian warships, though that may come. Erdogan does sometimes follow through on statements like this. At least he is talking about doing something other than set up a working group.

The Europeans so far have managed exactly nothing.

That of course brings me back to the topic of Ukraine.

Zelensky again talks about an energy truce with the Russians. I suspect that many of the drone attacks that we are hearing about by the Ukrainians against Russia are intended to build up the idea of an energy truce — to suggest that there is some degree of reciprocity between Ukraine and Russia on this score. I have said many times that given the number of drones that the Ukrainians are using for these attacks on Russia, the amount of damage they are actually doing appears to be minimal. The same is not true at all of Russian attacks against the Ukrainian energy and infrastructure systems.

The Russians have rejected the idea of an Easter energy truce. In fact, even as I am making this programme, there are reports that another big Oreshnik strike is being prepared. Two Oreshniks are going to be used plus a third new type of missile. This may be the missile I have heard about before which has some kind of fuel-air explosive warhead. I am not going to speculate further about it. I am going to guess that the targets will be electricity substations and other plants of that kind. We shall see, and we will see if this attack even takes place.

In the meantime, Events in Ukraine has done a massive, very interesting piece about the use of drones by each side on the battlefields. He reiterates points I have made in many programmes: that the Ukrainians mostly use their drones to try to hit the Russians on the actual battlefield itself where the Ukrainians and the Russians are in fact fighting each other, whereas the Russians tend to use their drones more to interrupt Ukrainian logistics — to stop supplies reaching the troops on the front lines. Clearly the blogger Events in Ukraine thinks that the Russian approach is better. I have no doubt that that is right.

I should say that in my opinion using drones against infantry — something which apparently Zelensky has insisted upon — is very much part of his insistence that Ukraine inflict as heavy casualties on Russia as possible. The objective supposedly in the autumn was to kill more Russians on the front lines than the Russians could replace them with — something which, together with the payments Ukrainian drone operators receive for every Russian they claim to have killed, is going to inflate inevitably the numbers of killed Russian infantry that the Ukrainians are reporting.

I have discussed this before. But anyway, it seems to me that the people who are most likely to be trained and to know how to deal with drones by this point in the war are going to be the experienced and well-trained infantry that the Russians now have on the front lines. Obviously they will suffer casualties, but by this point they will have learned how to avoid the drones and to mitigate against their effectiveness. Whereas the vehicles that bring the supplies from the rear to the Ukrainians won't have those abilities or those skills.

Events in Ukraine also says that the Ukrainians anyway don't have the reach to use drones in the way that the Russians do because they have significantly fewer drones than the Russians have and far fewer operators than the Russians do. The Russians supposedly now have 100,000 well-trained drone operators. Events in Ukraine doesn't give a figure for the Ukrainians, but my sense is that it is probably around half the number of the drone operators that the Russians have. Just saying.

I would add something else. I suspect that by now for the Ukrainians to use drones in the way that the Russians do — to try to stop supplies from reaching the front lines — is going to run into opposition and resistance from the Ukrainian drone operator community because it will stop the payments that they receive for the Russian soldiers they claim to have killed. The commanders of these Ukrainian drone units, who receive decorations and rewards based on the numbers of Russian soldiers they say they have killed, are probably also going to resist being redirected towards dealing with Russian logistics in that way.

But logistics, as Events in Ukraine says, do matter on the battlefields.

By chance yesterday I read a report that Ukrainian logistics in Konstantinovka have now collapsed — that the Russians, who have had drone dominance over Konstantinovka for some time, have now basically choked off the movement of supplies entirely to the Ukrainian garrison there. The Ukrainians, who are already being pushed out of the southwestern part of Konstantinovka, now face a major operational crisis. The overall impression is that Ukrainian resistance in Konstantinovka is now on the brink finally of full collapse.

The same is true of Ukrainian resistance in the Rodinska mine area to the west of Rodinska, which will in turn lead apparently to the collapse not just of Ukrainian resistance in this mine but to the collapse of Ukrainian resistance in the town of Belitskaya further to the north.

In Zaporizhzhia region it looks as if the Russians are now about to storm the village of Hulyaipole. I discussed how they had reached it in my programme yesterday. They have now reached Ray-Alexandrovka — the small town on the hill which overlooks Slavyansk and Kramatorsk which has been a major objective of the Russian advance westwards from Chasov Yar, the town that the Russians captured some weeks ago.

Logistics matter. The pattern of the war continues as before. Once Hulyaipole, Konstantinovka, Ray-Alexandrovka and Lyman by the way all fall — which I suspect they all will one after the other fairly quickly, as we often see in this war — then at that point it will be legitimate to say that we are looking at the start of Russia's spring offensive.

I checked back to the day when I thought the Russian spring offensive last year began. It was interestingly on the 7th of April 2025. That is the same date today. We hear rumours of Oreshnik being about to be launched. Maybe the offensive, the real Russian offensive, is now about to start.
