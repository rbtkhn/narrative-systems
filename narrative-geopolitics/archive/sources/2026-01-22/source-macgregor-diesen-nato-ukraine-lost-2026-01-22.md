# Douglas Macgregor × Glenn Diesen — Why NATO is Finished & the Ukraine War Was Lost (operator transcript)

**Interview title (operator / YouTube):** Douglas Macgregor: Why NATO is Finished & the Ukraine War Was Lost  
**Aired / published:** January 22, 2026

**Speakers:**

- **Colonel Douglas Macgregor** (guest)

---

**Glenn Diesen:** Welcome back. We are joined today by Colonel Douglas Macgregor, a combat veteran, author, and former adviser to the US Secretary of Defense. Thank you again for coming back on.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Sure. Happy to be here.

**Glenn Diesen:** We do see that transatlantic relations are fragmenting. This derives from the dispute over Greenland but also the pending defeat of Ukraine, or NATO’s defeat in Ukraine.

Four years ago when the Russians went into Ukraine, there seemed to be a consensus in the media and by the politicians that NATO would now be stronger than ever given that we face this Russian threat. With the exception of you though — within the first weeks I think you said something along the line that this would end with NATO’s possible collapse.

I was wondering how you could see this coming, because no one’s denying the fragmentation anymore.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Actually, I told — I think it was Dmitri Simes or George Beebe, can’t remember which one — in an interview with The National Interest in January of 2022, before the Russians went in, two things.

First, the Russians would very definitely intervene militarily. A lot of people in Washington and in Europe all said, “Oh no, the Russians will never do this. It would destroy their economy and the regime can’t withstand that. No, the Russians will never go into Ukraine. They will not do this.”

I said yes they will. Then I said that if they do, NATO will not survive the crisis.

Why did I say that? I think because a substantial portion of my career involved service in the US military inside the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, and I think that’s important to understand.

I was there for the first time. I came in in March of 1977 and my assignment for most of the time that I was there — until 1980 — was service on the border with Czechoslovakia and East Germany. I had a lot of interaction with many people. I had a chance to see the Warsaw Pact forces and I concluded three things.

First of all, the Warsaw Pact was not going to attack. The Soviets were not going to risk a war. They were effectively defending what they had gained as a result of the collapse of German military power at the end of the Second World War.

Secondly, NATO was a conglomeration of different national military establishments and this is not conducive to success on the battlefield. In most cases the smaller nations — the Dutch, the Belgians, the Canadians, the British and ultimately the French — were limited-liability partners. They were there because they saw it as being in their interest to be there, but they really weren’t prepared to fight anybody and their focus was extraterritorial. This is particularly true for the French and the British, the Belgians and the Dutch because they were former colonial powers and they all had interests overseas.

That was my first tour. I worked closely with German military units and I concluded that the Germans were the only ones that were adequately equipped and trained to fight effectively against the Soviets if anything happened.

Then I spent time in graduate school in Munich and in Vienna in 1983 and 1984 while writing my master’s. I went to those places to talk to members of the Mutual Balanced Force Reduction talks, both Soviet and non-Soviet as well as the US side. Again I reached very similar conclusions about what I thought was realistic.

In 1986, I was assigned briefly for about six weeks to an office in the Pentagon that used to be called Net Assessment. It was a very useful assignment because I was able to read translated works from the Soviet General Staff Journal which were extraordinarily highly classified at the time. These were very informative insofar as the Soviet understanding of us and our understanding of them. They pointed out that we had performed very poorly during the Second World War — the Anglo-British alliance — because we managed the forces as though they were separate countries and there were limited authorities exercised by Eisenhower and others whenever they commanded Canadian, French, non-American forces. They basically said you can’t run a war this way. If you fight somebody as competent as the Germans, you have no chance of success at all.

The bottom line is I went back then as a major. I served again on the border briefly and then I went to Supreme Headquarters Allied Powers Europe and I was there from November 1997 until January 2000. What I saw was evidence for the same problem. Everyone had their own agenda. They only temporarily or briefly cooperated and there had to be an incentive for them to cooperate. There had to be some payoff to get people to join together to do much of anything. The exercises that we did conduct were not very realistic.

When this happened a couple of things were very obvious to me. Russia was now a Russian state which meant they had unity of command, unity of effort. Everybody spoke the same language. There were no disputes about who was going to command what. NATO remained exactly what it was — a chorus of competing voices that couldn’t agree on much of anything.

I didn’t see much evidence that regardless of what Ukraine did that it could outlast the Russians. Ukraine wasn’t large enough, didn’t have the scientific industrial base, didn’t have the manpower. We knew almost from the outset that the Ukrainian population was diminishing in size not just for reasons of fecundity or the lack thereof but because Ukrainians didn’t want to fight in the war so large numbers of people left. Then of course you have the corruption issues.

I don’t think it was — I know a lot of people say “Oh, well, how could you possibly have figured that out?” — but if anybody looked at any of the things I’ve described objectively, it was hard not to reach that conclusion.

The one thing that I did not anticipate was that the Russians would move as cautiously and slowly at the beginning as they did. I didn’t understand the true state of affairs with the Russian army. The Russian army was designed for territorial defense, nothing else. That’s why all these claims of Putin’s determination to rebuild the Soviet empire — it’s all nonsense. Always was.

Today they have an army that is second to none in my judgment in many respects. But at the time it wasn’t up to the task that it was assigned. It took a year, year and a half to really build up that force to what you see today.

Everyone else — and this is true in Washington as well as in the capitals of Europe, Glenn — everyone suffers from a terminal case of wishful thinking. They fully believe in all the nonsense that is now crashing and burning so badly over in Davos and also in all of Europe’s capitals about this thing we call NATO and the European Union.

The wishful thinking is definitely what’s bringing down Europe.

What I think was interesting in the 1990s is a lot of people knew that NATO expansion would create these problems. You had people who opposed it — George Kennan or the US Secretary of Defense William Perry — but you also had people like Madeleine Albright who recognized that Russia would feel encircled and this could alienate the Russians but she still supported it with the logic that if we end up in a conflict with Russia, at least then NATO will be there — a huge, much larger, more powerful NATO.

When we now end up in this war — or proxy war, direct war, however one wants to define it — with Russia, the Europeans seem to think well we can now retreat into a new cold war. That is, Europeans and America essentially go back to the past and we do the same thing over again. The political leadership as well as the political commentary can’t seem to understand why America’s not joining in on this. Why aren’t you joining us for another cold war?

A couple of things we need to admit up front. If you look at Stoltenberg or his successor Rutte, there is no reward in Europe right now for men and women of character and intelligence. You are rewarded for mouthing the same stupidities over and over again, repeating aphorisms that make no sense, maintaining the fiction that things are better than they are or that there are capabilities on hand that don’t exist.

You’re not going to be rewarded for standing up and saying “Well, this is ridiculous nonsense.”

The argument that I made from the beginning — and there were a few other people — is that we have an interest in ending this war in Ukraine as soon as possible. This is going to be a terrible tragedy for Eastern Europe and ultimately for Europe in general if we’re not careful. The longer this lasts, the greater the probability that something really bad could happen.

Of course, at that point, I did not appreciate the extent to which Putin would exercise restraint, which he has remarkably. That’s not something that has made him popular with certain people in Russia. There are a lot of Russians who would like to see NATO taught a very valuable lesson on a permanent basis.

You’re asking now, well, what happened with the United States? President Trump is not someone who is steeped in research and understanding of anything. But President Trump, if he sticks with them, has pretty good instincts. His instincts told him — this is back during his first term — what are we doing in Europe? It’s been 75 years. What are we doing there? He’s right. What are we still in Korea 70 years later? This is crazy. This all ought to end.

To that extent, he shared President Eisenhower’s view that what we should be interested in is a Europe without us. Remember Eisenhower said, “If we are still in Europe as a result of this NATO alliance in 10 years, well then we failed because the goal should be to get out.” That was a traditional American view and a very healthy one, I would add.

But it was also a view that the permanent hostility with the Soviet Union at that point was not an answer to the problem. We had to come up with a way of interacting with the Soviets whether we like the way they governed or not. I think that President Trump early on — his view was why are we in this sort of position of permanent hostility to Russia? So that was always there.

Unfortunately once he got in he was surrounded by the usual permanent residents of the ring around Washington we call the Beltway. “Oh you can’t do that. You can’t just leave. You can’t go here. You can’t exchange this.” Pretty soon if you listen to everybody in Washington the one message that comes through over and over again is you can’t change anything because if you change something you disrupt the money flow.

All the think tanks that you have inside the Beltway are essentially resting on a foundation of permanent hostility to Russia, permanent hostility to China, permanent hostility to Iran, and absolute unconditional support for whatever Israel wants. That’s the standard. You have to look at who their donors are, who provides them with money, and suddenly it’s pretty obvious that the people pouring hundreds of millions of dollars into these places are absolutely adamantly opposed to any change that would end our position in all of these regions, change our position globally.

But President Trump has never completely given up on it. He just doesn’t know how to do it. He has surrounded himself with people who frankly are not much more knowledgeable than he is about the world. But they all know that they too have an interest in ensuring the money flow doesn’t stop from which they also benefit.

That’s the problem when you surround yourself with billionaires.

**Glenn Diesen:** I think that in Europe they still subscribe to the idea from the early 1990s of hegemonic peace which will be delivered by the NATO alliance primarily and again built into the security strategy. But I don’t think they appreciate that there’s a new political force growing out in America in response to the fact that this position has exhausted the US to a large extent. It’s introduced a lot of unhelpful globalist or cosmopolitan ideas and it alienates other great powers.

They keep thinking that the only reason why the United States doesn’t want to join in and continue what we’ve done over the past 80 years is that Trump doesn’t appreciate democracy. I just think the wider development, the change in the distribution of power, this doesn’t seem to factor in though.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Keep in mind that if you’re President Trump, you see yourself as the contemporary equivalent of the Roman Emperor, say about the year 200 AD, and you’re talking about a group of states in Europe that are analogous to the Greek city-states.

What were the Greek city-states within the broader framework of the Roman Empire? They were a source of admiration. The Romans admired and respected Hellenic culture. They thought the Greeks had created effectively the foundations for their civilization. The whole Eastern Roman Empire spoke Greek and everybody who sat down to write anything all the way up to and including Marcus Aurelius in the second century is writing in Greek, not Latin. Caesar was criticized for writing his treatise on Gaul in Latin because they said if you were an educated man you’d have written that in Greek.

Stop and think about that. I think that’s the way we view Europe. Or at least I was brought up to think in those terms. I thought of Europe as the wellspring of our civilization. If water is purest at its source, then Europe is the waterhead or fountainhead of our civilization.

But at the same time, we’re Romans. We’re magnitudes larger and more powerful than the European states. There’s been a tendency to see them as a group that needs to be dragged along in the directions that we want to go.

This is what’s really harmed NATO. We weaponized it. We used the Europeans. We instrumentalized it so that we would drag Europeans into places like Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, Libya. It’s all about what are they going to do for us? The European states ensured that they would become increasingly weaker over time by dismantling their military power. They said “Well, it’s in our interest to make the Americans happy and be their friends and supporters,” which they did. But they forgot that in the meantime the United States was growing ever more powerful with broader interests. Certainly interests reach far beyond Europe.

All of that results in what you’ve got today. President Trump goes over there and he says “This is what I want.” Well, if you don’t want that, then frankly I don’t need you. I’ll do whatever I want anyway.

NATO is also one of the components of that structure that I mentioned earlier. Inside the Beltway you have the Atlantic Council. Everybody worships NATO at the Atlantic Council. This is the totem. We sacrifice to it, we love it, we pray to it and it’s ours and it brings us lots of money. They’re all over Washington DC.

Change is very frightening to these people. If we don’t have NATO, if we’re not doing what we’ve always done, then what have we got?

The problem with Donald Trump though is that he has no strategy. There is no national military strategy. They call it a national security strategy. You read through that thing. It’s just an expression of hope and wishes. We’d really like to get along with China and we don’t want to really fight Russia and on and on, but there’s no there there. There is no framework for execution. There are no guideposts into the future. There are no systematic means of changing the way we do business because nobody really wants change.

When you have no strategy, any road will take you to your destination. We’ve had two destinations: Venezuela and Greenland, neither of which directly impinge on our interests in other areas. Although you could argue Greenland belongs to Denmark. Yes, that’s an issue. But who are the Danes? How many people live in Denmark? You’re back to this age-old problem in Europe.

When Napoleon was approached and told the Pope won’t like what you’re doing, he turned around and said “How many battalions has the Pope got?” Shut up. Get out of my way. It’s that sort of arrogance right now. It’s quite real and it’s unattractive, but it’s real because it’s truthful.

This is something I don’t know if we discussed this before, but I’ve told people repeatedly: the great tragedy in Europe over many centuries is that you did not coalesce many of these tribal communities into a larger entity.

We’re talking about Denmark. Very few people realize that after 1866 the king of Denmark actually approached Bismarck and asked him whether or not Denmark could join the North German Confederation. Today Danes would say “Oh, that’s impossible. That would never happen.” No, it happened. Bismarck sat down with the king of Prussia. The king of Prussia said “Well, look, we’ve got 2 million Poles right now, or almost that number. We don’t want anybody else inside the country that’s going to raise hell with us because we’re ultimately Germans. We speak German.”

He said “Everybody likes the Danes. I like the Danes.” Moltke, the great chief of the Prussian General Staff, was a Dane by origin. Everybody likes the Danes, but the Danes, they’ll want to have their own holidays. They’ll have their own little traditions. They’ll insist on speaking Old Norse, you know, this kind of thing. They turned the Danes down.

If history had progressed along the lines that it has elsewhere, all of Scandinavia would have been united with Germany at some point, just as Austria was brought in. We have all these little tribal groupings now. We have the Czech tribe. We have the Slovak tribe. How do you run a continent that way where you’re treating little tribes or larger family groups as sovereign individual entities?

I think in Washington — Trump may not understand this — but that’s a source of frustration for him. He says “What the hell am I bothering with these people for?” I’m not saying that’s right or wrong, but what I’m trying to point to are some realities.

There was someone in the Danish Parliament when I was at SHAPE — I think this was 1998 — who actually put forward a recommendation that the Danes should start speaking English as a first language. Of course that was voted down. But his argument was who’s going to speak what we speak? We might as well come to terms with English. English is modern Germanic. We dropped the vow problem. We eliminated the endings. We don’t have any more definite articles. We don’t have to go through all the grammatical mess.

Europe is a very strange place. Almost everywhere else in the world these small pockets have been subsumed into larger entities. I think that will eventually happen whether the people in Europe like it or not.

There was a solution. We did have a solution before 1914. It was not perfect but it worked. You had the Hapsburgs, the Romanovs, the Hohenzollerns. We Americans intervened and we saw to it that was dismantled. What do you have? You’ve had the legacy of multiple world wars, a great deal of confusion, no coherence.

All of this combines to create frustration at high levels in the American political elite to the point where they say “Well, you know, why bother?” That’s the problem.

I think Donald Trump is settled right now for some sort of compromise with Greenland. It’s meaningless in large part and I wouldn’t have bothered with it anyway. But I think it was suggested, you know, why don’t the Danes take the money and run — 700 million euros or something? That would go a long way for Denmark.

I’m not saying that’s the right answer. What I’m saying is that there are different ways to look at this problem, but I don’t think people understand that when Trump looks at it, he doesn’t understand what the problem is. He just sees a piece of real estate that is close to the United States and is within the framework of his security zone, like it or not.

You’ve heard these people all the time. They say “We don’t want any more balance of power politics. It’s wrong. There are no spheres of influence.” Okay, great. Fine. Thanks very much. Completely out of touch with reality. We know certain states live within the sphere of influence of the larger states. It’s a fact.

On the one hand you have Europe that wants things in a way that are not sustainable and you have the United States led by Trump who goes off on a tangent about Greenland.

I think we’ve learned our lesson already in Venezuela. I think we kidnapped these people after pouring millions of dollars in to buy everybody off and now we’ve discovered gosh, you know, it’s a big country. We can’t occupy this place. If we try to supplant the government, it’ll never work. So we said, well, I guess we’ll live with this government. We just want to cut a deal with them that’s beneficial to us.

I don’t know how long that will all last, but again, there’s no strategy. None.

**Glenn Diesen:** What do you want to do this week, boss? I mean, it’s almost that bad.

In terms of Greenland though, because of course NATO is already fragmenting a bit. I think people unfairly put it all on Trump’s shoulders. But again, it was people forget that it was Obama back in 2016 who said we have to pivot to Asia. If you’re going to pivot to Asia, you have to pivot away from somewhere. This new distribution of power and the priorities of the US was already shifting.

**Douglas Macgregor:** Yeah, except there’s been no pivot to Asia. That’s very important, Glenn. Because Obama’s argument — everybody said “Oh yeah, that’s right. That’s a great idea.” Then the bill was presented. Hundreds of billions of dollars that would have to be invested all over Asia in foreign bases and installations, not just for protection, but for dramatic modernization and expansion, assuming that you wanted to wage war against China. That was the underlying point.

People began saying “Well, we’re going to end up at war with China.” Sort of the Mearsheimer argument. It’s a big place. We’re going to have to go to war with it at some point. I disagree with that completely. I think increasingly it’s obvious to everyone that makes no sense.

Having said that, I think President Trump more than Obama ever did knows that he’s not going to start a war with China. That doesn’t mean that people under him aren’t going to try. There are plenty of people in his administration and over in the Department of Defense and the CIA who given the opportunity would create hostility with China unnecessarily.

The biggest joke that you can tell anybody is we have to have the US Navy go through the Strait of Malacca or the Strait of Hormuz or the Suez Canal. These are choke points. If we don’t keep them open, the Chinese could stop that. There’s no evidence for that. In fact, the evidence is the opposite. The Chinese have never stopped a commercial vessel. They have every interest in ensuring those waterways are free and open. Who has stopped, boarded, and seized a commercial vessel? We have.

I’m one of these people that thinks that President Trump understands very clearly there’s no benefit to us from going to war with China at all.

So where is he focused? He’s back to Europe. He continues to pulverize Russia with sanctions. He continues to double down stupidly by sending money to Ukraine. So I guess he’s essentially surrendered to the Russia war hawks who want to maintain that permanent hostility and of course he surrenders to Israel that wants the destruction of Iran.

**Glenn Diesen:** What do you expect though coming from this meeting now? Because Witkoff — well I think they already touched ground there in Moscow now. I might be mistaken but I think so. The mood in Moscow has shifted a bit from what I understand that nobody really believes in any of these diplomatic efforts. Well there might be something as the situation gets worse in Ukraine. But there’s also this concern that this incremental worsening of the situation between the US and Russia — that is if you look back in May I think of 2024 when there was this attack on Russia’s early nuclear warning radars and then last year in June there was this attack on the nuclear deterrent of Russia and now they argue that this assassination attempt of Putin — that there’s also nuclear command center nearby. This seems to be an idea that the US is contributing in escalating in a big way. The mood has soured a lot in Moscow.

Again, I was wondering how you see what might come out of these talks?

**Douglas Macgregor:** I think President Putin and Foreign Secretary Lavrov will as always be polite and they will listen and nothing will happen.

Why will nothing happen? I think they understand that Ms. Bruce Witkoff and Kushner represent international financial interests in New York City and London and they’re not going to overlook that truth when they deal with them. They also have long ago concluded that while President Trump may mean well and have good intentions, there’s not a whole hell of a lot of evidence that he can follow through on anything.

I don’t think they’re prepared to sign on for any of the proposals that undoubtedly the two gentlemen will bring with them. I think again that Putin will say first of all where’s the evidence that you have recognized our legitimate national security interests. Now it may be that in private President Trump has done that but he certainly has never done that publicly and that’s something that’s very important to the Russians. They want their legitimate national security interests recognized and respected.

The second thing is they’re going to wonder about his ability once again to follow through on anything that he signs up for.

Lastly, they’ve now concluded that he really doesn’t command NATO. Their assumption from the beginning was that the president of the United States was really in charge of NATO, the de facto leader. I think they were expecting an Eisenhower or a Kennedy who would step forward and say “No, stop. I’m in charge. Get back to where you belong. We are not going to war with Russia over Ukraine.” I think that’s what they really thought would probably happen. They got an education and now they would be very grateful if Trump did that. But there’s not much evidence that Trump can deliver on NATO.

They sit there and say well is he going to restrain Berlin, Paris and London who are the principal culprits? He’s also got problems in Warsaw, but those are the big culprits. Are you going to be able to restrain them? Are you going to be able to deliver on anything there?

The Russians are so angry over that they’re talking about potential use of nuclear weapons if they’re challenged by the British in particular.

Then you have more recently Zelensky who within the last 24 hours says that his goal as president of Ukraine, his Ukrainian national goal is to kill 50,000 Russians a week or something. That’s insane. How do you make peace with a government that makes a statement like that? Well, you can make peace with them when they’re on their knees and about to be executed for war crimes. That’s all that you can do.

I don’t think anything of substance will emerge from this meeting.

Once again, let me circle back to President Trump. That does not mean that President Trump is not sincere. It doesn’t mean that his intentions aren’t true. They are. Listen, I was around him and I know he doesn’t want a war with Russia. I know he doesn’t want a war with China. I know he’s not very excited about a war with Iran. That’s not him.

But he’s a prisoner of forces and circumstances, to be frank.

**Glenn Diesen:** This is an interesting issue though. What are the forces then? Or where does the power reside? Because I get the same impression even though he sits on the throne, it’s obviously he’s not able to carry through everything he wants. Is it weakness or is it that there’s a lot of power which resides in structures outside his control?

**Douglas Macgregor:** I don’t really want to go into too much detail in answer to that question. That would offend large numbers of people and it would just make life for me that much more miserable in the public eye than it already is in the mainstream media. The truth is not popular here.

But let’s simply say that most of the same people and forces that want war with Iran are also absolutely committed to continuous war with Russia. There isn’t much difference. You can go to the Hill and listen to these people. Whether it’s Ron Johnson, who a lot of people want to consider a somewhat reasonable man, where does he stand? Well, he’s in the war-hawk category. So is Lindsey Graham. So is Ted Cruz. Follow the money. So is Chuck Schumer.

Who funds them, who supports them, who elects them, who enriches them. I think we have to understand in the current environment that that’s the way you evaluate Congress. Trace that money back to its sources, then you’ll find out what the truth is.

You can also look at the people that surround and advise the president. Who are they? Who do they represent? It certainly isn’t the America First administration for which I and millions of others voted. We voted for an administration who would say “Look, we’re preeminently interested in what happens here, not what goes on over there” — to recognize that whatever happens overseas, it shrinks to insignificance next to what happens inside the United States.

That’s why we voted for it. We want him to succeed. I want him to succeed. I’m much more concerned about the enforcement of federal law inside the United States, which is vital to our survival as a nation, than I am about what happens on the ground in Syria, Iraq, Iran, Israel, Jordan, or anywhere else in the Middle East. I could care less about that because we’re in a position right now, if we’re not careful, we could lose everything here at home. If it gets very much worse than it already is, the potential for civil unrest — because it’s very well organized, it’s very well funded — against our government and against the American people.

If that succeeds to a large extent, and we end up in a very serious armed conflict, at least a low-intensity armed conflict, it won’t make any difference to anybody here in the United States what happens overseas. We just won’t care. I wish President Trump would focus there and understand that.

**Glenn Diesen:** I guess there should be more focus on domestic issues. I also worry about an economic crisis now looming which would create a lot more unpredictability.

As I think about Witkoff heading now to Moscow or arrived in Moscow — I would be critical or pessimistic about the willingness to present a deal which would be acceptable to Russia. But on the other hand it seems that we’re moving towards a perfect storm though. The front lines are cracking. The Russians appear to be semi-locking Odessa by striking ships, ports, bridges, and supporting infrastructure. The lights are going off. I think Klitschko, the mayor of Kiev, said that about 600,000 people had left Kiev only in January, which is a huge percentage of the total population.

Overall, with the political oligarchs turning on each other as well, this seems to be brewing a perfect storm here. The Russians are escalating in a big way with the missiles and the drones. Do you see this being possibly going on for much longer? Because if I was Zelensky, I would think this can’t play out well. Let’s strike any deal today.

**Douglas Macgregor:** You’re familiar with the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk and you’re well aware that in 1917 there was first the Kerensky revolution followed by the Bolshevik revolution and it was very obvious that Russia had lost its position in the First World War and they had lost territory as well.

The Germans really didn’t occupy that much. They occupied the Baltic littoral. They occupied most of Poland and parts of Ukraine and to the south the Austrians had taken but really that was it. People then sat on the defensive and the Russians expended themselves and they lost terribly as a result.

During the discussions at Brest-Litovsk with the likes of Radek and other Bolsheviks it became very clear that they were stalling and they were trying to negotiate. The German in charge of it was a Major General Hoffman and he called back to Berlin and he spoke to Ludendorff and he said “These people have lost the war. They have nothing to stop us. They’re wasting our time. We’re going to have to do something.”

Ludendorff said “Tell them that we will march on Moscow.” He gave orders to put a force of 400,000 German troops together and start them on the road to Moscow. The word got back immediately to Lenin. Lenin sent Trotsky to Brest-Litovsk and said “We’ll sign whatever you want.” That was the end of the war and they surrendered huge tracts of territory. Most of what you see today in this Ukraine — certainly west of the Dnieper River, half of White Russia and all the Baltic littoral — was all signed over to the Germans. End of discussion. War ended.

What’s the lesson? I don’t want to lecture Mr. Putin. He’s a bright, thoughtful man. I don’t want to give advice to the Foreign Secretary. He has to sort through what is in Russia’s interest. But I would say that until you are standing on the Dnieper River across from Kiev, until you move forces across the river at Zaporizhzhia on the bridge there and start moving towards Odessa, it’s not going to stop.

We had a similar experience in 1991. We called a halt to our advance. I was very upset at the time. I couldn’t see any reason why we would stop. I said “What’s changed? Is the government in Baghdad still there?” That was my question. We didn’t really know. Then suddenly we got word it’s all over. 100-hour war. We’re going to have a treaty. Blah blah blah. I couldn’t believe it.
