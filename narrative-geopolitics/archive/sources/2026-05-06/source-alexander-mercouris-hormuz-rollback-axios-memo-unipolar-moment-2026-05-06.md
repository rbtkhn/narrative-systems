# Mercouris on the failed Hormuz operation, rollback talks, the Axios memo, and the end of the unipolar moment

Good day. Today is Wednesday, 6th May, 2026. And before I proceed with this program, let me remind you again to hit the like button and to check your subscription to this channel if, once you've watched this program, you decide that you like what you've heard and you wish to indicate this fact.

Now, over the last couple of days, I have expressed my deep skepticism about the entire new American military operation supposedly intended to break Iranian control of the Strait of Hormuz and to guide - as opposed to escort - merchant ships, tankers, and other vessels through the Strait of Hormuz in defiance of the Iranians.

I said that it looked to me very much like an angry reaction by Donald Trump to the realization that his previous plan to suffocate the Iranians into capitulation by imposing a sea blockade on Iran had failed.

We were privileged yesterday to do a program with the brilliant US commentator Trita Parsi, which you can find on The Duran. Over the course of that program, it was pointed out that the entire assumption that the Iranian economy would be suffocated originated with one very hawkish neocon think tank in the United States. It was predicting, in effect, the collapse of the Iranian economy according to a timetable that in fact expired a few days ago - roughly at the time when President Donald Trump decided that he would launch or declare the launch of this operation in the Strait of Hormuz.

In my opinion, that is the entire and sole explanation for the events of the last few days.

We did see the US military go through the motions, I sense with deep reluctance on the part of the US military commanders who had been ordered by the president to break the stranglehold of the Strait of Hormuz. Anyway, they went through the motions of pretending that that was what they were going to do. So we had movements of US warships into the vicinity of the Strait of Hormuz. The Iranians fired various warning shots at them and it appears that the US pulled back.

There were stories floating around yesterday about two US Navy destroyers having transited through the Strait of Hormuz into the Persian Gulf. The Iranians categorically denied this claim and it seems on balance as if nothing like that did in fact happen.

There were claims that the Americans did successfully guide, as they put it, two tankers through the Strait of Hormuz, both flying US flags. Again, the Iranians categorically deny this. Supposedly the ship owner of one of these tankers has confirmed the successful transit. But it became very clear as yesterday was wearing on that the Iranians were again tightening their control of the Strait of Hormuz. Other ships that tried to transit through the Strait of Hormuz were encountering harassment and pressure from the Iranians, and by the afternoon yesterday the entire operation looked as if it had failed.

Once again in the Daily Telegraph there is an article by the former British naval officer Tom Sharpe who has described the naval geography of the Strait of Hormuz. He provides a fairly detailed and, as far as I can tell, accurate account of the events of yesterday. He speaks with the experience of a former naval officer, writing however in a fervidly pro-Israeli, neocon newspaper, the Daily Telegraph. But he describes the immense difficulty, complexity, and tremendous challenges of breaking Iranian control of the Strait of Hormuz.

He doesn't quite say it, but when you read his article, the conviction has to be there that with the forces available, it simply cannot be done. And that has been obvious to me and indeed to many commentators for several weeks now. It's been discussed by all sorts of people: Commodore Steve Graham of the former Royal Navy, a variety of military officers from the United States, Colonel McGregor, Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis, Jim Webb, people like that. They've all said that this is essentially impossible. So has the president's former director of counterterrorism who resigned over this affair and who is himself a special forces officer - I am of course referring to Joe Kent. They've all basically said that trying to do this thing with the forces available is impossible.

I pointed out in my program yesterday that in fact the naval forces the United States actually has in the region have reduced over the last few days as the USS Gerald R. Ford, the unhappy aircraft carrier that has been at sea for over 10 months now and whose crew are apparently deeply demoralized and which has suffered problems with its toilets and fires in its cleaning facilities and laundry - anyway, that warship, the Gerald Ford, is steaming back to the United States. And for the record, I assume it is doing so with its accompanying escorts as well. So it isn't just that the US is losing an aircraft carrier in the region, but there are probably fewer destroyers operating in the region also because some of them are returning to the United States with the Gerald Ford.

Realistically, there was little chance that this operation would succeed. There are reports in the British media this morning that one other important voice started to speak out against this whole operation and that was none other than MBS, the Crown Prince, Prime Minister, and de facto ruler of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, the United States' most important ally in the Gulf region.

MBS, according to many reports, was a strong advocate of the attack on Iran and lobbied for it before the 28th of February, doing so in conflict with his public statements which appeared instead to urge restraint. But anyway, it seems that he did lobby for the war on the assumption that American power would be overwhelming and would result in the rapid collapse of the Shia revolutionary government that holds power in Iran and which has been unpopular with the Gulf monarchies, including Saudi Arabia's, ever since it was established back in 1979.

MBS now knows better and he has been counseling Donald Trump to call the whole operation off. He knows that further escalation might lead to attacks on more Saudi oil facilities. It might bring in the Houthis. It might close access for Saudi oil exports through the Red Sea and that is not a situation that MBS can afford.

In the meantime, the Iranians, citing the breach of the ceasefire by the United States through these operations in the Persian Gulf, took advantage of it by intensifying their attacks on the UAE. Those attacks, by the way, continued late into the night yesterday and I believe early into this morning. Significant damage has now been done to more Emirati oil facilities and the Emiratis, in light of what happened next, must be feeling that they have been left hanging out to dry.

Late in the afternoon yesterday, US officials, looking at the military realities and aware of statements coming out of Russia but also China - where the Iranian foreign minister Abbas Araghchi has been meeting with Wang Yi, the Chinese foreign minister - began to speak of a rollback.

The US Defense Secretary, or more correctly War Secretary, Pete Hegseth said that the United States was not looking for a fight with Iran. Extraordinary words given that the United States attacked Iran on the 28th of February, killing Iran's Supreme Leader, a significant part of Iran's leadership, and a large number of school children on the first day. But anyway, never mind. That was what Pete Hegseth said. He said that the events that had taken place in the Persian Gulf over the previous few hours were, in the opinion of the United States, below the threshold that would justify speaking of the collapse of the ceasefire.

Then Marco Rubio came out and made a further statement saying that the major military operations carried out by the United States had already achieved their objectives. There was no need for further military operations. The period of armed conflict between Iran and the United States for that reason appeared to be over.

Shortly after, the President of the United States, Donald Trump, released a Truth Social post in which he said that following contacts with the Pakistani mediators, following proposals from the Iranians, the United States, out of its extraordinary sense of goodwill, had decided to pause the current operations to escort ships through the Strait of Hormuz. That whole operation had been put on pause and it looked as if some kind of a deal was likely to be done.

A few hours after that, Axios - an extremely well-informed journal in the United States which is said to have close contacts with the Israeli authorities (something which I cannot confirm but which I for the record believe to be true) - published what Axios says is the framework of a deal that is being discussed between the United States and Iran. It says that the US and Iran are closing in on a one-page memo to end the war. This is based on reports from our old friends, two US officials and two other sources briefed on the issue - four people in other words, altogether, all of whom of course remain anonymous.

It's a reasonable guess that these officials and these parties close to the matter are American and Israeli. I think it's inconceivable that Axios is getting any information from the Iranians. But anyway, what we are told is that this memorandum of agreement, which is all it would be - it would not be a final agreement - envisages a 30-day pause for negotiations leading to a final agreement.

The proposal is that Iranian restrictions on shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and the US naval blockade would be gradually lifted during this 30-day period. "Gradually lifted" is clearly wrong because when you read the rest of the Axios article it's clear that the sea blockade would be lifted immediately, and there are some greater uncertainties about what the Iranians would do. But it seems that the Iranians would allow shipping to pass through the Strait of Hormuz subject to conditions.

We're then told that there would be a moratorium by Iran on uranium enrichment that this might last 12 years or perhaps 15 and maybe even 20. That apparently, despite that moratorium, Iran would be able to enrich uranium to a level of 3.67%, though maybe that would be after it expires. Iran would enter into a commitment never to seek a nuclear weapon or to weaponize its enrichment activities. Iran would allow enhanced inspections and there would in turn be a gradual lifting of sanctions by the United States and a release of billions of dollars in Iranian funds that are frozen around the world. Some of these funds having been frozen, to my knowledge, as far back as the year when the Islamic Republic was established in 1979 in connection with the seizure of the US hostages in the US embassy by Iran in that year.

What this article in Axios does not say is that this does in fact actually look rather like an American-Iranian proposal that was made to the United States several days ago which President Trump himself rejected.

There are a few points about this proposal or at least this proposed deal which struck me immediately. For starters, there is nothing here about Iran's ballistic missile program. The Trump administration during his first term brought in the issue of restrictions on Iran's ballistic missile program at the time when the United States walked out of the JCPOA. The Biden administration insisted that Iran agree to restrictions on its ballistic missile program in return for the United States returning to the JCPOA. The Iranians rejected that categorically. There is no mention of this in this article by Axios and it looks as if this proposal does not appear in it.

Secondly, the suggestion that Iran conduct major changes to its foreign policy - that it basically cut off its links to its regional allies, proxies as the United States and Israel and the other western states, the European powers all call them: Hezbollah in Lebanon, the various militias in Iraq, the Houthis in Yemen - that demand which has been incessantly made and which was first made by the first Trump administration also when it pulled out of the JCPOA, that has also been abandoned.

The Trump administration, the first Trump administration, and more recently the president of the United States called for major changes to the domestic arrangements in Iran just before the start of the attack on Iran on the 28th of February. And for a time after the start of that attack, the United States was calling for the release of all prisoners in Iran, for all kinds of other changes to be made. The president of course spoke about the Iranian people seizing the opportunity presented by the American attack to seize control, to take back control of their country. In other words, to overthrow the current government. At one point, by the way, the president was demanding Iran's unconditional surrender. He also did the same thing during the 12-day war in June 2025.

Well, all of that has been abandoned. There is no mention of any of this here. That proposal appears to have gone. The talk from Israel about cutting off the head of the snake - that's gone. The briefing by the director of Mossad and Prime Minister Netanyahu to the president on the 11th of February, discussed at length by the New York Times, in which those two Israeli officials, backed by people within the administration and of course also by people like Senator Lindsey Graham, said that an attack on Iran would lead rapidly to regime change in Iran - well, that all seems to have been abandoned too.

So it's impossible to see the proposals set out here in this Axios article, if they are indeed reflecting the actual thinking of the administration, as anything other than a huge retreat from the goals and objectives the Trump administration set itself when it embarked on its various attempts to achieve regime change in Iran, starting with the June 2025 12-day war and continuing with the attack that it launched on the 28th of February thereafter.

I said before the start of the war - I said this in several places. We, Alex Christoforou and I, discussed this extensively on The Duran - that the entire project to achieve regime change in Iran depended on that regime change being achieved within the first few days, perhaps in the first week of the attack. That if it was not achieved within that timeframe then the situation would shift in Iran's favor.

I also speculated, though I have always said that I am not an expert on the internal situation in Iran, that it seemed to me that Western commentators were underestimating the level of support for the existing political system within Iran itself. That it always seemed to me that the political system inside Iran still retained a critical mass of support amongst the Iranian people and that if that was correct then the entire regime change project was doomed from the outset.

Well, it seems that some people in Washington, some people in the administration, perhaps the president himself have very belatedly and after presiding over a strategic debacle come reluctantly to that realization as well. And that's why we see these major concessions being made to the Iranians now.

No regime change, no internal reforms in Iran, no demands that Iran cut back on its ballistic missile program, no demands that Iran abandon its entire foreign policy in relation to the Middle East. All of that has been dropped and we are now only talking about nuclear enrichment.

Having said that, there are here concessions again, if this Axios article is to be believed, from the United States over the nuclear enrichment program as well. The United States prior to the start of the conflict insisted that Iran abandon nuclear enrichment entirely. It now accepts that Iran should be able to enrich uranium up to the level of 3.67% - the same level as permitted under the JCPOA - though it wants a grace period, a moratorium, during which over the course of which Iran should not enrich uranium further.
