# What Really Changed Yesterday – The U.S. Lost More Control of Gulf Oil

Iran didn’t just pressure Hormuz.

It hit the last bypass—and drove the odds of a good U.S. deal to near zero

Prof Robert Pape May 06, 2026

Yesterday looked like confusion -- It wasn’t.  Iran didn’t just threaten Hormuz—it hit the system’s last safety valve, the pipeline carrying nearly 2 million barrels a day out of the Gulf.  That single shift changes the logic of the war: the odds of a favorable U.S. deal are now near zero, and the chances this pause holds for two weeks are well under 20%.  Those numbers are not speculation.  They are the logical consequence of what just happened.  Donald Trump announced a naval escort operation to force oil through the Strait of Hormuz, then abruptly paused it. Iran struck energy-linked targets in the UAE. Shipping hesitated. Markets reacted instantly. Iran just announed Hormuz is “open” so long as every ship “coordinates” with its military — that is, on Iran’s terms.  Those are not disconnected events.  They are what it looks like when the United States can no longer impose outcomes at acceptable cost.  The key shift is not the naval escort.  It is what Iran did to the system behind it.

The Safety Valve Is No Longer Safe

For years, Gulf states built a workaround to Iran’s leverage over Hormuz.  The centerpiece is the Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline.  It runs roughly 235 miles from the Habshan oil fields to the port of Fujairah on the Gulf of Oman. It can carry up to 1.8 million barrels per day. Before this crisis, it was already moving over a million barrels daily.  Its purpose is simple: bypass Hormuz.  Fujairah allows oil to reach global markets without passing through waters Iran can threaten.  That pipeline is not a detail.  It is the system’s safety valve.  Strikes on UAE-linked infrastructure have already put at risk the only route capable of moving up to 1.8 million barrels per day outside Hormuz.  The implication is immediate: the only credible alternative to Hormuz is no longer secure.  The last major outlet designed to keep global oil flowing outside Iranian pressure—roughly 1 to 1.8 million barrels per day—is now at risk.  Iran has not just pressured a chokepoint. It has extended leverage to the bypass.  That is a structural shift in global power.

From Chokepoint to Power

About one-fifth of the world’s traded oil moves through Hormuz.  The assumption has always been that disruption could be managed—through pipelines like ADCOP, stockpiles, and rerouting. That assumption is now breaking.  When tankers hesitate to move even under U.S. escort, and the main bypass faces risk, markets do not wait.  Oil moving from the mid-$90s to well over $110 is not panic. It is a recognition that the system’s backups are failing. And when backups fail, power grows.  Right now, it is growing in Iran’s favor.  Once markets, insurers, and states adjust to this new risk structure, they do not revert quickly—locking in Iran’s leverage.

Why Diplomacy Guarantees US Failure

At the same time, reporting on a one-page diplomatic framework suggests phased steps tied to reopening Hormuz while deferring harder issues.  That is not negotiation. It is a slow-roll tactic for more Iranian power.  The United States has two core objectives: remove Iran’s enriched uranium or restore Hormuz to pre-war conditions.  This framework achieves neither. It postpones both.  Agreements that defer the central conflict while pressure continues do not resolve crises. They extend them.  Iran has been meeting with Pakistan, Russia and now China for nearly two weeks, not likely about how to help Trump and the US recover lost power, authority, and prestige — surely the opposite.  That is why the probability of a favorable deal by U.S. standards is effectively zero.  Once Iran can threaten both the chokepoint and the bypass, it has no incentive to trade that power away—making a favorable U.S. deal structurally impossible.

Why Peace Is Unlikely to Hold

The next phase turns on a single question: Will US test Iran’s limits?  If the US sends a single tanker without Iran’s explicit permission, this will put pressure on Iran to strike.  At that point, Washington faces a narrowing choice.  

1) Escalate further to restore credibility.
2) Or absorb another highly visible strategic loss.

Neither restores the pre-war status quo.  And because Iran now holds “the cards” over both the chokepoint and its primary bypass, the incentives to test that system are high.  That is why the odds of sustained calm—even over the next two weeks—are well under 20%.

The Bottom Line

This is no longer a war about defeating Iran.  It is a struggle over who sets the terms of Iran’s growing global power.  Iran has demonstrated it can impose costs on US allies and world economy faster than the United States can impose outcomes.  That is the definition of global strategic leverage.  The United States retains unmatched conventional military capabilities.  But capabilities that cannot secure outcomes—cannot keep oil flowing, cannot compel compliance—is no longer power – the only commodity that really matters in great power politics.  And this shift is now driving everything that comes next.

—Robert Pape
