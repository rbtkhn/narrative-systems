# Stage IV Begins? -- Inside the Bloomberg's Iran MOU

### The first strategic assessment of how the agreement could create an Iranian sphere of influence

Prof Robert Pape
Jun 17, 2026

Three months ago, the central question was whether Iran could survive.

Today, the question is very different.

If the reported memorandum published by Bloomberg and echoed by CNN and regional media reflects the broad outlines of Friday's agreement, the United States may now be entering Stage IV of the Escalation Trap:

acceptance of the gradual emergence of an Iranian sphere of influence.

If the agreement survives largely intact, it could mark the beginning of Iran's eventual emergence as the dominant power in the Gulf. Given the concentration of energy resources in that region, such an outcome could, over time, elevate Iran into a fourth center of world power alongside the United States, China, and Europe.

But the mechanisms begin immediately with the MOU.

Why does this matter beyond the negotiations?

Because the agreement could actually create new pressures for war.

The immediate reaction to the agreement has been relief. But a stronger Iran creates new incentives for Israel to act before Iranian power grows further. And if Israel concludes that diplomacy is accelerating rather than limiting Iranian influence, the next sixty days could become more dangerous, not less.

In this analysis, I examine four provisions inside the agreement that could translate Iranian wartime survival into long-term regional power—and explain why the decisions being made over the next sixty days may have consequences that extend far beyond the war itself.

Most commentary has focused on how the war ended. This analysis focuses on what kind of Middle East this agreement may now create.

## What A Fourth Center of Power Looks Like

Regional hegemony has never required global supremacy. Throughout modern history, centers of world power have emerged when four conditions come together.

First, the rising state controls resources large enough to affect the world economy. Influence over the Gulf carries special importance because the region contains roughly one-fifth of globally traded oil and natural gas. Control over that concentration of energy resources gives regional developments worldwide consequences.

Second, states that were previously neutral or even hostile gradually begin accommodating the rising power and adjusting their policies around it. Geography usually drives this process. Neighboring countries cannot relocate. As confidence in outside protectors declines, former rivals increasingly conclude that coexistence with the resident power is safer than continued resistance.

Third, the rising state develops the ability to deter even the strongest outside powers from imposing their will directly on its homeland. Regional hegemons do not need military superiority everywhere. They only need the capacity to raise the costs of intervention high enough that stronger states become cautious. Deterrence itself becomes a source of influence.

Fourth, the rising power strengthens and sustains its existing allies. Networks that survive war become more cohesive. Clients become more dependent. The result is not merely the preservation of old relationships but the consolidation of a wider political and security order centered on the dominant state.

Taken together, these four conditions produce something larger than military strength. They create a sphere of influence. A regional hegemon becomes the dominant power inside its neighborhood, neighboring states increasingly respond to its preferences, allies grow stronger under its protection, and the resources under its influence acquire global significance.

By these standards, the central question raised by the Iran War is no longer whether Iran survived. The question is whether survival has begun creating the foundations of regional hegemony. If former rivals increasingly accommodate Tehran, if Iran preserves the ability to deter attacks on its homeland, and if its regional allies emerge from the war stronger rather than weaker, historians may eventually conclude that the Iran War marked the beginning of Iran's emergence as the dominant power in the Gulf—and thus the rise of a fourth center of world power alongside the United States, China, and Europe.

## Mechanism One: The Resistance Belt Survives

The most important provision in the entire document may be Article 1 (exact text at bottom of the analysis).

Ending the Lebanon war effectively preserves Iran's forward security architecture stretching from Lebanon through the Red Sea and into the Gulf. What began over decades as separate proxy relationships increasingly resembles a continuous strategic belt.

The war did not dismantle Iran's regional security architecture. Hezbollah remains in Lebanon. The Houthis remain in the Red Sea corridor. Iran remains the center of that network. What emerged from the conflict was therefore not restoration of the previous balance. It was political acceptance of the military geography created by survival.

Wars often create new realities before diplomats recognize them. Article 1 appears to acknowledge a strategic fact that military operations failed to reverse. The resistance network connecting Lebanon, Yemen, Iraq, and Iran remains intact. Survival itself becomes the foundation of future influence.

## Mechanism Two: Gulf States Begin Bandwagoning

Article 7 proposes ending sanctions.

Article 6 proposes massive reconstruction and investment.

If sanctions disappear, Gulf states face powerful incentives to shift from balancing against Iran toward profiting from Iran.

Geography again shapes political outcomes. Iran is a resident power. The United States is an external power. As Iranian power appears increasingly durable, Gulf governments face growing incentives to hedge rather than confront.

Throughout history, neighboring states have often accommodated rising powers once they conclude those powers are durable. Economic integration gradually replaces containment. If Iran appears stronger after the war than before it, Gulf governments increasingly have incentives to hedge rather than confront.

Over time, economic interdependence can evolve into political accommodation. If Gulf states increasingly conclude that Iran is permanent while American deployments are temporary, the center of gravity inside the Gulf gradually shifts.

## Mechanism Three: Negotiations Become a Source of Power

Articles 10 and 11 may matter even before any final agreement is reached.

Articles 10 and 11 create an immediate recovery mechanism. Oil exports resume. Frozen assets are released. Revenue begins flowing before negotiations even conclude. Iran therefore does not need to wait sixty days to begin rebuilding military capabilities damaged by the war.

New resources can replenish missile inventories, expand drone production, finance reconstruction, and restore support for Hezbollah and the Houthis. In this sense, negotiations themselves become a source of strategic leverage. Diplomacy no longer merely manages power. It begins generating power.

The critical point is timing. Iran receives resources immediately, while most constraints remain deferred to future negotiations. As a result, the process of negotiation itself becomes a mechanism for increasing Iranian power rather than limiting it.

## Mechanism Four: The Threshold State Survives

Article 9 may ultimately prove to be the most consequential provision in the memorandum because it addresses the issue that originally triggered the war: Iran's nuclear capability.

Pending a final agreement, the memorandum states that Iran will maintain the status quo on its nuclear program. The significance of that language lies in what it does not require. Status quo is not dismantlement. Status quo is not rollback. Status quo means preserving whatever capabilities Iran possesses when negotiations begin. If Iran entered the talks as a threshold nuclear state, then the negotiations themselves preserve threshold status.

Article 8 reiterates that Iran will never produce nuclear weapons. But throughout the nuclear age, capabilities have generally proven more durable than declarations of intent. States may change policies. Capabilities are far harder to reverse.

Threshold capability itself creates leverage. A state that remains only months away from nuclear weapons possesses options that adversaries must take into account. For that reason, countries from Israel to Pakistan and North Korea have all discovered that nuclear latency can itself become a source of strategic influence.

The strategic irony is obvious. The war began with the objective of eliminating Iran's long-term nuclear leverage. If the agreement preserves the status quo, negotiations may stabilize the very capability the war originally sought to destroy. Survival on the battlefield would thus become enduring political leverage at the negotiating table.

## The Meaning of Stage IV

The Escalation Trap has always contained two possible paths after the failure of the opening campaign.

Stage I was the attempt to collapse Iran through precision strikes and decapitation. Stage II began when Iran survived and responded horizontally, disrupting Hormuz and imposing costs far beyond the battlefield. Once that happened, Washington faced a strategic fork in the road. One option was Stage III: continued escalation through blockade, expanded bombing, and potentially even the introduction of ground forces. The alternative was Stage IV: accepting that the war had produced a new balance of power and shifting from coercion to accommodation.

The reported memorandum points toward Stage IV.

Rather than forcing Iranian capitulation, the agreement implicitly recognizes Iranian leverage. Hormuz reopens because Iran agrees to reopen it. Sanctions are relaxed because Washington seeks stabilization rather than continued confrontation. Negotiations become the mechanism for managing a rival that has survived rather than eliminating a rival that has been defeated.

This does not mean the war is truly over. Stage IV does not eliminate the possibility of returning to Stage III. In fact, the two stages may coexist. As global oil inventories continue to decline and Iran attempts to convert leverage into additional concessions, Washington may again face pressure to escalate. Domestic politics will matter as well. No administration heading toward midterm elections wants to appear as though it is repeatedly conceding ground to an adversary.

But the strategic significance of the memorandum lies elsewhere.

For the first time since the war began, the United States appears to be operating from the assumption that Iran cannot simply be broken. The objective shifts from defeating Iran to managing Iran. That shift itself represents acceptance of a new political reality created by the war.

If the agreement survives largely intact, historians may eventually conclude that the memorandum did not end the Iran War.

They may instead conclude that it marked the beginning of the gradual acceptance of an Iranian sphere of influence.

And if that sphere of influence ultimately produces Iranian predominance inside the Gulf, control over one of the world's most important concentrations of energy resources could, over time, elevate Iran into a fourth center of world power alongside the United States, China, and Europe.

That transformation would unfold over years rather than months.

But major shifts in international politics rarely announce themselves at the moment they begin. Historians usually recognize them only after the new order has already arrived.

## Indicators I'm Watching

1. Early Gulf economic engagement with Iran.

The first signal will be whether Gulf governments and sovereign wealth funds begin publicly discussing investment, trade, or reconstruction projects inside Iran during the sixty-day negotiating period. Such moves would indicate that regional states increasingly view Iran as a permanent feature of the balance of power rather than a problem to contain.

2. Initial reductions in the American military footprint.

The second signal will be whether Washington begins repositioning naval assets or announcing withdrawals from bases surrounding the Gulf. Even modest reductions would suggest that the United States is shifting from coercion toward stabilization. Fewer American forces would also reinforce the belief among Gulf states that they must increasingly manage relations with Iran themselves.

3. Visible signs of Hezbollah's recovery.

The third signal will be evidence that financial flows are again reaching Hezbollah. Renewed salaries, reconstruction efforts in southern Lebanon, or expanded political activity would suggest that Iran is converting economic recovery into restored regional influence.

Taken together, these indicators will reveal whether the agreement is merely ending a war or beginning the gradual emergence of a new regional order.

The key question over the next two weeks is simple:

Does the direction of events increasingly point toward Tehran—or away from it?

## The MOU as Released by Bloomberg

Below is the text of the 14-point draft memorandum, as seen by Bloomberg News.

1. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States, together with their allies in the current war, declare upon the signing of this Memorandum of Understanding an immediate and permanent end to the war on all fronts, including Lebanon, and undertake that from now on they will not launch any hostile action against each other, and will refrain from the threat or use of force against each other. The final agreement will confirm the provisions of this Article and the remaining Articles.

2. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States undertake to respect each other's sovereignty and territorial integrity, and to refrain from interfering in each other's internal affairs.

3. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States undertake to negotiate and reach a final agreement within a maximum period of 60 days, extendable by mutual consent.

4. Immediately upon the signing of this Memorandum of Understanding, the United States Lift the naval blockade and prevent any interference or obstruction against the Islamic Republic of Iran, and restore traffic within a maximum of 30 days to its full capacity; the traffic of ships shall be proportional to the pre-war volume of traffic on the part of the Islamic Republic of Iran. The United States also undertakes to withdraw its forces from the surrounding areas within 30 days after the final agreement.

5. Upon signing this Memorandum of Understanding, the Islamic Republic of Iran will immediately take steps to ensure that the movement of merchant ships from the Persian Gulf to the Sea of ​​Oman and vice versa is resumed within 30 days to the pre-war volume, taking into account the need for the removal of technical obstacles and the neutralization of mines by Iran.

6. The United States undertakes, together with its regional partners, to create a comprehensive plan agreed upon by both parties for the rehabilitation and economic development of the Islamic Republic of Iran, While ensuring financing of at least $300 billion. The implementation mechanism of this plan, as part of the final agreement, will be formulated within 60 days.

7. The United States commits to ending, on a schedule to be agreed upon as part of the final agreement, all types of sanctions currently facing the Islamic Republic of Iran, including resolutions of the United Nations Security Council and the Board of Governors of the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), and all unilateral U.S. sanctions, both primary and secondary.

8. The Islamic Republic of Iran reiterates that it will never produce nuclear weapons. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States have agreed that the fate of enriched material and the fate of all other mutually agreed nuclear-related issues, including Iran's nuclear needs, will be adequately addressed in a final agreement; the final agreement will confirm the provisions of this Article.

9. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States agree that, pending a final agreement, they will maintain the status quo: Iran will maintain the status quo on its nuclear program, and the United States will not impose new sanctions on Iran or strengthen its forces in the region.

10. The United States undertakes that immediately after the signing of this Memorandum of Understanding, and until the date of the lifting of sanctions, the United States Treasury Department will issue waivers for exports of Iranian crude oil, petrochemical products and their derivatives, and all related services, including banking, insurance, transportation, and the like.

11. The United States undertakes that, in light of the progress of negotiations towards a final agreement, frozen or restricted funds and assets of the Islamic Republic of Iran will be released and made fully available. These funds, whether held in the master account or transferred, will be used for any final beneficiary payment determined by the Central Bank of the Islamic Republic of Iran and will be fully available for use. The United States undertakes to issue all necessary permits and licenses on this basis.

12. The Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States agree that an implementation mechanism will be established to oversee the successful implementation of and future commitment to the Final Agreement.

13. Following the signing of this Memorandum of Understanding, and upon receipt of assurances regarding the commencement of implementation of Articles 4, 5, 10, and 11 of this Memorandum of Understanding, and the continued implementation of these steps, the Islamic Republic of Iran and the United States will enter into negotiations for a Final Agreement solely with respect to the remaining Articles.

14. The final agreement will be approved through a binding resolution of the UN Security Council.
