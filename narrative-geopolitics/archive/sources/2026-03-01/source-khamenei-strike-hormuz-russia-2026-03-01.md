It seems to me most likely that this claim that this was an attack of opportunity falls into that category.

To me, it looks like this was an attack that had been prepared in advance. Maybe there were some adjustments as to the timing. Maybe some people weren't happy that it happened on a moonlit night. The second thing I wanted to say is that there has been a very sharp response about all of this, specifically from the Russians. They appear to be absolutely furious both with the Americans and with the Israelis.

That is perhaps a hint that eventually this might escalate into a nuclear confrontation.

The statement goes on in the same vein, but it is a very long statement and I am not going to say more about it in this programme.

The Russians are furious. They see this as a massive breach of faith by Israel. They are obviously less than happy — in fact, furious — with Trump himself. Putin speaks about a cynical act that violates all moral and international laws, and the Russian Foreign Ministry is saying the same. Inevitably in Moscow, more and more people are going to come forward and say: "Well, given how Trump has acted, how can we continue to even think of trusting him on any issue, least of all the conflict in Ukraine?"

Now to that I am now going to turn, though obviously what I am going to say is going to be less than I usually say.

Let's start first of all with Kyrylo Budanov — the man who is now Zelensky's chief of staff and who was one of the negotiators at the meetings in Abu Dhabi and in Geneva. Budanov gave an interview in which he said that the Russians have agreed to the United States giving security guarantees to Ukraine, that the only issue is territory, and that this agreement by the Russians was communicated to the Ukrainians over the course of the military-to-military discussions between Budanov himself and Admiral Kostakov.

I interpret that as part of the dissension and disputes that are taking place in Kiev at the present time, because I do not think that what Budanov is saying reflects the Russian position at all. On the contrary, the Russians have been saying in statement after statement — extending all the way back to Anchorage, but also over the last few days — that they have no objections to Ukraine receiving security guarantees, but certainly they don't agree to the kind of security guarantees that are being talked about in the West (Article 5-type security guarantees), and that those security guarantees that the West are going to provide to Ukraine not only need to be coordinated with the Russians (who should also be one of the guarantor states), but that Russia also, and simultaneously, must receive its own security guarantees from the West. In other words, there needs to be a recalibration of the entire security architecture in Europe.

That continues to be the publicly stated Russian position. I certainly don't think an intelligence officer like Kostakov, who is not even a member of Russia's Security Council, has the authority to negotiate an issue of that importance.

What Budanov is now saying is anyway contradicted by the reports that came from Kiev itself that at the last meeting which took place in Geneva, the Russians took an exceptionally tough line — something which the Ukrainians said was the fault of the chief Russian negotiator Vladimir Medinsky, who of course has been appointed to his position by none other than Putin himself.

So Budanov is clearly keen to try to get some end to the fighting. He knows how bad the situation for Ukraine is becoming on the battlefield and he is trying to get more flexibility in the negotiations from his own side. He is prepared, for example, apparently to consider some territorial concessions — something which Zelensky categorically rules out. I think this interview that he has given should be understood in that context. It was The Economist that revealed that there was this division between Budanov and Zelensky on these issues.

For the rest, the Russians continue to press forward with the special military operation. The last 24 hours seem to have witnessed a certain reduction in drone and missile strikes both by the Russians and the Ukrainians upon each other. But I get the sense that the Russian strikes previously had done an awful lot of damage. As for the Ukrainians, maybe they are running out of drones. Maybe they feel that drone attacks on Russia, at a time when all global attention is on the situation with Iran, don't matter as much. Who knows? Because for the Ukrainians, drone attacks on Russia are first and foremost a PR issue. Maybe that is the explanation for all of this.

But anyway, on the front lines in Ukraine things continue to move. Perhaps the most dramatic news actually is taking place in Sumy region far in the north, where the Russians now appear to be expanding the territory that they control around the city of Sumy very, very fast and in a way that must be making some people in Kiev nervous that the Russians have some plans to storm the city of Sumy itself.

There is a lot of speculation about what the Russians are doing in Kharkov and in Sumy region along the border. The Russians of course still explain all of this as the creation of a buffer zone. But in Sumy region especially, it seems to me to go well beyond that. It seems to me that this isn't actually a buffer zone exactly. Rather, it is a foothold. The Russians are carving out a large foothold — a base area, if you like — for a future offensive in northern Ukraine, which can only be ultimately directed at the city of Kiev itself.

Elsewhere there is also a great deal of activity. The Russians in particular seem to be focusing very heavily on the northern areas of the Donbass front lines. There are reports that they have captured more positions around Lyman itself. I have heard reports that the Russians have more than enough forces to occupy the whole of Lyman and could have done this weeks ago, but that they chose not to do so because they preferred to keep the Ukrainian garrison in Lyman pinned down there defending the town. It is not so much a plan to create a cauldron around this particular garrison, but rather to take more positions to the north and west of it — which is what the Russians appear to be doing — so that when the Ukrainians do eventually withdraw from Lyman (which they inevitably will), the Russian positions will be even stronger in the north.

The fall of Lyman and the fall of Ray-Alexandrovka further south (which the Russians are now approaching), combined by the way with the fall of Kupiansk in the north, is going to be decisive. It is going to close the trap around the Ukrainian troops in Slavyansk and Kramatorsk. As a Russian journalist writing for Readovka pointed out, it is also going to create a fragmentation in this area. It will make movement of troops and supplies between Slavyansk and Kramatorsk impossible, creating two cauldrons within the bigger cauldron around the Slavyansk conurbation that the Russians are busy creating.

Elsewhere I noticed that the Ukrainians have now coloured the whole of Gushchino — the village to the northwest of Pokrovsk — as being in the grey zone, which to my mind confirms the report from Readovka that Gushchino has essentially fallen to the Russians. I expect confirmation of that to be provided by the Russian Defence Ministry fairly soon.

The situation in Konstantinovka continues to involve intense fighting. My sense is that the Ukrainian forces in southwestern Konstantinovka are by now mostly surrounded and what we are probably looking at there are mopping-up operations.

The key in the battle in Donbass is this development by the Russians of a cauldron around the remaining Ukrainian forces in Donbass. This is clearly what the Russians are working towards. If it is ever established — which it will be if Zelensky has his way and insists that the Ukrainians cannot withdraw from there — maybe the reason why Budanov wants to withdraw from Donbass is because, unlike Zelensky, he generally worries about the emergence of a cauldron there.

If there is a big cauldron in Donbass of the kind that the Russians seem to be working towards, then as I said, it will be the biggest and most decisive battle of the war up till now.

There has been less news from Zaporizhzhia region over the last 24 hours. In fact, there has been practically no news at all. Bear in mind that the last we have been hearing is that the Russians were advancing and that the Ukrainian counter-offensive in the northeast of Zaporizhzhia was at a standstill. If there has been no news, that is further indication that this Ukrainian offensive in northeast Zaporizhzhia is indeed at a standstill.

I expect that over the next few weeks the Russian advance towards first Orikhiv and then Zaporizhzhia city will resume in earnest and then we will see.
