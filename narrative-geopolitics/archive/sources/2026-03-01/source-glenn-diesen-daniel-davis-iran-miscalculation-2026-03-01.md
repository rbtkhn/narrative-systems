**Glenn Diesen:** Welcome back. We are joined by Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis, a four-time combat veteran and host of the very popular *Daniel Davis Deep Dive* YouTube show. I’ll leave a link in the description. Thank you for coming back on. We only spoke a few days ago, when you were warning that this war was coming — and sadly, you were right, as usual.

As this war has now gone on for two days, it doesn’t seem to be going as planned. All sides have to put on a brave face and claim success in any war, but over the past two days we’ve seen a lot of chaos. It’s very hard to suggest this was intended. Do you see any strategic miscalculation on the side of the United States, or was this a cost they were prepared to absorb?

**Daniel Davis:** It is a major miscalculation — but unfortunately, one that was self-evident before it ever started. All the signs were in place. You mentioned that I saw this coming, and it frankly wasn’t that hard for me because the signals we were giving made it really clear.

Absent an 11th-hour change of mind by Trump — which was always possible, so I left some room for that — based on the movement of military equipment and gear, the statements that were made, and where forces were positioned, it looked to me like this was headed for war. I’ve been through this before. I lived it — twice, really — as a member of the armed forces watching this exact same kind of buildup.

That’s one of the reasons I was so effective in calling the Russian war in 2022. Most people thought it was just another buildup, that the Russians weren’t really going to do it, they were just posturing. But I said no, this one is fundamentally different for the same reasons as the other two I had seen. When you see the types of forces Russia amassed — not just demonstration forces, but everything needed with all the enablers: logistics, medical support, everything to sustain combat operations — all parked forward exactly like what I experienced when I was part of a deploying force.

The same thing happened here with the air logistics, refueling capabilities, the C-17s, C-5s — a massive number, like 50% of our entire inventory — plus large numbers of fighter jets and aircraft carrier strike groups. When you look at all that, it was clear: this was designed for use, not pressure. That convinced me Iran was already willing to negotiate.

In fact, I think history will look back and say the central moment where our failure crystallized was the night before the attack. The foreign minister from Oman went on CBS News and said he had secured profound concessions from the Iranian negotiator: zero reprocessing (which they had previously said wasn’t even on the table), elimination of their stockpiles, and even long-range missiles and proxy forces (to be addressed later). It was a golden opportunity for the Trump administration if negotiations were truly what they wanted.

When the attack came the next morning, it made it very clear this was never about negotiations — it was always about war.

Now we’re in a situation where we’ve started something we can’t finish. As you and I discussed last time, this is a war you can’t win. We set unattainable military objectives. When President Trump came out early that Saturday morning from Mar-a-Lago and laid out all the objectives, I was cringing. Unless the Iranian side unexpectedly collapses out of fear, there’s no way to accomplish those goals if they keep their backbone stiff.

They had already calculated — as reported in the *New York Times* just days before — that fighting a war with the US was less risky than submitting. So far, that’s exactly what they’ve done. They’re paying a heavy price: profound destruction of armed forces, senior leaders, and military infrastructure. But they have extensive underground facilities and can withstand this for an extended period. We cannot. That’s the key issue.

I said we might have four or five days of high-intensity fighting, maybe stretch it to a couple of weeks if judicious. But if the other side doesn’t capitulate, we’re in real trouble. And now it looks like that’s where we are.

There are reports just in the last few hours that President Trump is saying “maybe we can have a ceasefire” and that “they’re asking me for it.” That has to be complete BS. There’s no way they would ask for it now — not after their Ayatollah has been assassinated. That’s the last thing they’re going to do.

Evidence suggests the Ayatollah knew he was going to be assassinated and willingly sacrificed himself to become a martyr. He didn’t go to a protected location; he stayed in his known compound. Now, instead of breaking their backbone, it’s consolidating support behind the regime. Throughout the Shia world, there are major protests in support of Iran because of the Ayatollah’s sacrifice.

This was a profound miscalculation. I use the word “miscalculation” reluctantly because it implies they thought it might work. This outcome was well known ahead of time unless the other side simply quit — and everything indicated they wouldn’t. Yet they went ahead anyway.

**Glenn Diesen:** They didn’t seem to predict that Iran would attack bases across the region — Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, Oman, Kuwait, Iraq, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Israel — and kill the Ayatollah. They thought the government would collapse. In Shia culture, martyrdom runs very deep. As you said, it has consolidated support, and Shia communities across Iraq, Pakistan, Bahrain, and elsewhere have taken to the streets in support of Iran.

I saw Trump giving a speech arguing that the entire Iranian army should put down its weapons and civilians should take to the streets. Does he really think this is going to happen now?

**Daniel Davis:** I really do think he believes it. A lot of people project their own values onto others. He’s thinking: if someone stronger came in and bombed the crap out of me, I’d give up. That’s what happened with the speedboats, with taking the leader in Venezuela — they just rolled over. He thought showing toughness would make them collapse, not recognizing the radical differences between Venezuela and Shia Iran.

One area I didn’t foresee correctly: Iran said they would attack all American bases in the region. We took that seriously and bolstered air defenses. But I didn’t think they would also strike civilian sectors from the beginning. Their message seems to be: “We’ve been suffering since 2010 from Israel and since the 2019 assassination of Soleimani by the US. You’ve been protecting them and hosting these bases. Now it’s time for you to feel some pain too.” A big “screw you.”

They’re all in. They’re willing to inflict pain on the US, Israel, and their regional backers until everyone backs off. They might negotiate later, but not right now.

**Glenn Diesen:** What do you see as Iran’s wider war strategy? I was surprised by the civilian targets — airports, hotels. The foreign minister said they attacked hotels because they house American troops, but there have been many other civilian targets. They closed the Strait of Hormuz and attacked ships — four oil tankers so far. If you sat down with their war planners, what do you think they’d say?

**Daniel Davis:** I think they’ve calculated they’re going to run this for a number of months. This isn’t short-lived or gradual ratcheting. They’ve said, “We’re all in.” After 47 years of what they see as punishment, new rules are in effect. They view this as an existential crisis.

They know they’ll suffer badly, but they believe they have a shot at a positive outcome by draining the United States. Unlike the Russia-Ukraine war of attrition, the US does not have the capacity to sustain this for years. General Jack Keane boasted on the morning of the attack that we could do this for two or three weeks. That was another major blunder — publicly admitting our narrow window. Iran now knows to stretch this out.

We don’t have unlimited gasoline, bullets, bombs, or missiles. Our inventories are already strained from supporting Israel since 2023 and Ukraine. Iran has limited resources too, but they can go longer than three weeks. They have tens of thousands of Shahed drones and can produce 100 missiles per month even under sanctions.

As casualties mount — and they will — and if this drags past two or three weeks, political pressure in the US and Israel will grow intense. Iran’s goal is to cause enough pain that we come back seeking a negotiated settlement far worse than the one on the table the night before the attack. Those generous terms are gone. They’ll demand sanctions relief and other concessions.

**Glenn Diesen:** Were you surprised there was no gradual escalation ladder? By the end of day two, they hit all these countries, closed the Strait of Hormuz, and struck civilian targets. They went all out.

**Daniel Davis:** It was 50/50 for me. I wasn’t shocked, but they went a bit further than I expected. It’s logical — it’s what I would have done. Based on their history, I thought they might hold some things back as negotiating leverage, but it appears they decided in advance: if the US and Israel start this war, we’re all in.

They also seem to have decided the Ayatollah would serve as a martyr. It’s working so far. Even many Iranians who opposed the regime are now rallying because they see this as an existential attack by their enemies, not an opportunity for regime change. They remember what happened in Libya, Iraq, Afghanistan — we say nice things about the people and then walk away.

**Glenn Diesen:** How would you assess developments in the coming days and weeks? Is this ultimately a battle of wills and the ability to absorb pain?

**Daniel Davis:** Yes — ultimately it’s a battle of wills. We tried to do this on the cheap: massive air and naval power, no boots on the ground. Without ground forces to compel compliance, there’s no reason for them to capitulate. They just have to survive the initial onslaught.

The key vulnerability is missile inventories — both offensive and defensive. We’re already seeing Patriots miss on day one. If we run low, waves of drones and missiles will get through. Iran’s strategy is to increase the pain until we can’t sustain it.

The most vulnerable pressure point for the US is casualties. For the region, it’s economic pain — oil infrastructure, etc. There’s still room on the escalation ladder, and I hope we don’t climb much higher. This could become a major problem if it doesn’t end within a couple of weeks.
