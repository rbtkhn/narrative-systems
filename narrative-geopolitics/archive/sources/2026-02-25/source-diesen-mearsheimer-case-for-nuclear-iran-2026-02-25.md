# Diesen × John Mearsheimer — The Case for a Nuclear Iran (operator transcript)

**John Mearsheimer: The Case for a Nuclear Iran**

**Diesen:** Welcome back. We are joined today by Professor John Mearsheimer to discuss what appears to be a pending war against Iran. Thank you as always for taking the time.

**John Mearsheimer:** My pleasure as always, Glenn, to be here.

**Diesen:** The US appears to be in the final stages before an attack on Iran. I guess we can conclude this from the buildup of these military capabilities and the stated intentions. They all seem to indicate this. However, on the other side, I don’t really see a very clear outcome here or a strategy for how victory should be defined. A lot of things can go wrong.

Do you think this attack will happen? It’s almost impossible to feel confident leaning one way or the other. But I actually think a case can be made that after last night’s State of the Union address, it looks like Trump is looking for an off-ramp here. Let me give you my take on it. Again, I want to be clear: I’m not arguing that Trump has decided not to attack, but I think we’re leaning slightly in that direction.

First, let me just start with the broader context and then talk about what he said last night. With regard to the broader context, it’s very important to understand that virtually every country in the world except Israel does not want us to attack. It’s not like the Saudis and the Gulf States are saying here’s a great opportunity to attack Iran. On the contrary, the Saudis are working with the Iranians, the Gulf States are working with the Iranians to make it clear that they’re opposed to this attack and they will not participate.

The Chinese and the Russians are opposed. The Europeans are quietly opposed. Only Israel is in favor of this. That’s point number one.

Point number two is that President Trump’s military advisers have basically told him, “We do not have a workable strategy. We do not have a plausible strategy for winning this war.” This is really quite remarkable. He was told the same thing on January 14th when he was thinking of attacking Iran on that day. His military advisers — and of course General Caine, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, is the key person here — were saying that to him, and nothing has changed. We don’t have a viable military option.

Third, it’s quite clear from the news reports in the United States that his political advisers are telling him this is a really bad idea. He’s in deep trouble. What I’m saying here is that the midterm elections are coming up in November. The economy is not in great shape. His poll ratings are way down. If the Democrats score a big victory in the fall and capture both the House and the Senate, which is possible, he will probably be impeached or at least there’s a good chance he’ll be impeached and then convicted.

In terms of the political waters that he’s swimming in these days, these waters are shark-infested — very dangerous. It’s hardly surprising that his political advisers are saying: do not do this. Again, you want to think about the consequences of a Democratic-controlled Senate and House.

So again, just in terms of the general context, only one country on the planet — Israel — is pushing for this. Number two, his military advisers are telling him that this is an ill-advised attack. Number three, the politics say don’t do it. That’s the context.

Then you go to what he said last night. He did not say that nuclear enrichment had to be taken off the table. He did not say that Iran has to get rid of its ballistic missiles. He did not say that Iran had to stop supporting Hezbollah, Hamas and the Houthis. Those have been three demands that have popped up on numerous occasions over the past month or so, but he did not repeat any of them. All he said was that Iran cannot have a nuclear weapon.

He said that what he needs to hear from them — and he has not heard from them — are what he called “these are his secret words.” What are the secret words that he hasn’t heard? “We will never have a nuclear weapon.” So it’s just very important to focus on what he said. He was just dealing with the nuclear weapon issue here — not nuclear enrichment, not ballistic missiles, and not support for the Houthis, Hezbollah, and Hamas. He just said he wants to hear from them that “we will never have a nuclear weapon.” Those are exactly President Trump’s words in the State of the Union address.

Slightly before his State of the Union address, the Iranian Foreign Minister said — and I’m going to quote from him — “Our fundamental convictions are crystal clear. I underline the word crystal clear. Iran will under no circumstances ever develop a nuclear weapon.”

It seems to me, Glenn, those are the secret words. Those are what President Trump wanted to hear and it’s now been heard by him for sure. When you think about what President Trump said last night and what the Iranian Foreign Minister said slightly before the State of the Union address, it seems to me you have the makings of a deal here.

When you marry that to the broader context, it does look like you’re not going to have a war. Now again, I want to emphasize: I’m not saying that Trump won’t strike Iran. Nobody ever wants to feel any high level of confidence in predicting what he will do. But this morning as we talk here in the United States — or I guess it’s the evening there in Europe — it does look like President Trump is searching for an off-ramp.

**Diesen:** That’s a great point though because I kind of assumed that the war was unavoidable if there wasn’t any possibility of a peace deal. But of course that was because I knew that the Iranians wouldn’t move an inch on the issue of enrichment for civilian purposes, the ballistic missiles, as well as its regional ally alliances.

However, if it’s about nuclear weapons then there’s a possibility for a deal. There used to be a deal called the JCPOA. This is why that’s a reason for optimism if that suggests Trump is walking some of this back.

**John Mearsheimer:** It appears, Glenn — I mean, as you know, with President Trump, things can change minute to minute — but it appears that nuclear enrichment is not the issue. The Iranian Foreign Minister said after saying that Iran under no circumstances would ever develop a nuclear weapon, he said immediately thereafter they would also never give up their nuclear enrichment capability. So Trump knows that nuclear enrichment is not on the table as an issue.

I think there’s no question that the two sides can work out a deal that looks like the JCPOA. If the Iranians are smart — and I think the Iranians definitely want to avoid a war — and we move to some sort of agreement that looks like the JCPOA, the Iranians will say that it’s a better deal for the United States and for Iran than the JCPOA. In other words, the Iranians really have to help Trump here walk this one back.

Trump has boxed himself in. If he’s going to strike a deal that looks something like the JCPOA, both Trump obviously and the Iranians as well have to work together to make it look like this is a better deal for everyone so that we can put this one to bed.

**Diesen:** Let me ask a wider question though, just taking a step back. Why the war to begin with? Is there a misplaced threat perception here? Do you see how Iran is a threat to the United States? No one is suggesting that it has the capabilities now to attack the US. No one’s suggesting that it has the ability to have nuclear weapons now either. So if you look at the capabilities as well as the intentions, I don’t see neither the capabilities nor the intentions of striking the United States.

Do you think the threat perception has been inflated or why is it such a sudden and imminent need to attack Iran?

**John Mearsheimer:** There’s only one explanation for what’s going on here, and I can give it to you in one word: Israel.

I said before, virtually every country on the planet except for Israel is opposed to this war. His military advisers are telling him that we have no military option. Nobody can rebut what you said. Iran is not a threat to the United States. When Steve Witkoff says things like the Iranians are like a week or two away from developing a nuclear weapon, everybody at this point recognizes that this is sheer nonsense.

We’re doing this because of pressure from Israel and the lobby inside the United States. Very few people will say that explicitly because in the United States and in the West more generally you have to tread lightly when you talk about Israel.

The same thing happened with the Iraq war in 2003. If you look at the book that Steve Walt and I wrote on the Israel lobby, we have a whole chapter on the Iraq war. There is absolutely no question that Israel and the lobby played a central role in pushing the United States into that war. It was not Israel and the lobby alone, but Israel and the lobby played a central role. We document that very clearly.

Of course, when the war went south, Israel and its supporters went to great lengths to say that Israel and the lobby had nothing to do with pushing us into that war. But they did. If you look at the literature on the Iraq war, on the causes of the Iraq war — why did the United States do this? — hardly anybody discusses Israel. If they do discuss Israel and the lobby, they dismiss those two forces because they fully understand they’ll get into a lot of trouble if they blame Israel and the lobby in any way for causing the Iraq war.

What we have here in the West is this completely distorted discourse about Middle East politics that leaves Israel out of the story when it is in fact oftentimes the key factor that’s driving the train. That’s what’s going on here.

Just think, Glenn, about all the times that Benjamin Netanyahu has come to the White House over the past year to visit President Trump. He’s not come to talk about European-Israeli relations. He’s not come to play tiddlywinks. He’s come to talk about Iran. His last visit was prompted by the fact that he was getting cold feet — that he thought that Trump was getting cold feet about attacking Iran.

This just goes to show you that Benjamin Netanyahu is profoundly committed to getting the United States to attack Iran for Israel. That’s what’s going on here. It’s a bit of an indictment of academia these times as well. I’ve noticed the same. It’s not about getting the story right. It’s about obedience to the narrative.

**Diesen:** But let me ask one question though. If even if Iran had the capabilities of attacking the United States, surely a lot of the discussion should have been organized around deterrence because anyone planning to attack the United States would be out of their mind. It is the US by far the most powerful military force on the planet. So this is madness and anyone could be deterred, especially the Iranians.

Often the argument then is organized around the idea that well Iran’s simply not a rational actor and this is kind of the image we have — that Iran is this fanatic, irrational mullahs just seeking to destroy Israel and America at any cost. How do you read Iran though? Is this just war propaganda or do you see Iran as a rational actor? If they are a rational actor, what do you see as being the threats they’re responding to? What’s the rational way of responding to those threats?

**John Mearsheimer:** The thing is that Israel and the lobby have gone to enormous lengths to portray Iran as the devil incarnate. Part of that is describing Iran as an irrational actor, a crazed state, a state that mindlessly supports terrorism, that is interested in destroying Israel and is in fact trying to develop nuclear weapons so as soon as it gets those weapons it can launch a nuclear attack on Israel and wipe it off the map.

That’s the basic narrative that you hear from Israel and its hardline supporters here in the United States. What’s going on here, Glenn, is the Middle East is one giant disaster zone at this point in time. The question is who is responsible for this? Lots of people believe Israel is responsible for this — that Israel is like a rogue elephant.

The Israelis and the lobby understand this. So what they are doing is they are portraying Israel as the victim and they’re making the argument that Iran is the state that is responsible for all this chaos. It’s not Israel. It’s Iran. They want you to understand that Iran cannot be deterred because it is an irrational state. That means we need regime change. If we can go beyond that and break the country up that would be even better because then what we would do was we would take this rogue elephant off the table. It wouldn’t matter anymore.

You have this whole narrative that has been developed over time in very sophisticated ways. It’s amazing the extent to which modern Western governments — to include Israel in this case — the ability of them to propagandize is just truly amazing. You and I know that very well from the Ukraine case as well.

The end result is that lots of people out there believe the narrative that you were describing and I’m trying to embellish here. But if you’re interested in facts, the truth is that the rogue elephant in the Middle East is not Iran. I want somebody to tell me when was the last time that Iran started a war. When did Iran start a war? I don’t think modern Iran has ever started a war.

You’ll hear in the media about Iran attacking Israel twice in 2024 and the rhetoric implies that Iran struck Israel out of the blue twice in April and October of 2024. But if you look carefully at those cases, those were retaliations for Israeli strikes against Iran. Iran is not a highly aggressive state by any means.

I don’t want to make the argument that Iran or any state is always well-behaved and is a model citizen in the international system. This is not the way international politics works. States sometimes misbehave. Sometimes states are very aggressive. I’m not arguing that Iran is a model citizen. But Iran is not a particularly aggressive state. It’s been a victim in almost all the wars that it’s fought in recent years.

Remember, it was the Iraqis who invaded Iran in the early 1980s. Iran did not invade Iraq. It’s been Israel that’s been attacking Iran before Iran attacked Israel. Iran, as I said before, was retaliating.

In terms of rationality, I think all of the evidence is that Iran is a rational state. When you listen to the Foreign Minister talk and when you listen to the Ayatollah talk, they talk to me in rational, legal terms. What they say usually makes sense. This is not to say I agree with everything they say, but to make the argument that these are sort of raving lunatics — these are people who are foaming at the mouth and just looking for an opportunity to start a war and do great destruction to the United States or to Israel — I just don’t see that.

**Diesen:** No, I agree. I think the image of Iran has been severely manipulated. When I was in Tehran, I almost expected another version of a Taliban. When you just saw people walking outside, women aren’t covered, polite people — you realize how much you’ve been influenced by the media.

On the issue of rationality and nuclear weapons though, does Iran have to be irrational to acquire nuclear weapons? Because I would think there’s a very rational argument for why Iran should get that nuclear weapon. I just want to make it very clear that this is not a normative statement about whether they should get it, but rather just a rational security-maximizing argument.

I hope they wouldn’t because I think it would be a disaster and would encourage regional proliferation. However, if I was advising the Iranian government and I had the security interest of Iran at heart as the first priority, I would see a rational argument for why they would want this ultimate deterrent, especially when you have the most powerful country in the world seemingly seeking to destroy them.

The lesson over the past few decades is that countries who develop these weapons — be it Pakistan or North Korea — they’re safe. The countries who abandoned these programs — be it Iraq or Libya — they’ve been destroyed. This is not a normative argument being made. But wouldn’t there be a rational argument why Iran should get nuclear weapons?

**John Mearsheimer:** I agree 100% with what you said, every word. I can’t add much to it, but I’ll make one or two additional points.

One, Israel has nuclear weapons. Does that mean Israel is irrational? No. It was the strategically smart or rational thing for Israel to do. If I were David Ben-Gurion or I were advising David Ben-Gurion back in the day, I would have told him, “Let’s get nuclear weapons.” Now, that’s not in America’s national interest. I would prefer as an American that they not have nuclear weapons, but from Israel’s point of view, it made eminently good sense. It was the rational thing to do to acquire a nuclear deterrent.

What’s good for the goose is good for the gander. That same logic applies to Iran as you laid it out.

To take this just one step further and go back to Israel: you don’t see Israel or the United States or any other nuclear-armed country for that matter moving to get rid of its nuclear weapons. They happen to like their nuclear weapons and the reason is that nuclear weapons are the ultimate deterrent.

I don’t blame the Israelis for wanting to be the only state in the Middle East with nuclear weapons. It makes perfect sense. My dream is to have an international system where only one country has nuclear weapons — only one country on the planet has nuclear weapons and that’s the United States of America. That’s the ideal situation for us.

But that doesn’t mean the Russians or the Chinese or any other country that has nuclear weapons is irrational because they want nuclear weapons as well.

I would just say that the idea that Iran is irrational because it wants nuclear weapons — it’s a laughable argument, but it’s the kind of discourse that we have in the West. I believe again that much of that is driven by Israel and the lobby.

**Diesen:** Before you expressed some optimism after Trump’s State of the Union speech that he seems to be walking some of this back. In the past it appears that he’s been putting himself in a trap, boxing himself in to some extent because he sent all of these military capabilities to the Middle East. Do you think this is enough to diffuse the whole situation?

**John Mearsheimer:** It might be. That’s the most we can say at this point. It might be. We haven’t talked much about the pressures from the other side and the story that one might tell from the other side, which is the story that leads to war.

The truth is that Trump is under tremendous pressure from Israel. There’s no question about that. We just talked about all those Benjamin Netanyahu visits to the United States. The Israelis really want us to do this.

There’s another dimension to this that bears mentioning. It’s a very sensitive subject, but almost everybody I know believes that Israel and the lobby led us into this war. If it’s a catastrophe, there is a great danger that people will say that the Jews are responsible for causing this war, which is simply not true because huge numbers of Jews oppose this war. The Israel lobby is comprised of Christian Zionists as well as Jews. By no means do all Jews belong to the lobby.

It’s just very important to understand that. Nevertheless, there is a real danger here that if this war goes south in a serious way — we go off a cliff, we hit the iceberg, whatever phrase you want to use — and people see it as a war that Israel and the lobby is principally responsible for, that would cause a wave of anti-Semitism, not just in the United States, but outside the United States as well.

I actually think that a lot of people inside of the lobby and a lot of Israelis and certainly a lot of American Jews understand this danger here. So I think if it looks like we’re going off a cliff, I think the lobby would not put much pressure on Trump to cut a deal with the Iranians because of the potential threat of a massive increase in anti-Semitism as a result of a lost war, a catastrophic war that’s blamed on Israel and the lobby.

**Diesen:** I think you’re probably correct in this. It also has to be said that a lot of the leading critics within the US of Israel are American Jews though. The European elites believe that the only way you can do that is to lick America’s boots. In this case that means lick President Trump’s boots.

As you would expect, the Europeans are following the Pied Piper. The question is: do the Europeans matter in terms of winning this war? The answer is no. Who cares whether the Europeans get involved or not? Maybe they’ll help on the margins, but that just doesn’t matter at this point. President Macron talks like this is 1799 or 1805 where France is under Napoleon and by far the most powerful state in Europe. It’s Europe’s Godzilla and France can do X, Y and Z. It just doesn’t matter very much. This war is going to be settled between the Iranians on one side and the Americans and the Israelis on the other side. So Europe is in real trouble. It started with the Ukraine war and this war just makes a bad situation much worse.
