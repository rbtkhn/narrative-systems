# The 5 Warning Signs That the Iran War Is Escalating

The Iran conflict is no longer primarily a tactical contest

Prof Robert Pape
May 11, 2026

It is gradually expanding as strategic test of whether the United States can sustain order in the Gulf without triggering a wider regional commitment.

After Sunday’s briefing, one question kept recurring in different forms: what indicators actually tell us whether this war is stabilizing or moving toward a larger escalation?

News coverage focuses on the latest strike, the latest Trump statement, the latest Iranian response, or the latest rumor of negotiations through Oman or Qatar.

But wars are rarely determined by isolated tactical episodes. They are determined by whether the underlying political and strategic pressures are widening or narrowing over time.

The central mistake in many discussions of Iran is the assumption that escalation will look sudden and dramatic. Historically, that is not how major entanglements usually develop.

Vietnam in the 1960s, Lebanon in the 1980s, and Iraq and Afghanistan post-9/11 -- all expanded through cumulative commitments that initially appeared temporary, limited, and manageable. The danger signal was not a single decisive event. The danger signal was the gradual expansion of obligations, assets, and political credibility tied to the conflict.

The most important thing to understand about the current phase of the war is that Iran is not primarily pursuing a strategy of punishment. It is pursuing denial.

Accordingly, Tehran does not need to defeat the United States militarily in a conventional sense. It only needs to make stable control of the Strait of Hormuz progressively more costly, uncertain, and politically exhausting over time. That is a fundamentally different strategic logic from the one dominating Washington.

The real indicators of escalation are found in the expanding economic, military, and political systems surrounding the battlefield.

1. Gulf Economic Stress Is Becoming Structural Rather Than Temporary
The first warning sign is persistent economic disruption spreading across the Gulf region itself.

In recent weeks, maritime insurers have sharply increased war-risk premiums for vessels operating near the Strait of Hormuz -- $1,000–$2,200 per million in coverage to $20,000–$120,000 according to reliable reports (basically unaffordable) -- while some shipping firms have rerouted cargoes away from Gulf transit routes entirely. Several energy traders have begun pricing Gulf instability not as a temporary shock but as an enduring operational risk.

Short-term market shocks are manageable. Structural uncertainty is not.

The strategic significance of Hormuz has never depended solely on whether the waterway is physically blocked. Roughly one-fifth of globally traded oil and a substantial portion of global LNG exports move through the Strait. Even intermittent disruption produces cascading effects through shipping schedules, insurance markets, refinery planning, energy futures, tourism investment, and regional capital flows.

This is why denial strategies are so powerful.

Iran does not need to sink large numbers of American warships or permanently close the Strait. Once investors, insurers, and shipping companies conclude that instability is becoming chronic rather than episodic, the economic effects begin compounding independently of daily military events.

The Gulf monarchies are particularly vulnerable to this kind of pressure because their political stability depends heavily on sustained economic normalcy: functioning desalination systems, uninterrupted maritime commerce, tourism expansion, financial inflows, and large-scale infrastructure investment.

Saudi Arabia’s Vision 2030, the UAE’s financial hub ambitions, and broader Gulf diversification projects all assume a relatively predictable regional security environment. A prolonged environment of maritime insecurity directly threatens that assumption.

Wars become strategically dangerous when economic strain spreads faster than military success.

2. U.S. Military Commitments Are Beginning to Expand Horizontally
The second warning sign is the gradual widening of American force commitments.

This process is frequently misunderstood because people tend to imagine escalation as an abrupt, dramatic political decision.

In reality, modern military escalations often occur through “mission creep.” New missions emerge to protect existing missions. Temporary deployments become semi-permanent.

The United States has already expanded naval escort discussions in response to shipping threats in the Gulf while increasing missile defense coordination with regional partners. Additional surveillance assets, logistics preparations, and maritime security arrangements have quietly followed.

The strategic issue is not whether Washington currently plans a major ground war. The strategic issue is whether the existing strategy can achieve its objectives without generating pressure for broader commitments later.

Historically, this is where coercive campaigns become dangerous. When punishment strategies fail to produce political capitulation, leaders rarely interpret failure as evidence that the strategy itself is flawed. More commonly, they conclude that insufficient punishment has been applied. That creates pressure for additional commitments designed to rescue the original strategy.

Air campaigns produce demands for escorts. Escorts produce demands for missile defense. Missile defense produces demands for advisers and logistics personnel. Eventually the line between limited intervention and structural military commitment becomes increasingly difficult to define.

This is one reason escalation traps are so dangerous politically. Once a state publicly commits itself to coercive objectives, withdrawal begins carrying reputational costs of its own. Leaders then become trapped between visible failure and deeper involvement.

And this leads directly to the next indicator — the political language surrounding the war itself.

3. U.S. Leaders Redefine and Enlarge Strategic Goals
The third warning sign is linguistic rather than military. Historically, major escalations are often preceded by subtle but important shifts in official political language. Leaders stop talking primarily about punishment and begin talking about necessity – essentially trying to solve failure by enlarging the problem set.

Phrases such as “freedom of navigation,” “protecting allies,” “regional stability,” and especially “securing nuclear materials” – emphasizing the global benefits of military action -- matter because they expand the perceived scope of legitimate military objectives. Once those concepts enter official rhetoric consistently, the political foundation for broader commitments is already being prepared.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio’s remarks last week fit this pattern closely.

Rubio repeatedly framed the conflict less as a discretionary coercive campaign and more as an international obligation tied to maintaining global order. He argued that the United States “cannot allow Iran to normalize control” over the Strait of Hormuz and described “freedom of navigation” as a principle the United States would continue to defend militarily if necessary.

More significantly, Rubio repeatedly emphasized that any diplomatic resolution must still address Iranian nuclear material buried “deep somewhere,” while warning that a nuclear-armed Iran could permanently hold the global economy hostage through Hormuz.

That language matters because it gradually broadens the perceived stakes of the war beyond retaliation for Iranian behavior alone. Historically, that is precisely the kind of rhetorical shift that often precedes wider and more enduring commitments.

4. Growing Political Fragmentation among Gulf States Previously Unified in Opposing Iran
The fourth warning sign is even more consequential: whether Gulf states gradually begin drifting toward accommodation with Iran rather than dependence on Washington. This is the deeper geopolitical competition unfolding underneath the military conflict itself.

Gulf monarchies do not simply fear Iranian coercion. They also fear prolonged instability and the possibility of being trapped indefinitely between a weakening American security umbrella and an increasingly entrenched Iranian denial capability. If regional elites conclude that permanent confrontation threatens their long-term economic future more than limited accommodation with Tehran, the strategic balance in the Gulf begins shifting fundamentally.

Saudi Arabia is the pivotal state to watch here.

Riyadh’s behavior over the past several years already suggests an emerging hedging strategy rather than full alignment with Washington. Saudi Arabia has been deepening ties simultaneously with China and Pakistan — China as its largest long-term energy customer and investment partner, Pakistan as a critical military and strategic relationship with deep historical ties.

That logic becomes even stronger under prolonged Hormuz instability. The more Gulf instability threatens sustained economic normalcy, the greater the incentive for Riyadh to pursue some form of regional accommodation rather than permanent dependence on an increasingly strained American security architecture.

5. Growing Escalation Instability as Global Economic Costs Mount
Finally, long wars often appear deceptively manageable in their early phases. The Korean War, the Iran-Iraq Tanker War, Vietnam, and post-2003 Iraq all evolved through incremental escalation before eventually becoming structural features of international politics.

That is why I will now track several indicators weekly: Gulf shipping and insurance rates, U.S. force posture expansion, official rhetoric shifts, GCC political positioning, and Chinese diplomatic behavior.

The key question is no longer whether escalation exists -- but whether the conflict is becoming self-sustaining.

One of the most important lessons from long wars is that economic pain alone rarely produces rapid political settlement.

Escalation in Vietnam continued despite severe inflationary pressure and domestic unrest inside the United States. The Iran-Iraq War persisted for eight years despite catastrophic economic destruction on both sides. Russia’s war in Ukraine has continued despite sweeping sanctions and massive battlefield costs.

Once wars become tied to political credibility, regime legitimacy, alliance structures, or broader questions of international order, states often absorb levels of economic damage that outside observers initially assume are unsustainable.

That is the deeper danger in the Gulf today. Policymakers may still assume that rising oil prices, shipping disruption, or market stress will eventually force compromise. History suggests the opposite is often true: prolonged economic pressure can harden strategic commitment rather than weaken it.

One reason strategic surprises occur so frequently is that systems often appear stable until accumulated pressures suddenly become impossible to reverse. That is why, in the weeks ahead, I will continue tracking these escalation indicators closely as the conflict evolves.

—Robert Pape
