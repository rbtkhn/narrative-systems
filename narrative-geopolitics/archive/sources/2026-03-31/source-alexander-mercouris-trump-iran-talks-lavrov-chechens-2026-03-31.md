Good day. Today is Tuesday, 31st March 2026.

Before I proceed with this program, let me remind you again to hit the like button and to check your subscription to this channel. If, once you've watched this program, you decide that you like what you've heard and wish to indicate that fact, please do so.

Over the last 24 hours, we have had a whole succession of extraordinary statements coming either directly from the President of the United States or filtered for us by parts of the US media. All of them reinforce the impression — which I and many others share — that is set out in the title of an article that appeared this morning in the Daily Telegraph. 

That British newspaper, by the way, never failed before the start of the armed conflict with Iran to agitate, insist upon, and demand an attack on Iran — the attack which duly happened on the 28th of February. The title reads: "World recession warning as Trump loses control of Iran war."

The comment about "losing control" seems to originate in a report provided by a British financial institution. We are starting to see opinion in the markets beginning to harden, or at least the first signs of it, that the President of the United States is losing control of the war.

For a US president involved in a war, that is the most disastrous position he can be in. Previous presidents who found themselves in exactly that kind of position were George W. Bush over Iraq and, more damagingly still, Lyndon Johnson back in the 1960s over Vietnam.

This goes directly to a comment made in a recent program we did on The Duran with Jim Webb — former US Marine officer and national security adviser to Robert Kennedy Jr. during his presidential campaign. Jim Webb, who repeatedly and insightfully writes about the military situation in both Iran and Ukraine, said that the United States has lost the military initiative in the conflict with Iran.

The financial community is now beginning to say the same thing. This is inevitably going to have an impact on the developing situation in the financial markets, which in turn will shape the direction of the war.

Trump himself has been issuing all sorts of extraordinary statements. Yesterday he made another truly remarkable Truth Social post. He said that he is in contact with, and in negotiations with, a much more reasonable, much more rational, and much more amenable alternative Iranian government, and that he confidently expects to do a deal with them.

But if this deal is not done, he warned, "fire and thunder" will be unleashed on Iran. All its energy facilities and desalination plants will be destroyed. Kharg Island will also be destroyed. Its energy industry will be destroyed.

To me, this statement again gave precisely the opposite message to the one Donald Trump perhaps thought he was sending. I do not believe that this alternative, more reasonable government — or what Donald Trump would judge to be a more reasonable government — exists in Iran, either in fact or in waiting.

The only government in Iran is the one that holds power there today. When Trump talks about being in discussions with this alternative government and expecting a satisfactory agreement, what he is actually doing is pleading with the existing government of Iran to sit down and negotiate with him and to give him some kind of off-ramp.

That, I am sure, is the conclusion the Iranians themselves will draw. They read all of these comments. They know far better than he does the actual situation within their country. They will see this message as a plea from the President of the United States addressed directly to them.

As for the extraordinary threats President Trump is making, the Iranians will conclude that it is all bluff. They have seen the attacks the United States has already made. They know the size and resources of their country. They will be well informed about the various capabilities the United States actually has in the region. They can read the American media just as well as we can.

If they are not reading those reports about dwindling US military inventories and reductions in precision-guided weapons, their friends the Chinese and the Russians will certainly be telling them.

The Iranians will therefore conclude that they are winning, that the United States (or at least this administration) is losing, and that this message is a sign of weakness. They will respond accordingly — by declining to give Donald Trump the negotiations he is pleading for.

They will say to themselves: "We have the Americans on the run. We have Trump on the run. Let us keep doing what we are doing — keeping the Strait of Hormuz closed, preventing ships carrying oil and other products from leaving the Persian Gulf. Let us wait until energy prices get even higher and fears of a global recession grow greater. Let us continue pounding the Israelis and the American bases. Our strategy is succeeding."

If they persist, in a week's time — when all the threats and bluffs have fallen away — if the Americans still want to talk, the Iranians will be in an even stronger position.

I don't know who is advising Trump, but I can say with absolute certainty that this is how the Iranians — and not just the Iranians, but the Russians, the Chinese, and people across the Middle East — will read this extraordinary Truth Social post.

The Iranians will also be reading the Wall Street Journal, which reports that Donald Trump has told his officials something quite different from the threats in his post. Far from escalating and doing all the terrifying things he warns about, he plans to continue hitting Iran for a further two weeks, inflicting as much damage as possible.

He will talk about the destruction of Iran's ballistic missiles, launchers, and air defense systems. He will pretend the United States is achieving more results than it really is. After all, who in the West keeps detailed inventories of Iranian weapons systems? If Trump claims in two weeks that 99% of Iran's missiles have been destroyed, who can conclusively contradict him?

At the end of that two-week period, he intends to declare victory and stop the war — ending direct US military involvement. As for the Strait of Hormuz, he has apparently told his officials that it can remain closed. The United States is self-sufficient in energy, so it is not directly affected. Others are — the Europeans and the Japanese, who failed to come to America's help. It is for them, not the United States, to sort out the problem and get the Strait open again.

We have seen a follow-up from Secretary of State Marco Rubio, who in far more elegant language has essentially said much the same thing. No longer is this spoken of as a war to change the government in Iran or a regime-change war. The focus, according to Rubio, is on military objectives — where it is difficult for others to flatly contradict the US. Declare victory on those objectives, say the closed Strait is unacceptable, but hint that it is for others (Europeans, Gulf States, Asians) to resolve.

Over the last couple of hours, the President has said more things in that direction: it is for the Europeans, the British, and others who are short of oil to find it where they can. It is not for the United States to sort out their problems in the Persian Gulf. The US is self-sufficient. They are not. They didn't join the battle, so there is no reason for America to pull their chestnuts out of the fire.

These are not sustainable policies. If Donald Trump believes that in two or three weeks he can simply walk away from this conflict, pull all US troops and warships out of the Middle East, and leave the situation unresolved — with the Iranian government still in power and still in control of the Strait of Hormuz — then he is living in a world of illusions.

If he does that, America's Middle East allies — the Persian Gulf States, the Israelis — and its Asian and European allies would all feel abandoned and, in some sense, betrayed. This would be particularly true of Israel, which has been agitating for war against Iran for as long as I can remember and which saw US involvement as a means to achieve regime change or the complete collapse of Iran.

The position of the Persian Gulf States would be even worse. I believe they have been playing a very complex and, in my opinion, duplicitous game. Back in 2023, Saudi Arabia, using Chinese good offices, appeared to achieve a reconciliation with Iran. At the time I took it seriously and discussed it as a diplomatic revolution.

I no longer believe that apparent reconciliation was made in good faith. Looking back, it seems the Saudis went ahead with it because Joe Biden was in the White House. MBS and Biden detested each other. MBS felt bitterly betrayed by Obama and Biden over the Khashoggi affair. He no longer believed the United States would conduct the military campaign against Iran that the Saudis wanted.

Then Donald Trump returned. He was known to be very sympathetic to the Saudis and very hostile to Iran. This was the opportunity to return to Saudi Arabia's long-standing policy of intense hostility toward Iran and support for action to change the political system there.

Far from being a factor for restraint, I believe the Saudis encouraged this war. The reports that MBS urged Trump to attack Iran were true. MBS was not alone. Some of the other Persian Gulf producers — the Emiratis, the Kuwaitis, and others — were also urging Trump to begin the conflict. Without downplaying Israel's role, the encouragement from the Gulf producers should not be understated. It played a significant part in persuading Trump to launch this war.

If Trump now does what the Wall Street Journal says he is thinking of doing — pulling US forces out, declaring victory, claiming he prevented Iran from developing nuclear weapons and destroyed their ballistic missiles, and leaving the Saudis and Gulf producers facing a hostile, undefeated, and still powerful Iran by themselves — the feelings of betrayal among those Gulf producers will be intense.

Though I would argue they have brought it entirely upon themselves, after such a debacle they will not want to trust the United States in the same way again.

There is also the situation with Israel, which will feel angry, and with the Europeans, who will feel abandoned. And we come back to the basic issue of energy prices. If the US simply pulls out, leaving the Strait of Hormuz closed, global energy costs will continue to rise. Because energy prices are fungible, this will eventually cause higher energy prices in the United States itself.

I read this morning in the Financial Times that in some American gas stations, the price of gas is now $4 a gallon. Trump, who is very sensitive to this, will be even more concerned. The US oil reserves are being depleted by the day. Sooner or later, if the Strait remains closed, any US administration would likely be obliged to take measures like those President Carter took in the early 1980s — prohibiting oil exports. Despite talk of a big surplus of natural gas, LNG exports might also be restricted.

That might stabilize prices in the United States, but it would leave the rest of the world in an even deeper crisis and drive global energy prices even higher.

At some point, the people around Donald Trump are going to explain all of this to him. If he is entertaining the plan of simply walking away in two or three weeks, he will be made to understand that it simply cannot be done. As Colin Powell is supposed to have told George W. Bush: "You broke it. You own it."

It seems to me that Trump has only two available courses. The very bad one is to escalate the war — to seize islands in the Strait of Hormuz or perhaps Kharg Island. I have discussed in many places how this looks like a trap. Professor John Mearsheimer, on his Substack, says it looks like a kamikaze mission. Jim Webb, Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis, Joe Kent, and other military voices say the same thing. Any US troops occupying those islands would become, in effect, hostages of the Iranians.

This would set the United States up for an even bigger military debacle with dramatic geopolitical consequences. We must never overlook the human cost — to American soldiers and their families, and the enormous losses in Iran itself.

The other option is to seriously seek talks — not with some fictitious, non-existent "reasonable" Iranian government, but with the actual government in Tehran. They have negotiated with the United States in the past. Perhaps, despite everything, they can be persuaded to do so again.

I do not think it is hopeless. Serious US negotiators should sit down with the Iranians from a clean sheet of paper, forgetting all the ultimatums about stopping nuclear enrichment or ballistic missile production. I was dismayed to see Secretary Rubio still talking about those things. That will only disincentivize the Iranians from negotiating.

The Americans need to say: "We are in this situation. You cannot want this war to continue indefinitely. You may be doing well at the moment, but war is unpredictable and high-risk. The United States could propose this through the Pakistanis, but it would be far more meaningful to use the good offices of China and Russia, which have potential leverage over Iran.

Moscow is ready to provide mediation and other assistance to resolve the crisis in the Persian Gulf through diplomatic means."

Lavrov also criticized, indirectly, attempts by the United States, Israel, and others to draw the Persian Gulf states more deeply into the war. He warned that the US and Israel are trying to prevent normalization between Iran and its neighbors and even to incite members of the Gulf Cooperation Council against Iran.

To me, this looks like advice from the Russians to the Gulf States: stop listening to the Americans and Israelis urging you to get more deeply involved. You have made a critical mistake. Let us help you resolve these issues with Iran. We are prepared to act as mediators between Iran on one side and the Americans and Israelis on the other.

The Russians have significant leverage over the Iranians — probably more at this time than the Chinese. They have been sending supplies (what they call humanitarian goods) to Iran to keep the economy functioning. There is now an all-but-universal consensus that Russia is also providing significant intelligence and military aid.

Forces loyal to Ramzan Kadyrov, head of the Chechen Republic, reportedly described the American-Israeli war against Iran as a religious war and their possible intervention as a jihad — a battle of good versus evil in defense of the Islamic Republic.

I must say straight away that this is a report I have seen only in the Iranian media. I have checked the Russian media and can find no trace of it. The Iranians say there has been such an announcement from Chechen militia units loyal to Kadyrov, but they do not specify the source. The Russian media is not publishing it.

However, it is not impossible. Given the tight integration between Kadyrov's government and the Chechen militias (many of which are integrated into the Russian National Guard), such an announcement could hardly have been made without some kind of green light from Moscow.

Everybody acknowledges that the Chechens are formidable fighters. If the Americans are unwise enough to conduct this "kamikaze mission" of a ground operation against Iran, they could find themselves facing not just the Iranians — some of whose forces are formidable — but the Chechens as well, and behind them, the Russians.

The Russians are extending both the mailed fist and the velvet glove. Lavrov is talking about a potential new world war and a crisis spiraling out of control — exactly the same warnings the Chinese made in that Global Times editorial.

It seems to me that the potential for a diplomatic resolution exists. What needs to be done is for the President of the United States to come to his senses and authorize the right people to make the right contacts.

If the government in Tehran is informed that both Russia and China want Iran to start negotiations with the Americans on the basis of a clean sheet of paper, without fantastic demands from either side, I think it could happen. It would calm global markets and might eventually achieve something meaningful.

Moreover, it should not be that difficult for the United States and Iran to come to some kind of serious long-term understanding. The US has now seen the extent of Iranian power and its ability to shut the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's regime cannot be overthrown. At the same time, Iran does not want permanent war. Both sides have agreed up to now that Iran should not have nuclear weapons.

If one approaches this rationally — something in very short supply — I cannot see why an agreement cannot be reached.

But things are not rational. Trust hardly exists. The president has to make that move, and at the moment he shows no sign of doing so. Given that, it seems far more likely we will see the kamikaze mission Professor Mearsheimer warned about materialize — that disastrous escalation.

Perhaps the Chechens will become involved. Perhaps we will see American troops trapped on islands or facing problems on the ground in Iran. If escalation happens, and if the Americans attack Iranian desalination plants (which produce only about 2% of Iran's fresh water), the Iranians will likely retaliate against desalination plants across the Middle East. We could quickly find ourselves in the biggest humanitarian crisis since the end of the Second World War.

The Iranian Parliament is already debating legislation to pull Iran out of the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. The Iranians say this does not yet mean a decision to develop nuclear weapons, but pulling out would give every appearance of paving the way toward eventual nuclear weapons possession — exactly what North Korea did.

In this huge and dangerous crisis, we can understand why Lavrov is increasingly talking about a potential world war. When he speaks in that way, he is clearly hinting at the possible use of ultimate weapon systems — perhaps not by the United States, but by Israel. People like Colonel Lawrence Wilkerson and Colonel Douglas Macgregor have floated this possibility.

If we reach that scenario, we are in a completely different and far more dangerous world than the one we have lived in since 1945.

The Iranians will not make the first move toward talks themselves. They know it would be treated by Trump and his officials as a sign of weakness. So they will wait for the Americans to do it. The Americans should do it now, before their situation gets weaker and before the overall situation becomes far more dangerous.

Does the president understand this? Will he come to his senses? Are the people advising him doing so? I'm afraid I don't have the answers. I am very worried — extremely worried — that the probable answer is no. I hope it is otherwise. We will just have to wait and see.

In the meantime, the other war — the war in Ukraine — continues. I read a very interesting discussion by an institute in Kiev looking at the conditions of the Russian economy. They provided a far more realistic assessment of the financial benefits the Russians will get from higher oil prices. They estimate that higher oil prices will increase Russian receipts by $750 million a month over what they were receiving before.

If oil prices rise still further over the summer — as they may well do if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed — the Russians could end up making tens of billions of dollars more, more than enough to cover their budget deficits.

On the battle fronts: it looks as if the situation in the Kurakhove area on the east bank of the Oskil River has been resolved and the Ukrainian cauldron there has collapsed. There is more footage from Konstantinovka confirming Russian operations in the built-up and industrial areas in the center of the town.

But the most interesting development is that the Russians seem to be moving quite fast toward the city of Sumy in the Sumy region. A few weeks ago, the Russian general in that area said his troops were 20 kilometers from Sumy. They now appear much closer — perhaps 10 to 15 kilometers. Ukrainian defenses in this area look very weak.

I wonder whether, along with operations in Zaporizhzhia and Donetsk, the Russian summer offensive will also include an operation against Sumy. If Sumy falls, the road to Kiev would lie open.

There was speculation about whether the United States would try to prevent it. I said at the time that the US was unlikely to interfere, and so it has proved.

Another ship, the Seahorse, carrying advanced energy products, also reportedly reached Cuba in early March, unloaded its cargo, and used complex maneuvers and spoofing to keep the US uncertain about its whereabouts. The Russian media claims this happened; I remain somewhat skeptical that the Americans were genuinely uncertain, but the cargo appears to have been delivered.

At normal consumption levels, that delivery would carry Cuba through another month or so. The Cubans can stretch it further by careful management.

This is only an interim solution for Cuba's acute energy crisis. Its over-dependence on Venezuelan oil has been exposed. There are some reports of Mexican deliveries, but the big problem remains that Cuba cannot pay for regular oil shipments.

Cuba now faces a clear choice: use this breathing space bought by the Russians to negotiate a better deal with the Americans (which many in Cuba would prefer), or decide that, seeing how hostile the US remains, it has no realistic choice but to align more closely with Russia and China.

The Russians will not provide oil for free — those Soviet days are gone forever. If Cuba goes with Russia (and China, which is supplying solar panels and batteries), there will be a price. The Russians will want access to the Cuban tourist industry, nickel, rum, cigars, sugar, and changes in how Cuba runs its affairs.

The Cuban leadership must now make that decision and negotiate terms. The days when Cuba could go it alone relying on Venezuelan oil are over.
