# It's Getting Late Early for Trump

## Xi's Beijing Warning Reveals the Geopolitical Costs of America's Failure in Iran

Prof Robert Pape

May 14, 2026

Donald Trump arrived in Beijing needing help.

Not symbolic help. Strategic help.

After months of escalation in the Gulf, Washington now quietly needs China's cooperation to stabilize energy markets, pressure Iran, and prevent wider regional breakdown. But instead of arriving as the confident leader of the world's dominant power, Trump walked into Beijing increasingly weakened by a war he promised would restore American strength.

Xi Jinping understood that immediately.

In meeting after meeting, Xi reportedly emphasized that Taiwan is China's military red line and warned directly against falling into a "Thucydides Trap" - the classic dynamic where a rising power and declining power slide toward confrontation.

That was not diplomatic theory - it was strategic signaling.

## Trump's Escalation Trap Is Now Global

The deeper problem is that the Iran war is increasingly exposing the structural weaknesses underneath Trump's foreign policy.

The original promise was straightforward: rapid coercion would maximize US power, intimidate adversaries, reassure allies, and strengthen American leverage globally.

Instead, the opposite dynamic is emerging - Trump's strategic failure in Iran is diminishing US power, and rapidly so.

Iran absorbed the initial strikes, Hormuz remains unstable, Chinese sanctions enforcement has weakened, and even Iran's negotiators have repeatedly slow-walked or publicly undercut American demands while Washington keeps expanding its objectives.

Germany's Metz and other European leaders have increasingly distanced themselves from the conflict, Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states are hedging, and Asian allies now see American strategic bandwidth being consumed in the Middle East precisely as China accelerates its military and industrial rise.

This is the core logic of the Escalation Trap: When coercion fails, leaders often double down to avoid admitting failure. But every additional commitment consumes resources, attention, credibility, and military capacity needed elsewhere.

Beijing now sees an America trapped inside widening regional commitments while China grows relatively stronger.

## That Changes Great Power Politics Across the International System

Allies do not permanently align with powers simply because of history or shared values. They align with powers they believe are capable, stable, and likely to prevail over time. Once doubts about long-term strength begin spreading, hedging behavior accelerates quickly.

That is why Xi is lecturing Trump in Beijing - and exuding confidence, while Trump is offering platitudes and hoping for minor business announcibles.

China increasingly believes the balance is shifting in its direction - not because America suddenly became weak, but because Trump's Iran strategy is slowly exhausting American leverage faster than Washington realizes.

And America's allies can see it too.
