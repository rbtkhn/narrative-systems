# Victory Narratives Are Not Noise

They Are a Signal That Escalation is Coming

## The Intelligence Trigger — How the Iran War Actually Began

Decapitation wars often begin not with a fully developed strategy but with a fleeting intelligence opportunity. The pressure to strike a high-value leader can overwhelm the slower work of designing a plan for how victory will actually be achieved.

That appears to be how the war with Iran began.

According to reporting circulating across Washington and allied capitals, the United States had quietly assembled a large strike armada in the region days before the attack. Carrier strike groups, long-range bombers, cruise-missile submarines, and Israeli air assets were positioned across the eastern Mediterranean and the Gulf while intelligence agencies monitored Iranian leadership movements.

The deployment was not tied to a fixed timetable for war. It was tied to the expectation that intelligence might reveal a decisive target.

That moment reportedly came when Israeli intelligence tracked the location of Iran’s supreme leader and relayed the information to Washington through a secure communication between Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and President Donald Trump. Within minutes, the long-prepared strike package received the order to execute.

Precision strikes hit leadership compounds, command facilities, and military infrastructure across multiple provinces almost simultaneously. Cruise missiles and stealth aircraft struck hardened facilities while follow-on sorties targeted missile bases, air defenses, and drone launch infrastructure believed to support Iranian retaliation.

Operationally, the results were extraordinary. Iran’s supreme leader was killed. Senior command facilities were destroyed. The opening strikes demonstrated the full technological reach of modern precision warfare.

But tactical brilliance does not answer the strategic question that decides wars:

Does the regime survive?

If the state survives the strike, the war does not end.

It expands.

## Iraq 2003 — The Pattern Democracies Repeatedly Follow

The Iraq war began with the same logic.

On the night of March 19, 2003, the United States launched a sudden decapitation strike after intelligence suggested Saddam Hussein and senior Iraqi leaders were meeting at a compound outside Baghdad. Two F-117 stealth fighters dropped precision bunker-busting bombs while cruise missiles from U.S. ships in the Gulf targeted the site in an attempt to kill the Iraqi leadership before the wider war even began.

Saddam survived. But the war had already started.

Within forty-eight hours the United States launched the massive air campaign known as “shock and awe.” On the first night alone coalition forces flew roughly 1,700 sorties and fired more than 500 cruise missiles against leadership bunkers, command networks, air defenses, and military headquarters across Iraq.

The campaign escalated rapidly. In the first three weeks of the invasion, more than 1,800 aircraft flew over 41,000 sorties and dropped roughly 29,000 tons of munitions while ground forces drove toward Baghdad to destroy the Iraqi army and collapse the regime.

Operationally, the campaign worked exactly as designed. Iraqi military resistance collapsed with stunning speed, and Baghdad fell in less than a month.

Yet the war itself had only begun.

The strategic assumption behind the campaign was that once Saddam’s regime collapsed, political order would quickly follow. But the United States had never fully planned how to create a stable successor regime after the Iraqi state itself was shattered.

The result was insurgency, civil war, and nearly two decades of a quagmire.

This is the recurring danger of decapitation warfare. Early battlefield success can hide the deeper strategic question that decides whether wars end—or expand.

I call this dynamic the Smart Bomb Trap.

## The Precision Illusion Meets Political Reality

Precision warfare creates the appearance that modern technology has solved the central problem of war.

When stealth aircraft, cruise missiles, and real-time intelligence destroy leadership compounds and command networks in the opening hours of a conflict, the results can look decisive.

Political leaders quickly conclude that the strategy is working. The enemy appears disoriented. Military infrastructure burns. Television images show explosions lighting the skyline of the capital.

But these images measure destruction, not enemy political collapse.

Authoritarian regimes — Iran’s more than most — are built precisely to survive the loss of individual leaders. Power shifts to surviving factions, security forces tighten control, and retaliation becomes the primary mechanism for restoring internal legitimacy.

The regime survives. Which means the war continues.

## The Victory Narrative: When Tactical Success Becomes Political Strategy

Once the opening strikes appear successful, political leaders face a powerful incentive to declare that the strategy is already working.

Public confidence must be maintained. Allies must be reassured. Domestic critics must be neutralized. The easiest way to achieve all three is to tell a simple story: Victory is already underway.

In the current war, President Donald Trump has repeatedly emphasized the success of the opening strikes and the destruction inflicted on Iranian leadership and military infrastructure. The war is a “15 on a scale of 1 to 10,” he said yesterday.

The message from Washington has been clear: the campaign has already achieved historic success.

The problem is that victory narratives often appear precisely when the underlying strategic problem remains unresolved. If the war continues — and continues escalating — the victory narrative is not noise. It is a signal.

The victory narrative, in other words, is a powerful indicator that the war has entered a new phase. And it is the moment when major escalation begins – now fueled by both Washington and Iran.

## The Long War Illusion: How False Promises Create Political Losses

Modern presidents often believe that projecting confidence is politically necessary during war. In the short term, it often is.

In the long term, it can be politically fatal.

Escalation traps follow a predictable political sequence. Early battlefield success produces optimistic claims about progress. When the war fails to end quickly, leaders face a growing credibility problem. The temptation then is to double down on promises that the next operation will finally deliver victory.

Failure quietly turns into fear – of loss of approval ratings, of political abandonment by domestic and foreign allies, of, as is common to hear, “losing the narrative.”

The Vietnam War illustrates the pattern with brutal clarity. By late 1967 the administration of Lyndon B. Johnson repeatedly assured Americans that the war was nearing a turning point. U.S. commanders pointed to rising enemy casualties and emphasized that communist forces were weakening under relentless American pressure.

Then came the Tet Offensive in January 1968.

Militarily, U.S. and South Vietnamese forces ultimately defeated the attacks. But the scale of coordinated assaults across dozens of cities shattered the narrative that victory was close. The gap between official optimism and battlefield reality suddenly became impossible to ignore.

Public confidence collapsed.

Johnson’s approval ratings plunged, and within weeks he stunned the country by announcing that he would not seek reelection. What began as an effort to maintain political confidence ended by destroying it.

A similar pattern later confronted Republicans in Iraq. Early assurances that the war would be swift and decisive collided with years of insurgency, sectarian violence, and mounting American casualties. By the 2008 election cycle, frustration with the conflict had become a central political liability for the party that had launched the war.

Victory narratives can sustain a war for a time.

But when the promise of imminent success collides with prolonged conflict, the political backlash can reshape entire elections.

## The Irony of Escalation — When Political Skill Becomes Strategic Blindness

The deepest irony of escalation traps is that they are often created by leaders who are exceptionally skilled politicians.

Presidents are trained to manage narratives, build coalitions, and project confidence. Those instincts are indispensable for winning elections and governing effectively.

But long wars punish those same instincts.

When battlefield reality fails to match promises of quick results, political leaders rarely respond by stepping back and reassessing strategy. The pressures of domestic politics push in the opposite direction. Doubt appears dangerous. Admitting uncertainty risks weakening alliances and emboldening adversaries.

So, the narrative of success becomes stronger precisely when the strategic situation becomes more fragile. This is the moment when the escalation cycle begins to tighten.

First comes the failure of the initial strategy to produce rapid victory. That failure generates fear of political backlash, of appearing weak, that admitting uncertainty will erode public support. Doubling down – hoping tactical success will somehow result in a magical strategic victory -- becomes attractive as the political solution to the strategic problem.

Each new tactical destruction of a target – satellite images of bombs blowing up enemy ships, missile launchers, buildings labeled as command bunkers – fill our screens and all framed as the steps that will finally break the enemy’s will.

When strategic success does not come and the war goes on, repeat: More televised strikes. More pressure. One more push. This often works for weeks.

But wars do not end because leaders repeat the language of victory. They end when strategy actually solves the political problem that produced the conflict in the first place. Or, as in Vietnam, Iraq and Afghanistan – when leaders finally withdraw, rather than face more years of seemingly endless quagmire.

This is the political element of the Escalation Trap that precision warfare can conceal – the moment when major escalation becomes most likely.

And it is the moment when the Smart Bomb Trap begins to close.
