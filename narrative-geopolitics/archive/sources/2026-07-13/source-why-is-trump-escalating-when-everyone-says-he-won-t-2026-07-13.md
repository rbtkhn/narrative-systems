Why President Trump may now be politically more likely—not less likely—to escalate the war.

Two Engines of Escalation
For months, many observers assumed the conflict would end once one side accepted military reality. But military defeat rarely ends wars by itself. Wars end when the principal combatants reach a common understanding of the new balance of power. Iran, Israel, and the United States are nowhere close to that point.

Recent weeks reveal two distinct—and mutually reinforcing—pathways toward further escalation.

The first is strategic escalation. Iran’s Revolutionary Guard insists only Tehran will control the Strait of Hormuz and determine the conditions for commercial shipping. U.S. Central Command continues preparing to guarantee freedom of navigation. These objectives cannot both be achieved simultaneously.

The second is revenge escalation. Millions filled Iranian streets beneath red flags symbolizing vengeance. More important than the symbolism was the message delivered by Iran’s new Supreme Leader, who called on supporters around the world to avenge the deaths of Iran’s leaders and thousands of Iranian “martyrs.” Revenge is no longer simply rhetoric. It has become an independent political objective.

Most analysts are treating these as separate problems. They are not. My argument is that they are beginning to interact in ways that fundamentally change the logic of the war.

Together these two engines create a far more dangerous conflict than most commentary recognizes. Even if military operations temporarily slow, the strategic and political incentives driving escalation remain intact and reinforce each other.

America’s New Objective: From Punishment to Reversing Defeat
When the war began, the Trump administration sought to coerce Tehran through punishment. The objective was straightforward: destroy military assets, eliminate senior leaders, and force Iran back to the negotiating table on American terms.

Punishment failed. Iran survived—and then seized effective control of the Strait of Hormuz.

President Trump next pursued a different strategy. The June Memorandum of Understanding sought to exchange economic benefits for reduced tensions in the Gulf. As Vice President J.D. Vance explained, the goal was not only to restart diplomacy but also to create the political appearance that freedom of navigation had been restored. That would allow Trump to avoid owning a visible strategic defeat while easing growing pressure on the global energy market.

That strategy also failed.

Rather than permit commercial shipping to resume without its explicit authorization, Iran chose continued military pressure around Hormuz. Tehran denied President Trump the face-saving exit he needed while preventing oil flows from returning to normal.

Washington now faces a different political reality. Iran’s continued control over the Strait of Hormuz increasingly appears less like a temporary disruption than a permanent American strategic setback. As long as Tehran can determine the conditions under which energy moves through one of the world’s most important maritime chokepoints, every passing week reinforces the perception that President Trump has failed – putting at risk support from his most loyal supporters.

Domestic politics magnifies these pressures. According to the Reagan Institute’s nationwide Summer Survey, conducted May 26–June 3, 51 percent of self-identified MAGA Republicans favor replacing Iran’s government, while only 25 percent prefer a negotiated settlement. Most Republicans overall express similar preferences.

Economic pressures reinforce the political ones. Although the administration hoped the June MOU would stabilize global energy markets, commercial disruption around Hormuz continues to keep oil inventories under pressure and uncertainty elevated. The expected economic dividend from diplomacy has yet to materialize – oil inventories are running down and at about the same rate as before the MOU. Accordingly, President Trump still faces the risk he himself acknowledged, that he could become the next Herber Hoover.

These two pressures—domestic demands for a stronger response and the continued deterioration of the global energy outlook—are steadily changing Washington’s incentives. The goal is no longer simply how to punish Iran. It is increasingly how to reverse the strategic defeat at Hormuz before both the political and economic costs become even greater.

Confirmation of CNN’s new satellite footage that Iran is now working at critical nuclear sites would only make the incentives great for at least a symbolic victory before the midterms.

Iran’s New Objective: From Survival to Revenge
Iran’s political objectives are also expanding -- again.

The first phase of the war was about survival. After the decapitation of much of its senior leadership, Tehran’s overriding goal was preserving the regime and preventing military collapse.

The second phase was about ambition. Once Iran demonstrated that it could contest control of the Strait of Hormuz, it began using that leverage to pursue something larger: a new regional security order centered on Iranian influence stretching from Lebanon and the eastern Mediterranean through Iraq and the Gulf to Yemen and the Red Sea.

This week, Iran added a third objective: revenge.

This does not replace Iran’s earlier objectives of regime survival and regional ambition. It adds a new political objective that leaders will now have to satisfy alongside them, even when doing so increases the risks of further escalation.

Hours after President Trump warned that any assassination attempt against him would trigger overwhelming American retaliation, Iran’s new Supreme Leader issued a nationally broadcast statement declaring: “We pledge to avenge your pure blood and the blood of all the martyrs of these two wars... This vengeance is what our nation is demanding, and this must definitely be done.” The statement continued: “Soon individuals among the freedom-seekers across the world will each carry out part of this divine mission.” It concluded that this mission “depends neither on my personal existence nor on that of other officials... Whether we are present or not, it will come to pass.”

These are not simply emotional words. These statements have the structure of what terrorism scholars describe as a classic call to action.

The statement by Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei vowing to revenge was widely broadcast by state and regional networks. The Iranian Intelligence Ministry also released an official decree stating that vengeance is “a national demand”. Globally, the statement went viral on social media because it directly triggered an immediate, high-stakes digital confrontation with U.S. President Donald Trump.

For more than twenty-five years I have studied suicide terrorism and international political violence. The greatest danger is often not centrally directed operations ordered from the top, but inspired attacks carried out by individuals or small networks responding to a broader political appeal. The Supreme Leader’s message came after what Iranian media and CNN described as historic funeral participation involving tens of millions across Iran and Iraq. By explicitly calling on “freedom-seekers across the world,” he transformed grief into political mobilization.

The closest historical parallel occurred in 1989, when Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini issued a fatwa calling for the execution of author Salman Rushdie for his novel The Satanic Verses. The directive stated: “...I inform all the valiant Muslims of the world that the author of the book entitled The Satanic Verses, which has been compiled, printed, and published in opposition to Islam, the Prophet, and the Quran, as well as those publishers who were aware of its contents, are sentenced to death.” The author and publishers were in fact attacked.

Although not identical, Iran’s Supreme Leader’s mechanism is strikingly similar: create a narrative that encourages sympathizers outside the battlefield to become participants in the conflict.

The main difference is that the strategic implications of this call to action would be significantly greater.

The Self-Reinforcing Spiral to Stage 3
The two engines of escalation – growing American pressure to reverse defeat and Iran’s new objective for revenge-- are beginning to reinforce one another.

The Iran war is beginning to resemble a positive feedback system in which each side’s military actions increase the other’s political incentives to escalate further.

Every additional American bombing campaign kills more Iranian leaders, soldiers, and civilians. Military, those strikes may degrade capabilities. Politically, they also expand the pool of people whom Iran now portrays as martyrs whose deaths demand vengeance. That strengthens the legitimacy of the Supreme Leader’s call for revenge and expands the pool of sympathizers from which future attacks could emerge.

If even one limited revenge attack succeeded—against an American official, U.S.-linked business executives in an overseas hotel, a commercial airliner, or civilians perceived as supporting the United States or Israel—the political effects inside the United States would almost certainly outweigh the military damage.

Public pressure for decisive retaliation would intensify immediately. President Trump, already under pressure to reverse what many supporters view as a strategic defeat at Hormuz, would face even stronger incentives to authorize larger military operations – including now politically difficult ground options.

Each side therefore creates the political conditions for the other’s next move.

This is no longer a simple cycle of tit-for-tat retaliation. It is an escalation spiral – where violence ratchets upward on its own momentum. Every bomb, every revenge attack, and every response drive the next decision to move up the rungs of the escalation ladder – because the alternative becomes less and less politically acceptable.

That is precisely how the Iran war is likely to enter Stage Three of the Escalation Trap.

The Military Already Preparing for Stage Three
Military preparations now point beyond an air campaign — preparing for Stage 3 ground operations in the Escalation Trap framework.

Although the US struck more than 450 targets inside Iran last week, today the pattern of force deployments and target selection suggests preparation for a broader set of military options if bombing fails to restore freedom of navigation through Hormuz.

The Pentagon has moved substantial additional combat power into the region. Two carrier strike groups now operate in and around the Gulf of Oman, placing carrier aviation within immediate striking distance of Iran’s southeastern coastline. Strategic bombers, fighter aircraft, aerial refueling tankers, and additional naval forces have also surged into theater. At the same time, a Marine Expeditionary Unit has been repositioned closer to the Gulf of Oman, giving U.S. commanders a rapidly deployable force should limited ground operations become necessary.

These are not risk-free deployments. By moving major naval assets closer to Iran, Washington has knowingly accepted substantially greater exposure to Iranian missiles, drones, mines, and fast-attack craft. States do not normally incur these additional military risks unless they believe the potential strategic gains justify them.

Realists often distinguish between cheap talk and costly signals. These movements are costly signals, since US naval forces are now well within range of Iran missiles and drones.

Equally important is what the United States has been bombing.

The overwhelming majority of recent strikes have targeted precisely the military capabilities that would have to be suppressed before any effort to reopen the Strait of Hormuz by force: coastal surveillance radars, command-and-control networks, anti-ship missile batteries, drone launch sites, naval facilities, ammunition depots, and other elements of Iran’s anti-access defense system.

These are not random targets. They are the classic preparatory targets for establishing local air and maritime superiority before amphibious or littoral operations. U.S. joint doctrine has emphasized this sequence for decades.

None of this means President Trump has decided to launch ground operations.

It does mean the military is increasingly creating the operational conditions that will allow him to do so quickly if the President orders it. That distinction matters. Decisions about war often occur only after the military option has become operationally feasible.

Meanwhile, U.S. Central Command has publicly declared that it is “ready to ensure freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz.” The gap between that declared objective and the military preparations now underway is becoming steadily smaller.

Three Indicators I am Watching
The purpose of the Escalation Trap is not to predict every tactical event. It is to recognize when political incentives become stronger than military restraint. Three indicators now matter most.

First, watch Iran’s revenge campaign. If Tehran continues amplifying the Supreme Leader’s call through state media, religious leaders, proxy organizations, and social media networks, revenge is becoming an enduring political objective rather than a temporary emotional response. At that point, the risk of inspired attacks against Americans, U.S.-linked firms, and other perceived supporters of Washington or Israel should no longer be treated as a remote possibility but as a growing operational risk.

Second, watch where the United States is bombing. If strikes expand well beyond Iran’s coastal defenses and increasingly target missile depots, drone production, logistics networks, transportation infrastructure, and command facilities deep inside the country, Washington is no longer merely protecting navigation through Hormuz. It is systematically reducing Iran’s ability to sustain prolonged military resistance.

Third, watch preparations for littoral operations. Additional amphibious shipping, Marine units, engineering forces, mine-clearing assets, and reports—however fragmentary—of increased reconnaissance by U.S. special operations forces along Iran’s southeastern coast would signal preparation for military options extending beyond an air campaign. Most of these activities will remain classified, but changes in force posture, satellite imagery, and occasional operational leaks often reveal when military planners are making ground operations a realistic option rather than merely a contingency.

Together these three indicators will reveal whether the political logic of escalation has become stronger than the remaining restraints against Stage Three ground operations—when the next major escalation may already be underway before most governments, markets, and commentators recognize it.
