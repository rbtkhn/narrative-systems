Why the Funeral Stiffens Iran’s Resolve
The funeral for Iran’s Supreme Leader is more than a religious event. It is the political culmination of the most sweeping foreign decapitation campaign against a modern state since the Second World War.

The facts speak for themselves.

The opening phase of the U.S.-Israeli air campaign killed Iran’s Supreme Leader, his wife, 3 other members of his immediate family, and approximately 50 of the country’s highest political, military, intelligence and religious leaders within weeks.

Although no analogy is perfect, the scale and status of the Iranian leaders killed since February 28 would be structurally equivalent to a catastrophic, simultaneous loss of the U.S. President, First Lady, First Family members, and the top tier of the U.S. Executive Branch, the Pentagon, the Intelligence Community, and numerous Cabinet officials in the line of succession.

During the weeks of bombing, up to 3700 Iranians have been killed, including an alleged airstrike that hit the Shajarat al-Tayyiba Primary School in the southern city of Minab, killing 168 civilians, including schoolgirls as young as seven years old.

Proportionately, the death of 3,700 Iranians is roughly equivalent to approximately 14,000 deaths in the United States – or the equivalent of more than four 9/11s (which killed ~ 3,000 people in 2001).

By any measure, Iran has endured a level of punishment unprecedented in the history of the Islamic Republic—far beyond the cumulative impact of decades of economic sanctions, covert operations, assassinations, and episodic air strikes. The immediate military objective of the U.S.-Israeli campaign was straightforward: decapitate Iran’s leadership, disrupt its command structure, and cripple Tehran’s ability to wage war. The political consequences are only now becoming visible.

The central mistake is to assume that punishment of this magnitude inevitably weakens a society’s willingness to resist. History often points in the opposite direction.

When air campaigns fail to produce immediate political collapse, they frequently transform national trauma into nationalist mobilization. Grief becomes anger. Anger becomes resolve. Resolve increases a society’s willingness to endure economic hardship, absorb further military punishment, and support leaders who promise not compromise, but retaliation. The political center of gravity shifts from minimizing costs to imposing them on the enemy.

The American experience after 9/11 illustrates this mechanism with unusual clarity. The attacks did not produce a frightened public demanding strategic restraint. They produced an overwhelming surge of national unity and determination. Hundreds of thousands volunteered for military service. Americans accepted enormous financial and human costs in the name of preventing another attack.

Public support extended not only to destroying al-Qaeda’s sanctuary in Afghanistan but also to invading Iraq—a country that had not carried out the attacks—because many believed that striking potential future threats was preferable to waiting for another catastrophe.

Today, after two decades of costly wars, many Americans conclude those decisions were mistakes. But that is hindsight. At the time, national trauma fundamentally altered political incentives. The dominant instinct was not “never again.” It was: they hit us once; we must hit back harder.

That same political logic is now becoming visible in Iran. The funeral is not simply a display of mourning. Throngs cheered when hearing slogans and phrases that explicitly included, “Kill Trump,” “Revenge, Revenge,” “Kill Bibi,” and “We won’t let you go!”

This is evidence that the war has generated a broad nationalist response that emboldens the regime to become more aggressive. Leaders confronting millions of citizens demanding fierce payback are less likely to moderate their objectives than leaders confronting a fearful or divided public.

That does not automatically make Iran militarily stronger. It makes its leadership more willing to sustain confrontation, demand greater concessions, and accept higher risks. In the coming weeks, those political changes may matter more than any individual military development.

The New War Aim: Ending America’s Military Position in the Gulf
The funeral does not simply make Iran more stubborn. It changes what Iranian leaders believe they can realistically achieve.

For months, Tehran’s immediate objective has been to survive, preserve its nuclear program, and exploit growing leverage in the Strait of Hormuz. Massive nationalist mobilization makes a more ambitious objective increasingly plausible: using the political momentum created by the war to weaken—and ultimately end—the permanent American military presence on Iran’s southern frontier.

This would not represent a sudden ideological shift. Iranian leaders have called for reducing American military influence in the Gulf for decades. What has changed is their assessment of political opportunity. Millions of Iranians publicly celebrating resistance rather than demanding accommodation make it easier for the new leadership to pursue larger objectives while accepting the risks that accompany them.

The immediate negotiating consequences are likely to appear across several issues. Iran will almost certainly harden its insistence on maintaining domestic uranium enrichment, since abandoning the nuclear program after enduring such enormous sacrifice would now appear politically impossible. Tehran is also likely to demand permanent recognition of its authority to regulate commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, transforming temporary wartime leverage into an enduring strategic advantage.

But these issues increasingly serve a larger objective.

The central demand is likely to become the gradual removal of American military power from the Gulf itself.

Iranian officials have repeatedly argued that Gulf states hosting American forces inevitably become legitimate military targets during any future conflict. In late June, Iran’s Foreign Ministry declared that lasting regional security can only be achieved through cooperation among regional states “without external intervention,” warning that continued foreign military deployments would only deepen instability across West Asia. A few days later, Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi sharpened the point, arguing that “outsiders cannot even protect themselves” and that genuine regional peace requires eliminating external interference.

These statements reveal something important. Tehran is no longer simply demanding concessions from Washington. It is appealing directly to the Gulf monarchies. The message is straightforward: the future security of Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia depends not on larger American military deployments, but on reducing them.

But the strategy is broader than coercion alone. It also offers positive incentives. On July 4, Iran’s ambassador to China announced that countries friendly to Iran would receive “special treatment” under a new maritime fee structure for the Strait of Hormuz. Although directed broadly at commercial shipping, the logic readily extends to Gulf governments themselves. States that continue serving as platforms for American military power should expect higher economic costs. States that distance themselves from Washington can expect lower costs and more stable commercial relations.

That distinction matters because Iran’s greatest source of leverage is no longer simply the ability to threaten shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. It is the ability to discriminate. Tehran can impose the highest economic costs on states that continue supporting the American military presence while offering lower transit fees, more predictable shipping access, and greater commercial stability to governments that reduce or eventually eliminate U.S. bases. Instead of presenting Gulf rulers with a binary choice between peace and conflict, Iran can present them with a choice between becoming preferred regional partners—or remaining frontline targets.

Iran has not yet explicitly applied this principle to U.S. basing rights. But the strategic logic points in that direction. If Tehran is prepared to reward “friendly” states economically, the most valuable political concession it could seek in return is the gradual removal of American military bases from their territory.

August: When Every Incentive Points Toward Confrontation
For months, I have argued that Iran’s period of maximum leverage would arrive in August. The funeral does not change that timeline. It makes the political consequences of reaching it far more dangerous.

The reason is simple. Every week the United States is drawing down the Strategic Petroleum Reserve to cushion the global economy from disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz. As of June 19, the SPR had fallen to 326 million barrels—the lowest level since the early 1980s—and continues declining as emergency releases offset tightening global supplies. At current drawdown rates, the reserve is on course to approach its congressionally mandated operational floor of roughly 243 million barrels by the middle or latter part of August, precisely as hurricane season begins threatening Gulf Coast production. Meanwhile, commercial inventories remain well below historical norms, and international agencies do not expect supply conditions to normalize until well into 2027 at soonest.

That is the strategic window for Tehran’s maximum leverage.

By August, Iran is likely to possess three forms of leverage simultaneously: growing nationalist confidence at home, continuing influence over maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, and an opponent with steadily diminishing economic room for more disruption.

If Tehran concludes that this combination offers its best opportunity to reshape the regional balance of power, it will have every incentive to press its most ambitious political demand yet: reducing the permanent American military presence in the Gulf.

That places President Trump in an increasingly difficult position.

Withdrawing US bases under Iranian demands would signal another major strategic retreat just months before the midterm elections. Rejecting them risks provoking precisely the maritime confrontation over Hormuz that the Strategic Petroleum Reserve is becoming less able to absorb. Neither option is attractive. Both increase the incentives for Washington to challenge Iranian leverage before it reaches its peak.

This is why I believe August is becoming the decisive month of the war.

The greatest danger is not simply that Iran closes the Strait of Hormuz. It is that both Washington and Tehran conclude they cannot afford to let the other enter September with greater leverage than they possess today. When both sides believe time favors immediate action rather than continued delay, the probability of confrontation rises sharply. That is the strategic logic now driving the Iran War.

The Three Signals That Will Tell Us If August Is Breaking
The coming weeks will not be decided by speeches or summit announcements. They will be decided by three observable indicators that reveal whether Iran is translating its growing political confidence into greater strategic leverage.

First, watch Iran’s demands—not just negotiations. The key question is whether Tehran begins explicitly linking maritime access, preferential shipping rules, or long-term stability in the Gulf to reducing the American military presence. If Iran starts offering neighboring states economic advantages for becoming “friendly” while increasing pressure on governments hosting U.S. forces, the strategic contest will have shifted from nuclear diplomacy to the future balance of power in the Gulf.

Second, watch shipping through the southern Strait of Hormuz. Continued subnormal traffic, rising insurance costs, or renewed interference with commercial shipping would indicate that Iran still possesses—and intends to preserve—the economic leverage needed to press harder political demands.

Third, watch Lebanon. If pressure on Hezbollah intensifies or fighting expands again, it will signal that Washington and Jerusalem are attempting to weaken Iran’s regional position before Tehran reaches its period of maximum leverage in August.

These are the indicators I will be tracking every week.
