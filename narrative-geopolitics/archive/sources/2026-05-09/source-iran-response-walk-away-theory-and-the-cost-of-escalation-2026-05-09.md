# Iran Response, Walk-Away Theory, and the Cost of Escalation

## Cleaned Transcript Notes

Daniel Davis frames the episode around the expectation that Iran will answer the latest US proposal soon and says there is essentially no chance Iran will accept the demands. He introduces a “walk-away theory” as the alternative to escalation: stop digging deeper and exit the crisis before it worsens.

He then criticizes Mark Thiessen and other Iran hawks as advocates of resuming “Project Freedom,” striking Hormuz-related targets, Iran’s energy infrastructure, and Iranian leadership. Davis argues that assassination-based strategy is irrational and would not force capitulation, citing Iran’s long experience under assassination pressure and Hezbollah’s survival after extensive Israeli decapitation efforts.

Davis says reports from Israel and allied sources point toward a return to war if diplomacy fails, including demands for broad strikes on Iran’s energy system within 24 hours of renewed conflict. He says any Arab support for that approach would be self-destructive because Iranian retaliation would likely target Gulf energy infrastructure and regional bases.

He emphasizes that recent damage reports from US strikes showed precision hits on Iranian targets, which he takes as evidence that Iran can strike exactly what it aims at. On that basis, he argues that energy infrastructure warfare would be catastrophic for everyone involved, including ordinary people in the Gulf, Israel, and the United States.

The episode also criticizes other hawkish voices, including Mark Levin and Ken Timmerman, for portraying Iran as a revolutionary regime bent on conquest and nuclear breakout. Davis rejects that as false, says Iran has not pursued a nuclear weapon, and argues the regime-change / escalation narrative is being sold to Trump through influential advisors.

He then highlights an economist’s warning that Middle East oil production has already collapsed by roughly 14 million barrels per day and that the shock could push the global economy into recession or depression-level damage. He says the world needs the war to stop quickly because physical shortages and supply-chain damage are already spreading.

In the last part, Davis critiques the addition of an FDD-linked negotiator, Nick Stewart, to the US team, arguing that the choice looks like a poison pill because Stewart publicly favored acting quickly against Iran and assumed escalation could be managed. Davis presents this as another sign that the administration is being steered away from the walk-away option and toward further war.
