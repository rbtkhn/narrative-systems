# Victory Day, Kiev Threats, Hormuz Standoff, and Britain’s Local Elections

## Cleaned Transcript Notes

Alexander Mercouris opens by marking 9 May 2026 as Victory Day in Russia and the former Soviet Union, emphasizing Soviet sacrifice in the Second World War and arguing that the West often forgets the scale of the Soviet and Chinese contribution.

He then turns to the immediate Ukraine risk. He says Russian warnings about a possible Ukrainian attack on the Moscow Victory Day parade were taken seriously, including the implied threat of Oreshnik strikes on central Kiev. He argues that the EU treated those warnings as bluff, but that the White House ultimately did not. In his account, the Americans pressured Zelensky into accepting a short ceasefire, paired with a prisoner exchange, so the parade could proceed without incident.

Mercouris presents that outcome as a Russian success, but also as a rare case where the Americans acted prudently. He says Russian threats were reinforced by public warnings from the Russian defense and foreign ministries, and that people in Kiev appear to have taken the risk seriously, as shown by reports of cars leaving the city. He also says Russian drones flew over central Kiev in daylight, highlighting the weakness of Ukrainian air defenses and the reported shortage of Patriot interceptors.

On the Victory Day parade itself, he stresses the symbolism. Putin’s speech, in his reading, linked the Soviet victory in the Great Patriotic War to the current war in Ukraine, with repeated emphasis on the unity of the Soviet people and on the sacrifices made to liberate Europe from fascism. Mercouris says this was also a reminder to Ukrainians that they were part of the same Soviet whole as Russians. He notes the presence of leaders from Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan, Belarus, and Malaysia, and highlights the participation of North Korean troops as a sign of the deepening Moscow–Pyongyang relationship.

He describes several pre-parade meetings in Moscow, including Putin’s meetings with the head of Kamaz, the head of Rostec, and members of the Security Council. He says these meetings touched on Russian industrial capacity, civil aviation development, drone attacks on Russian targets, and the need for counter-drone measures. Mercouris says the Russians are increasingly focused on rapid-response air defense, including short-range missiles and cannon systems, and suggests that a new Russian drone-tracking system from Almaz-Antey could be decisive once deployed.

The program then shifts to the Persian Gulf and Hormuz. Mercouris says commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz appears to have stopped and argues that this is more damaging to the global economy than to Iran. He says the Americans have attacked Iranian-linked tankers and Iran has retaliated by seizing third-party vessels, but he treats the broader stalemate as the main story.

He also discusses an Economist article claiming Russia has supplied Iran with a three-stage plan for defeating a US ground operation. He focuses especially on the reported transfer of fiber-optic drone technology, which he says would make Iranian drones unjammable and much more effective against a ground assault. He frames this as further evidence of deep Russian-Iranian military cooperation.

### Segment 2 - Iran Middle East Russia

Mercouris says the United States has sanctioned three Chinese companies for publishing satellite information about US bases in the Middle East, and he expects China to react. He argues Trump’s upcoming trip to China is necessary because China now has leverage over Iran and because US, Russian, Iranian, and Chinese interests are all converging in the wider crisis.

### Segment 3 - Russia Gulf Security

He then moves to Gulf security architecture, saying Saudi Arabia and Qatar may now be considering Chinese-Russian proposals for a regional security arrangement that would reduce US basing rights and potentially return freedom of navigation in the Gulf without the United States as the guarantor.

The final section turns to Britain. Mercouris says the local election results show a collapse of the old establishment parties. He says Reform won the largest share of the vote, the Greens emerged as the second strongest party, and Labour, the Conservatives, and the Liberal Democrats all weakened. He reads this as a larger nationalist and anti-establishment realignment across England, Wales, and Scotland, and suggests it may be the beginning of the end of the United Kingdom.
