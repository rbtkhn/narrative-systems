# Hormuz, UAE, Ceasefire, and the New Security Architecture

## Cleaned Transcript Notes

Nima Alkhorshid and Seyed Mohammad Marandi open with the latest naval incidents around the Strait of Hormuz. Marandi says the United States initiated the violence by striking tankers and killing sailors and civilians, while insisting that no Iranian naval boats were hit. He says Iranian drones struck US ships but that the strikes were calibrated to avoid serious escalation.

Marandi argues the real strategic result is that ships linked to the five Gulf states that joined the war against Iran are being kept out of the Strait. He says ships from friendly states, or states not involved in the war, can still transit, and he treats the failure of the US operation to force passage as proof that Trump’s approach is failing.

On the question of escalation, Marandi says Iran still expects a major assault and that the armed forces remain on full alert. He says the Iranians think an attack could happen at any hour, though he suggests Trump may hesitate until after the coming China meeting. He also repeats that the Iranians are preparing for possible assaults from US forces, terrorist groups in northern Iraq, and groups on the Pakistan border.

When Alkorshid presses him on reported casualties, Marandi says the Americans killed one person on a small cargo vessel and injured people on fishing boats that came to help, but that no Iranian naval boats were hit. He uses this to argue that the US and its allies are spreading false claims about the engagement.

The discussion then turns to the economic impact of the Hormuz standoff. Alkorshid cites reports that no commercial ship passed through the Strait in the last 24 hours and that Europe is running low on jet fuel. Marandi says the US response to Iran’s proposal should not be expected quickly because Iran is under attack and will not be intimidated into speed.

The interview moves to the UAE and the Gulf Arab states. Marandi says the UAE has long been the most closely aligned with Israel and is now being treated by Iran as different from the other GCC states. He says Iran will now treat the UAE more like northern Iraq, meaning that if anti-Iranian groups operate from its territory, they will be struck. He adds that this policy shift reflects the reality that the UAE has hosted Israeli and US military activity against Iran.

Marandi also says Bahrain remains hostile and that support for the resistance across the region is broader than sectarian identity. He argues that many ordinary people across the Gulf, including in Kuwait and elsewhere, now support the resistance and that the old Sunni-Shia divide is collapsing.

The conversation returns to Lebanon and Gaza. Marandi says Iran will not accept any ceasefire framework that does not include both fronts and the lifting of the siege on the Strait of Hormuz. He says Lebanese institutions aligned with the United States are trying to strangle the resistance financially, but Hezbollah remains steadfast and effective.

Later, Alkorshid raises the question of enriched uranium leaving Iranian territory. Marandi says plainly that Iran will not accept any demand to ship enriched uranium out of the country. He says Iran will answer the US proposal on its own timetable, and that the response will not be one the Americans like.

In the later part of the interview, Marandi says the Strait of Hormuz has permanently changed. He says Iran will now control the Strait and that a new regional security architecture should exclude the United States entirely. In that vision, the Persian Gulf states themselves would need to build a security structure that protects all of them, rather than relying on US power.

He closes by saying that Russia, China, and Iran have all grown stronger together after the war, that their relations with Iran have deepened, and that the presence of figures like Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff on the US side only proves how much control Zionist interests have over US policy. He says Iran is not counting on a real peace agreement with the US, only on a ceasefire that ends the fighting, restores shipping, and pushes the US military back out of the region.
