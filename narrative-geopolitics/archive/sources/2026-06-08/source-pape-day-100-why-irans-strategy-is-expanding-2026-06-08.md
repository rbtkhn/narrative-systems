# Day 100: Why Iran's Strategy Is Expanding – and the Ceasefire is Breaking Down

### Escalation Trap This Week: Staying Ahead of the Risks that Matter.

Prof Robert Pape  
Jun 08, 2026

Today marks Day 100 of the Iran War.

It also marks a dangerous new phase: Iran and Israel are once again exchanging direct strikes against each other's homelands, raising the possibility that the already fragile April ceasefire may now collapse altogether.

One hundred days ago, many observers expected a short campaign. Decapitation strikes against Iran's leadership, attacks on nuclear facilities, and mounting economic pressure were all expected to produce rapid political consequences inside the Islamic Republic. Instead, the regime survived, the opposition remained fragmented, and Iran preserved enough military and nuclear capability to continue the struggle.

Those outcomes matter because wars rarely remain fixed around their original objectives. When an opening campaign fails to produce a decisive political result, states begin adapting. Military survival creates new possibilities. New possibilities create new ambitions.

The important question now is how Iran intends to convert survival into regional power—and what that means for the future of the war and the global economy.

The biggest surprise of Day 100 is that Iran increasingly appears to be gaining leverage rather than losing it.

That helps explain why the ceasefire itself is beginning to break down.

In this week's Escalation Trap briefing, I explain why Iran's strategy is expanding—and identify the three indicators I will be watching most closely over the coming weeks to determine whether the war is entering an even more dangerous phase.

## Iran's ambitions are growing from survival to gaining leverage in the Gulf

The clearest evidence arrived in the last twelve hours.

For the first time since the April ceasefire began, Iran fired missiles directly at Israel. The attack followed Iranian warnings that Israel must halt operations against Hezbollah strongholds near Beirut. Tehran threatened escalation. Then it acted.

States preparing to surrender generally narrow the battlefield. States that believe their leverage is growing often widen it.

Although Iran is not a regional hegemon today, it increasingly appears to be acting like a state that believes the balance is shifting in its favor.

Three developments point in that direction.

First, Iran's rhetoric is becoming more ambitious.

Iranian leaders are no longer talking primarily about survival. They are demanding concessions, declaring negotiations deadlocked, and threatening to expand the war from Hormuz to the wider region and beyond.

Last week, senior adviser Mohsen Rezaei gave a prominent public interview where he wasn't pleading for relief or warning of regime collapse. He declared that negotiations were deadlocked, demanded the immediate release of $24 billion in frozen assets from the United States, and warned that if the war resumed Iran would expand the conflict beyond Hormuz into the Indian Ocean, Red Sea, Bab al-Mandab, and Mediterranean. Iran is talking like a state that believes its leverage is growing, not shrinking.

Second, Iran's military behavior is becoming more ambitious. Iran has escalated attacks against the American military infrastructure that underpins U.S. power in the Gulf, striking targets in Kuwait and Bahrain and even disrupting operations at Kuwait International Airport. At almost the same time, Iran threatened renewed missile strikes against Israel if it did not pull back from its military assault on Hezbollah strongholds near Beirut – which Israel, under US pressure, did temporarily. And in the last 12 hours, Iran followed through with its threat and fired missiles at Israel for the first time since the April ceasefire began two months ago.

States preparing to surrender generally narrow the battlefield. Iran is widening it.

Third, this shift in Iran's ambitions is occurring at exactly the moment Tehran sees its leverage improving. Global oil inventories are being drawn down, strategic reserves are shrinking, and the International Energy Agency is warning that stockpiles could reach critically low levels before peak summer demand (more below).

This context matters. Tehran is speaking and acting in ways that suggest it increasingly believes that time is working in its favor rather than Washington's.

For the first one hundred days, Washington relied heavily on accumulated cushions: strategic petroleum reserves, commercial inventories, missile stockpiles, naval superiority, and the expectation that economic pressure would eventually translate into political pressure on the regime. Those cushions remain substantial. But they are not unlimited. As inventories decline, costs accumulate, and negotiations stall, time may not be operating in the way many American policymakers originally expected.

This is where the escalation trap logic matters greatly. The failure of the opening campaign produced an unexpected strategic consequence. Iran survived. Hormuz remained contested. Negotiations stalled. And with survival increasingly secured, Tehran now faces a different problem: how to convert wartime endurance into long-term political advantage.

Success changes incentives.

States fighting for survival behave differently from states that believe survival has already been achieved. Once immediate dangers begin receding, leaders stop asking how to avoid defeat and start asking how to exploit opportunities created by the conflict itself. Military survival creates leverage. Leverage creates opportunities. Opportunities expand objectives.

Nor are they isolated objectives. Tehran has increasingly linked separate theaters into a new strategic architecture and coherent bargaining framework. Progress in negotiations with Washington is now connected to events in Lebanon. Pressure against Hezbollah is treated as pressure against Iran. Issues that once appeared geographically separate are increasingly being folded into a single strategic equation.

Pressure on Gulf states hosting American forces has also become more explicit. From Tehran's perspective, the Gulf monarchies occupy a pivotal position in the regional balance. American military power ultimately depends on access, basing rights, and political consent from local governments. If that consent weakens over time, American influence weakens with it.

The result is a dynamic familiar from the history of great-power competition. A state that expected quick success discovers that the conflict is becoming protracted. The weaker state survives. Survival creates leverage. Leverage creates ambition. Ambition expands the political objectives at stake.

The central issue is no longer whether Iran survives.

Increasingly, it is whether survival can be translated into regional power—with global effects.

## Seeing the Bottom of the Barrel

During the first weeks of the war, the United States and much of the world absorbed the shock with resources accumulated over decades. Strategic petroleum reserves were released. Commercial inventories cushioned disruptions. The underlying assumption was straightforward: temporary reserves would buy enough time for military pressure and diplomacy to produce political results.

Instead, Day 100 finds many of those buffers under increasing strain.

The renewed exchange of direct strikes between Iran and Israel only reinforces that pressure. Markets had increasingly assumed that the April ceasefire would hold and that the most dangerous phase of the conflict had passed. The return of direct attacks between the two countries raises the possibility that the war is entering a new phase just as inventories and emergency reserves are becoming thinner. A conflict expected to stabilize is instead becoming more unpredictable – including destabilization in the Red Sea, the major bypass allowing 3-4 million barrels of oil a day to escape Hormuz.

Global energy inventories are now being consumed faster than policymakers expected only weeks ago. In early June, the International Energy Agency warned that continued stock draws could push inventories toward critically low levels before the peak summer demand season. Since March through May, approximately 500 million barrels have already been withdrawn from global stocks – over a third of total reserves -- requiring the largest coordinated emergency release in the history of the IEA.

The pressure has become increasingly visible inside the United States itself. During the week ending May 29, commercial crude inventories fell by another eight million barrels while the Strategic Petroleum Reserve declined by an additional eight million barrels, producing a combined sixteen-million-barrel weekly draw. American crude stocks have now fallen for eight consecutive weeks, and strong exports to Europe and Asia have increasingly turned the United States into the supplier of last resort for countries attempting to compensate for disrupted Gulf flows.

These developments matter because wartime cushions are designed to absorb temporary shocks, not permanently replace normal systems of trade and production. Strategic reserves can stabilize markets for a period of time, but every emergency release reduces the margin available for future crises – and increases the risk from weather events.

The same logic applies to advanced munitions. Cruise missiles and interceptors can be expended rapidly in combat, but industrial production rebuilds inventories far more slowly than wars consume them.

Stockpiles can only be spent once.

Here's the crucial point: As cushions shrink, leverage changes.

States possessing large reserves can afford patience. States watching reserves decline begin searching for exits.

Time therefore does not operate neutrally.

If Tehran concludes – as is likely -- that American economic and military buffers are becoming thinner while its own domestic political position remains stable, the incentives for compromise diminish. Success itself begins generating new ambitions. A state that originally fought to survive may gradually conclude that it can demand larger concessions, impose greater political costs, and reshape the regional balance more fundamentally than appeared possible at the beginning of the war.

Success breeds ambition when the other side's cushions begin disappearing. And renewed escalation only reinforces that perception.

## Another Missed Off-Ramp

Last week President Trump extended the naval blockade and signaled that major concessions would remain off the table until at least Labor Day. The decision reflected an increasingly visible assumption inside Washington: time favors the United States.

That assumption is understandable – at a high level of abstraction. The Iranian economy has suffered enormous damage. Its oil exports have been disrupted and inflation has accelerated. From this perspective, additional time simply means additional pressure.

But this logic rests on a particular theory of coercion.

The theory is familiar. Economic pain imposed on society generates dissatisfaction. Dissatisfaction produces pressure on political leaders. Political leaders then moderate their objectives or accept concessions they previously rejected.

In practice, however, foreign-imposed punishment almost always proves weaker than expected.

History provides numerous examples of this problem. Consider Iraq.

During the 1990s, the United States and its allies imposed one of the most severe economic coercion campaigns in modern history against Iraq. Combined sanctions, oil restrictions, and nearly continuous no-fly-zone operations reduced Iraqi GDP by almost half during the first year and kept the economy depressed for more than a decade. Yet the regime survived. More importantly, growing economic hardship did not produce political accommodation. After 12 years of Iraq's GDP down by 47%, Saddam Hussein had become increasingly resistant to outside inspection and confrontation with the United Nations intensified rather than diminished – until the US marshalled a mass army that ultimately conquered the country.

A similar pattern emerged during the first Trump administration. The "maximum pressure" campaign sharply reduced Iranian oil exports and pushed the economy into recession. Iranian GDP contracted significantly and inflation accelerated. Yet the result was not greater political leverage. Instead, Tehran gradually abandoned the constraints of the nuclear agreement and accelerated uranium enrichment. Economic pain produced retaliation rather than capitulation.

The current blockade rests on the expectation that larger economic losses will finally produce a different outcome. Estimates suggest that Iran's economy could contract by roughly ten percent this year, substantially exceeding the damage imposed during previous sanctions episodes. But after one hundred days there are still no signs of a mass uprising capable of threatening the regime. Instead, reports from inside Iran point to intensified repression, widespread arrests, prison abuses, and the further concentration of power inside the security apparatus.

Over the last several weeks, Iranian authorities violently suppressed student demonstrations, expelled and suspended dozens of students, and continued a broader wave of political executions against protesters. International human rights organizations report that the Iranian regime is fast-tracking the executions of political prisoners and demonstrators, resulting in 95 executions in the last half of May and dozens of deaths following grossly unfair trials and forced confessions extracted under torture.

The renewed exchange of strikes between Iran and Israel over the past twenty-four hours suggests that this dynamic may already be unfolding. Instead of creating incentives for compromise, the combination of blockade pressure, military attrition, and stalled negotiations appears to be producing renewed escalation. Both sides increasingly seem to believe that additional pressure will improve their bargaining position. In effect, each side is betting that time favors itself. That is precisely the condition under which wars often become self-reinforcing.

The historical pattern is difficult to ignore.

Punishment often hurts societies faster than it weakens governments.

Authoritarian regimes rarely experience social pain directly. Instead, they experience political threats. When external pressure increases, governments frequently respond not with compromise but with repression. Economic hardship becomes evidence of foreign aggression. Internal opposition becomes easier to portray as disloyal. Security institutions acquire greater authority. Nationalism substitutes for prosperity.

The first one hundred days of the Iran War increasingly point in that direction.

This does not mean economic pressure is irrelevant. It means the political effects of pressure depend on how societies and governments adapt to it. Punishment does not automatically translate into concessions. Most often foreign punishment produces nationalist resistance.

That distinction matters because bargaining leverage ultimately depends on expectations about time.

If Washington believes additional months will steadily weaken Tehran while Tehran believes additional months will steadily increase its leverage, both sides acquire incentives to continue the struggle. The result is an Escalation Trap: each side interprets the other's resistance not as evidence that its strategy is failing, but as evidence that even more pressure is required.

Negotiations become harder, not easier. The search for compromise gives way to the expectation that one more round of pressure will finally produce the desired political outcome.

The renewed exchange of strikes between Iran and Israel suggests that this process may already be underway.

Another off-ramp disappears.

## Three Indicators that I'm Watching

The next phase of a war almost never arrives with a formal announcement. Strategic transitions usually become visible first through small changes that, viewed individually, appear unrelated. Only later does the pattern become obvious.

Over the coming weeks, I will be watching three developments in particular.

The first concerns new government responses to declining cushions. What I will be watching over the next several weeks is not simply the price of oil. Prices fluctuate. Inventories reveal stress. But the most important indicator may be political rather than economic.

The key question is whether emergency measures are quietly becoming normal policy. The first reserve release after a crisis is extraordinary. The second is reassurance. By the third or fourth round, governments are no longer managing a temporary disruption. They are adapting to a new environment.

That is why I will be watching less for dramatic price spikes and more for subtle policy decisions: additional IEA stock releases, new SPR exchanges, extensions of emergency authorities, relaxed stockpile requirements, and discussions about maintaining extraordinary measures through the summer. The International Energy Agency has already warned that inventories could approach critical levels before peak summer demand arrives.

Those decisions matter because they reveal expectations. Governments do not repeatedly reach for emergency tools unless they have begun to believe the emergency itself may endure – and are settling in for long haul consequences.

The second indicator concerns the gradual expansion of military objectives through what increasingly appear as routine tit-for-tat exchanges. Escalation rarely occurs through a single dramatic decision. It often proceeds through a rolling series of seemingly limited adjustments in what each side considers a legitimate target. Indeed, the US bombing of North Vietnam famously called "Rolling Thunder" gradually escalated over many months.

During the past several days, Israeli strikes against Hezbollah positions near Beirut were followed by Iranian missile attacks against Israel itself—the first direct Iranian strikes since the April ceasefire. Israel then responded with attacks inside Iran. What began as separate disputes involving Lebanon, Israel, and Iran increasingly appears to be operating as a single interconnected conflict.

None of these targets represented the original object of the war.

That is the point.

Wars rarely become regional because leaders suddenly decide to make them regional. More often, separate theaters gradually become linked together. Lebanon becomes connected to Iran. Iran becomes connected to Gulf basing. Gulf basing becomes connected to Hormuz. What were once distinct political disputes increasingly become part of a single strategic contest.

That is why I will be watching less for the number of strikes than for evidence that the boundaries are disappearing. If events in Lebanon increasingly trigger responses from Iran, if attacks on Gulf infrastructure become connected to events in Israel, or if Red Sea and Hormuz disruptions become integrated into the same bargaining framework, it would suggest something important.

The most dangerous sign is not more fighting.

It is the disappearance of boundaries within conflicts.

Wars usually become regional before governments admit they have become regional.

Air and naval campaigns often widen through categories. First come launchers. Then sensors. Then command networks. Soon economic infrastructure becomes intertwined with military operations. Ports servicing naval forces become legitimate targets. Re-constituted drone and missile launch areas become legitimate targets. Energy facilities sustaining naval, drone, and missile forces and their logistics become legitimate targets. Each individual step appears defensive when viewed in isolation. Collectively, they reveal a war that is slowly redefining itself.

For that reason, I will be watching less for the number of strikes than for changes in the kinds of targets being struck. New target categories often reveal strategic change before official policy does.

Wars usually widen before governments admit they are widening them.

The third indicator concerns the geographic scope of the conflict itself.

Wars rarely expand all at once. More often they spread by linking previously separate theaters into a single strategic contest. What begins as a dispute over one issue gradually absorbs others. Actions taken in one arena trigger responses in another. The battlefield expands not because leaders formally decide to widen the war, but because each side increasingly treats separate conflicts as parts of the same struggle.

The events of the past twenty-four hours illustrate the danger. Israeli strikes against Hezbollah targets near Beirut were followed by the first direct Iranian missile attacks against Israel since the April ceasefire. Israel then responded with strikes inside Iran itself. What had appeared to be separate fronts are increasingly operating as a single system.

This is precisely the dynamic that often precedes major escalation.

During the early stages of a conflict, governments attempt to compartmentalize disputes. Lebanon is treated separately from Iran. Shipping security is treated separately from negotiations. Regional militias are treated separately from state actors. Over time those distinctions begin to disappear.

That is why I will be watching less for the number of strikes than for evidence that additional theaters are becoming linked together. If events in Lebanon increasingly trigger responses from Iran, if attacks on Gulf infrastructure become connected to events in Israel, or if Red Sea and Hormuz disruptions become integrated into the same bargaining framework, it would suggest something important.

The Red Sea will be especially important to watch. Markets have gradually adapted to disruptions around Hormuz on the assumption that roughly 3 to 4 million barrels of oil per day moving through the Bab al-Mandab and Red Sea corridor would continue to flow and potentially increase over time. That assumption has helped contain the broader economic consequences of the war.

If the conflict begins spreading into the Red Sea, that assumption could quickly break down.

For that reason, I will be watching several operational indicators closely: renewed Houthi missile or drone attacks against commercial shipping, attacks against tankers transiting the Bab al-Mandab Strait, increases in maritime insurance rates, emergency rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope, and new American or allied naval deployments designed to escort shipping traffic. The most important early indicator is any sign tankers are refusing to follow their normal shipping routes.

The significance of these indicators is not simply military. They would signal that two separate energy corridors are becoming part of the same war.

Once that occurs, oil markets would no longer be pricing a localized disruption in Hormuz. They would be pricing the possibility that both major routes connecting Gulf energy supplies to world markets are becoming contested simultaneously.

The most dangerous sign is not more fighting. It is the disappearance of boundaries between conflicts.
