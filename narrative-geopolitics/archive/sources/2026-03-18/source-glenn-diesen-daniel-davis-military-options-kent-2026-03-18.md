# Daniel Davis × Glenn Diesen — Iran’s aims, Hormuz, military options, Joe Kent (operator transcript)

**Glenn Diesen:** Welcome back to the program. We are joined today by Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Davis, a four-time combat veteran and host of the very popular YouTube channel Daniel Davis Deep Dive. Thank you as always for coming back on the program.

**Daniel Davis:** Always a pleasure to be here, Glenn. Thanks for having me back.

**Glenn Diesen:** Trump has declared victory over Iran and it seems he might be planning to go home. Yet the Iranians show no indication that they are ready for him to declare victory and end the war. What do you see as Iran’s current aims or goals to end this conflict?

**Daniel Davis:** As far as I can ascertain, Iran has two primary objectives, and everything else centers around those two.

Number one is to survive. They have to continue to endure politically and maintain military viability. They don’t have to match the United States or defeat us militarily. They just need to remain viable — meaning they sustain some level of missiles and drones and continue to strike either our assets or Israeli assets in the region. Politically, they must survive repeated assassination attempts. Some have succeeded and more will follow. They need to maintain control of their armed forces, the IRGC, police, and regular army.

As long as they hold that together for an extended period, they can live to fight another day. Time will hurt them, but if time is on anyone’s side, it is more on Iran’s than on the United States. We have a much greater sense of urgency. They can endure for months. We cannot.

Their second objective is to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed. They have many ways to do this: sea mines, missiles, drones, speedboats, drone boats, submarines, underwater torpedoes, and more. The longer they keep it closed, the more pressure builds on the Trump administration. Oil is already hovering around $100 per barrel. If it doesn’t open soon, it will climb higher again. That puts economic pressure on the US and our allies — pressure that Iran does not face to the same degree.

**Glenn Diesen:** When you say time is on Iran’s side, it makes sense with US weapon shortages, logistics strain, weakened bases, weary Gulf states, and the energy crisis. But what about the non-material side? Trump is facing growing political hardship at home. Today we saw Joe Kent, whom Trump appointed as Director of the National Counterterrorism Center, resign. He said there was no immediate threat from Iran and blamed the Israeli lobby, while noting that America’s closest allies are sitting this out and refusing to help open the Strait. How significant is this in Iran’s calculations — the political undoing of President Trump?

**Daniel Davis:** This is exactly part of that time pressure. Iran doesn’t face these internal problems. Even many who opposed the regime have now rallied around it, seeing this as an attack on the Iranian nation, not just the government. The idea that the US and Israel are trying to “liberate” them for democracy isn’t believed on the ground. They’re rallying around the flag.

Trump, on the other hand, has serious problems. He can’t get allies on board. Every one of them has refused to send ships into what they see as a suicide mission. The former vice chief of staff of NATO compared it to booking passage on the Titanic after it hit the iceberg. Trump’s response this morning on Truth Social — basically saying “we don’t need you anyway” — comes across as petulant.

Then you have Joe Kent’s resignation. He was the number two at the Office of the Director of National Intelligence. He stated clearly that Trump was lied to, that Israel manipulated the situation, that the media ran a misinformation campaign about an “imminent threat” that didn’t exist, and that this is not “America First.” Trump dismissed him, but misrepresented what Kent actually said.

All of this puts real pressure on Trump. If he can’t solve this militarily or diplomatically, and Iran refuses to release its grip on the Strait, the oil price will keep rising. The Gulf allies are also taking hits — losing revenue, facing strikes, and struggling with imports of food and essentials. They will pressure Trump too. If this drags on for a month or two and gas prices keep climbing, even Trump’s own supporters will start bailing and demanding an end. That’s why the clock is ticking much louder for Trump than for Iran.

**Glenn Diesen:** What do you think they are discussing in the White House in terms of military solutions? Is it simply intensifying attacks on Iran? Is boots on the ground realistic? Can they open the Strait by force? We’re hearing about Marines deploying — based on the numbers, it’s unclear what they’re for. Could there be an effort to control parts of western Yemen to keep the Bab el-Mandeb Strait open, since Saudi Arabia relies on the Red Sea?

**Daniel Davis:** Trying to secure the Bab el-Mandeb Strait in Yemen is probably the most feasible option they have. A growing part of me thinks all the talk about Qeshm Island is disinformation, because sending a carrier strike group up there would be suicidal — it would have to run a gauntlet of hundreds of miles under Iranian fire. Our leaders aren’t that stupid.

But even taking something in Yemen wouldn’t solve the problem. The Houthis can launch missiles and drones from anywhere. There’s no single point to control.

I fear they’re grasping at straws. In the Situation Room and Pentagon, they must be pulling their hair out because there are no viable military options to force the Strait of Hormuz open. Iran has too many tools: submarines, underwater missiles, speedboats, drone boats, aerial drones, ballistic missiles, and artillery. You can’t eliminate all of that.

Continued assassinations won’t work either. The country is too big and they have deep benches of replacements. They have half a million active forces and can mobilize a million. We don’t have enough ordnance to bomb them into the Stone Age, and history shows populations endure that kind of campaign.

Our carriers are already wearing out. The Gerald R. Ford is heading home after a fire reduced its capabilities. We’re expending munitions far faster than we can produce them, draining inventories needed for other contingencies. Senior generals know this and will be asking: toward what end?

**Glenn Diesen:** Are the weapons being used against Iran largely the same ones that would have gone to Ukraine? Patriots are an obvious example, but what about the rest?

**Daniel Davis:** Most of it is different, but we’re still draining key stocks. Air defense interceptors (what Zelensky desperately wants) are gone. JASSMs, Tomahawks — we’ve already used hundreds. We can fall back on dumb bombs and other air-to-ground munitions for a while, but sustained high-tempo operations will run those down too.

**Glenn Diesen:** How feasible is boots on the ground? Would it be on Iranian territory? The Iranian coastline is vast, mountainous, and populated by 93 million people. What would the mission even be — occupy the entire coast? How quickly could it be done?

**Daniel Davis:** It would take a minimum of six months just to assemble, train, and equip a force of that size. Our entire active Army and Marine Corps combined is only about 475,000. You’d need 400–500,000 troops for even a theoretical chance, and even then it wouldn’t work.

Look at a topographical map of Iran: nearly impregnable mountain ranges on the western side. Any invasion would be funneled into killing zones that heavily favor the defender. It would be like the Italian campaign in World War II — a bloody, slow slog with massive casualties. Even if their soldiers were only a fraction as capable as ours, fighting on home soil they would chew us up.

No serious general today would willingly execute that mission. Many would refuse on moral and professional grounds. So we can take large-scale ground invasion off the table.

Air power isn’t working. Naval power has already done what it can. We are in a militarily unattainable situation. President Trump will eventually be forced into a negotiated settlement — and because of our actions, Iran now has real leverage it never had before.

**Glenn Diesen:** Last question: How will this war affect US-Israeli relations? In a quick war, objectives aligned. In a long war, Israel may want to keep weakening Iran, but that runs directly against US interests — especially the goal of pivoting away from the region. Are there growing tensions, at least at lower levels? Joe Kent’s statement seems significant.

**Daniel Davis:** For those of us who have long argued this serves Israeli interests far more than American ones, Joe Kent’s letter is important. He’s an 11-time combat veteran who said the quiet part out loud: Israel manipulated Trump into a war that was not America First. That’s much harder to dismiss.

We’ve already suffered 200 US wounded and 13 killed in just 17 days — in a war not tied to our national security. As casualties mount and economic pain spreads, more Americans will question it. Support for Israel remains strong in some circles, but it’s eroding in others. If this drags on, we may see meaningful degradation in unconditional support, and Trump could come under pressure to distance himself.

The White House citing Ben Shapiro as validation while dismissing Joe Kent doesn’t look good optically. One is a combat veteran who lost his wife in Iraq; the other is a commentator. Who are people going to believe?

**Glenn Diesen:** Thank you very much for taking the time.

**Daniel Davis:** Thanks for having me on. Appreciate it.
