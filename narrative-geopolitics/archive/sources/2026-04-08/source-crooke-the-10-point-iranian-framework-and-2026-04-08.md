# the 10-point iranian framework and the empowering of iran

https://conflictsforum.substack.com/p/the-10-point-iranian-framework-and The 10-Point Iranian Framework and the Empowering of Iran
Alastair Crooke, 8 April 2026
Conflicts Forum
Apr 08, 2026
∙ Paid
What was agreed by Iran on 7 April is not a ceasefire per se, but rather a brief cessation in military actions in order to provide the space in which to test whether meaningful political discussion with the US is possible or not.

The US however, has accepted Iran’s National Security Council’s 10-point framework as the agreed ‘anchor’ to possible discussions, due to begin in Islamabad on Friday —

“[Trump] said he had received a 10-point proposal from Iran which was a “workable basis on which to negotiate”“.

The 10-points, in effect, represent Iran’s pre-conditions to any move to a more substantive ceasefire.

The Framework emanates not from fringe Iranian political figures talking with outside mediators, but from out of the core leadership in Iran. After considerable discussion within the Security Committee of Iran’s National Security Council (NSC), the Framework was finalised and proposed to the Supreme Leader. The latter discussed the text, amended some aspects, and gave it his imprimatur. The Framework is therefore a serious and grounded statement of Iran’s position.

Clearly, the present situation nonetheless remains very tentative. The contradictions between the 10-point plan and that of Witkoff’s 15 point exposition from the US side are stark -- and possibly the tension between the two cannot hold.

Furthermore, the key counter-party to this tenuous framework — Israel — claims it was not consulted, and is furious at the US acquiescence to holding discussions based around the Iranian framework that it views as a strategic defeat for the US, and therefore for Israel too.

The 10th point in Iran’s Framework calls for the halting of military action on all fronts — including Israel’s conflict with Hezbollah in Lebanon. Yet Netanyahu now states that he will not observe this on the Lebanon front (where the war continued today with massive Israeli attacks across Beirut and south Lebanon).

Iran has already announced that it is preparing to launch “deterrent operations against Israeli military sites in the occupied territories” in wake of today’s attacks by Israel on Hizbullah:

“In Tehran, there is a prevailing belief that the continuation of the [Israeli] regime’s attacks on all fronts, despite the agreement, is evidence of either the United States’ inability to control Netanyahu or CENTCOM granting the Zionist entity freedom of action” (FARS News).

Today’s breach alone might be enough to collapse the Framework, were Israel to not commit to the terms, and instead use the Lebanon card to surreptitiously sabotage the Framework.

Leading Israeli intelligence commentator Ronen Bergman queried earlier today --“Is Lebanon Included in the Ceasefire Agreement? Depends on Who You Ask”:

“Senior [Israeli] security sources told [Yedioth Ahornot] that the ceasefire announced by President Trump includes Lebanon. The Prime Minister of Pakistan also confirmed the details. But [Israel’s] Prime Minister’s Office denies it”.

Trump’s shift in stance from threatening ‘civilisational obliteration’ to a last-minute agreement to call off threatened incapacitating strikes on all Iranian civil infrastructure – and to accept Iran’s 10-point plan – seemingly followed upon the collapse of his ‘Plan B’ over the weekend:

Trump’s hope was for a Venezuelan-type, snap Special Forces operation (In-boom-Out) to seize a substantial part of Iran’s 430 Kg of enriched uranium, which Grossi, Head of the IAEA, has said is stored in a tunnel in the Isfahan nuclear facility. He would then claim ‘victory’ before markets opened after the Easter break.

The mooted Special Forces seizure raid became entangled in efforts to rescue the crew of a US F-15 that had been shot down by Iranian air defences, not far from Isfahan. The rescue helicopters launched to extract the pilots were damaged by ground fire (seen trailing smoke), but reached base outside Iran. However, the Special Forces aircraft dispatched for the snap Isfahan raid were ambushed by Iran and crashed heavily.

The Isfahan operation thus collapsed with a heavy loss of aircraft. It is unlikely now that such a sneak raid could be repeated.

‘Plan B’ for a quick in-out ‘win’ for Trump had thus evaporated, leaving him with the options of a major military escalation that would extend beyond his self-imposed 4-6 week deadline to conclude the Iran war ahead of (already fraught) midterm elections.

For Iran, were their preconditions to be accepted by the US (a big ‘if’), this was viewed in Tehran to provide an opportunity to reshape the regional geo-political balance of power.

Crucial to this analysis is continued Iranian control of Hormuz, the lifting of all sanctions, the eviction of US military forces and US bases from the region, and the ending of the concomitant petro-dollar dominance across the Persian Gulf.

In short, Iran sees its present resilience (which has shocked and overturned many expectations) as the opportunity to escape the ‘cage’ in which it has been entrapped for seven decades. This was envisaged as Iran resuming its position as an acknowledged strategic power.

For Israel, by contrast, nothing could be worse as an outcome: Iran emerging as the more powerful state in the region, and with their former Gulf allies – and many countries in Asia too — forced to come to terms with an empowered Iran.

Historically, evidence is that ceasefires which incorporate substantial contradictions usually erode -- and then erode in an accelerating fashion.

In the Russian-Ukrainian context, similar contradictions have prevented any agreed regional security architecture from emerging, as Europe insisted on pursuing war on Russia through its Ukrainian proxies.

Likewise, any agreement to bring to an end America’s long attrition and siege of Iran may similarly be frustrated as America’s proxy — Israel — continues to pursue its long war for hegemony over the region.
