## Findings

### SR-01

Priority: material  
Location: opening, “The archive in a well,” and closing return  
Issue: repeated references to “the center” compress a layered administration
into a direct center-to-county relation. Excavated evidence instead shows
several offices, divided responsibilities, and local decision-making inside a
hierarchy.  
Recommended revision: use “higher offices” where the sentence concerns document
travel; retain “central government” only where the cited scholarship makes that
specific claim.  
Source or reason: Staack 2023, especially pp. 147–150 and conclusion; You 2016,
pp. 461–506.  
Disposition: accepted.

### SR-02

Priority: material  
Location: opening and “A line an empire could read”  
Issue: saying Qin unification made “people” and “goods” comparable confuses
persons and things with their administrative representations.  
Recommended revision: name representations, measures, written forms, and
official acts as the objects made comparable.  
Source or reason: the documentary evidence concerns categories and records,
not ontological sameness among governed people.  
Disposition: accepted.

### SR-03

Priority: material  
Location: “A cleaner script, a thinner record”; HE01-C04  
Issue: Chen establishes disappearance from ordinary written use and the gradual
elimination of nonconforming graphs. The lecture's claim that contexts became
less recoverable is an inference about the surviving record.  
Recommended revision: explicitly identify this as the lecture's inference and
avoid making “absence” sound like a class of evidence.  
Source or reason: Chen 1997, official English abstract.  
Disposition: accepted.

### SR-04

Priority: material  
Location: “The Han afterlife”; HE01-C05  
Issue: commanderies and counties did not simply continue as an exclusive Han
territorial template. Early Han kingdoms and changes in commandery authority
are essential to the lecture's own continuity-versus-sameness distinction.  
Recommended revision: name the coexistence of kingdoms and commanderies and add
peer-reviewed scholarship on Qin commandery structure.  
Source or reason: You 2014 abstract; You 2016, *Bulletin of IHP* 87.3.  
Disposition: accepted; a human specialist should still recommend the best
full-length Han institutional source before publication.

### SR-05

Priority: material  
Location: “What this does not prove”; HE01-C07  
Issue: the statement about later Han explanations of Qin collapse is accurate
in outline but lacked a precise claim location.  
Recommended revision: locate Sima Qian's introduction of Jia Yi's analysis and
the attack-versus-holding argument in *Shiji*, Qin annals, items 61–70,
especially 68–70.  
Source or reason: Chinese Text Project, Qin First Emperor annals.  
Disposition: accepted in dossier.

### SR-06

Priority: minor  
Location: “A line an empire could read”  
Issue: “preserve records longer” is a functional possibility not demonstrated
by the cited Qin evidence and risks turning survival into intended effect.  
Recommended revision: retain portability and comparison; remove the durability
claim.  
Source or reason: source-fit discipline.  
Disposition: accepted.

### SR-07

Priority: minor  
Location: “The archive in a well”  
Issue: “received, checked, routed, stored” sounds like an exhaustive
reconstruction of workflow. Staack directly supports production, processing,
and archiving.  
Recommended revision: use the source's narrower vocabulary.  
Source or reason: Staack 2023, introduction.  
Disposition: accepted.

### SR-08

Priority: minor  
Location: throughout  
Issue: “negotiation” can imply reciprocal bargaining not demonstrated by the
document-layout evidence.  
Recommended revision: use “adaptation” for the rule/practice relationship.  
Source or reason: Staack demonstrates pragmatic adjustment and tolerated
variance.  
Disposition: accepted.

## Requested Response

Overall decision: `pass-with-revisions`  
Most consequential strength: The lecture refuses both perfect-uniformity and
simple-decline narratives while keeping retrospective text and excavated
practice in distinct evidentiary roles.  
Most consequential weakness: The rhetoric occasionally centralizes agency and
turns a theory of recoverability into a direct empirical property of the Qin
evidence.  
Missing rival reading: Standardization may preserve otherwise inaccessible
administrative activity; moreover, variance in Liye documents may reflect
tolerated flexibility and differentiated scribal training rather than loss.
The commentary already contains the first point and should retain Staack's
training explanation as a live alternative.  
Required source addition: A full-length, peer-reviewed treatment of early Han
territorial administration should replace or supplement reliance on an English
doctoral-thesis abstract before publication.  
Publication recommendation: remain non-public until an independent early-China
specialist checks the revised C04 and C05 language and a fresh reader completes
the value test.
