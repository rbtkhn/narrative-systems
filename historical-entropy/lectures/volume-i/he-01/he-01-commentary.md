# he-01 Commentary

## Neutral Summary

The lecture argues that Qin–Han standardization created administrative order
and historical entropy at the same time. Common categories, written forms, and
document routines increased what a central administration could compare and
move. The same filters reduced the visibility of regional forms and everyday
variation. Excavated practice complicates the official image of uniformity,
and early Han reuse is treated as adaptation rather than unchanged continuity.

## Rival Readings

The strongest rival reading is not that standardization caused no loss. It is
that common forms can increase recoverability: regular documents are easier to
archive, compare, copy, and decode. The Liye cache exists as evidence partly
because an administrative documentary system produced it. On this reading,
standardization is a preservation technology as well as a filter.

The lecture accepts that correction. Its narrower claim is dimensional: the
system can preserve more instances in comparable form while preserving fewer
distinctions among the forms it displaces. Whether total recoverability rises
or falls cannot be calculated from this case.

A second rival reading emphasizes state formation rather than entropy. Terms
such as legibility, bureaucratic reach, and uneven implementation may explain
the evidence without a new concept. Historical entropy earns its place only if
it repeatedly helps readers ask what a successful carrier made irretrievable,
without pretending to measure an aggregate quantity.

A third reading treats some Liye variation as the result of tolerated
flexibility, material constraint, and differing scribal training rather than as
evidence of resistance or loss. Staack explicitly keeps these explanations
open. The lecture therefore uses “adaptation,” not “negotiation,” and makes no
claim about the motives of individual scribes.

## Entropy Assessment

| Dimension | More ordered or recoverable | Less recoverable or more uncertain |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Content | recurring administrative fields and categories | local distinctions screened out by those categories |
| Context | transactions become comparable within the system | reasons for noncompliance and informal practice often disappear |
| Provenance | document routines can expose office, route, and date | retrospective slogans compress how and where standards spread |
| Capacity | central and local offices can coordinate at greater scale | displaced scribal and regional capacities become harder to reconstruct |
| Contestation | excavated variation makes disagreement visible | absence of regional forms cannot prove their immediate disappearance |

This is not a net score. Order and entropy occur in different dimensions and
at different points in the carrier chain.

## Limits

The case is bounded to Qin imperial standardization and early Han adaptation,
with unusual weight on a surviving archive from Qianling. It does not represent
all regions, social strata, languages, or practices. The lecture relies on
English-language summaries and translations alongside cited Chinese text; a
publication review should include a reader competent in early Chinese history
and relevant paleography. It does not adjudicate Qin political legitimacy,
explain dynastic collapse, or rank Qin and Han morally.
