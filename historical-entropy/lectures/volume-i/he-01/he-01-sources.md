## Verified Sources

### Primary and near-primary

- Sima Qian, [“Basic Annals of the First Emperor of Qin,” *Shiji*](https://ctext.org/shiji/qin-shi-huang-ben-ji/ens),
  Chinese Text Project bilingual edition. Claim location: paragraph containing
  `分天下以為三十六郡` and `一法度衡石丈尺。車同軌。書同文字。` The account was
  compiled under the Han and is used as retrospective representation, not an
  eyewitness administrative record. Translator wording is used only in the
  brief footnote formula.
- Liye Qin administrative wooden documents, 222–208 BCE, treated through the
  published material analysis and document study in Staack rather than through
  an unverified translation of individual slips.

### Scholarship and public orientation

- Thies Staack, [“The pragmatics of standardization: document standards and
  their implementation in Qin administration (late third century BCE)”](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0041977X23000228),
  *Bulletin of the School of Oriental and African Studies* 86.1 (2023),
  pp. 147–173. Open-access article; exact support in abstract, introduction,
  and the two case studies.
- Chen Chao-jung, [“New Assessment of the Qin ‘Unification of Script’”](https://www1.ihp.sinica.edu.tw/en/Publications/Bulletin/980/Article/403),
  *Bulletin of the Institute of History and Philology, Academia Sinica* 68.3
  (1997), pp. 589–641. Exact support in the English abstract and article. The
  official page's human-readable record gives `68.3`; its auto-generated
  citation incorrectly renders `68.8`, so this dossier follows the record.
- Yuri Pines, [“Legalism in Chinese Philosophy”](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/chinese-legalism/),
  *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, especially §7 on the Qin–Han legacy.
  Used to bound continuity: later rulers retained some practices while others
  were abandoned or transformed.
- You Yi-Fei, [“Reform in the Institute of the Commandery from the Warring
  States Period to the Early Western Han Dynasty”](https://scholars.lib.ntu.edu.tw/entities/publication/1ecdbebc-0f9c-4514-a2d5-9b85144beea0),
  National Taiwan University doctoral thesis (2014), English abstract. The
  abstract traces the commandery's changing authority and internal structure
  from Qin into early and later Western Han; used to replace a broad continuity
  formula with a specific reuse-and-transformation claim.
- You Yi-Fei, [“Division of Power: An Analysis of the Qin Commandery System
  Based on the Bamboo Slips”](https://www1.ihp.sinica.edu.tw/Publications/Bulletin/1024/Article/1973),
  *Bulletin of the Institute of History and Philology, Academia Sinica* 87.3
  (2016), pp. 461–506. The English abstract distinguishes the Qin commandery's
  divided offices from Western Han arrangements and guards against treating
  similarly named institutions as structurally identical.
- Metropolitan Museum of Art, [“The Qin and Han Dynasties”](https://www.metmuseum.org/exhibitions/listings/2017/age-of-empires/exhibition-galleries),
  *Age of Empires* gallery overview. Used only for public chronological
  orientation and the cautious formula “adopted and adapted,” not as the main
  support for contested claims.
- Tsang Wing Ma, [“Categorizing Laborers: Glimpses of Qin Management of Human
  Resources from an Administrative Document from Liye”](https://doi.org/10.1017/eac.2021.1),
  *Early China* 44 (2021), pp. 351–391. Corroborating scholarly lead on the
  difficulties of implementing administrative control at Qianling; not needed
  to carry a unique manuscript claim.

## Exclusions

- Unlocated candidate quotations—including “Qin fell, but its system endured”—
  are excluded from the manuscript.
- “Book burning and burying scholars” is omitted. Its textual and historiographic
  problems would distract from the packet's bounded standardization mechanism.
- “Legalism” is not used as a total explanation of Qin action; the label's
  retrospective construction requires a separate lecture.
- No causal claim links standardization to the Qin collapse.
- No claim is made that regional speech, practice, or identity vanished because
  official writing was standardized.

## Independence Notes

The *Shiji* and later interpretations of Qin are not independent contemporary
testimony. Liye documents are a materially distinct evidence class and can test
implementation, but their survival is geographically and administratively
selective. Chen and Staack are independent scholarly analyses with different
objects—script forms and document standards—yet both depend on excavated
corpora whose preservation is uneven. The Met overview is not counted as an
independent scholarly lineage. CMC is counted only as the discovery trail.
